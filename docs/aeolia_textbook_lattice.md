## Unit 1.1: The Founding Conditions

The Lattice occupies the dense archipelago cluster of the southern temperate zone, a region of approximately fourteen major islands and several hundred smaller ones arranged around an inland sea whose waters are shallow, sheltered, and subject to tides of exceptional range. The maximum tidal range at the principal harbors exceeds seven meters. This is not a decoration on our civilization. This is the foundation on which the civilization stands.

The ancestors of the Lattice walked into these islands during the last glacial period, when the submarine plateaus were exposed and the crossings were straightforward. When the seas rose, the populations were separated from every other human group on the planet. The records of the founding period are fragmentary. The Ministry's position is that the Lattice has existed as a continuous civilization for approximately fifteen thousand years. This figure is conservative; some scholars have proposed longer continuities, but the documentary evidence becomes unreliable beyond the fifteen-thousand-year mark.

The early Lattice discovered that the tidal flats could be cultivated. **Paddi** — the managed saltwater grain that remains our staple today — grows in shallow terraces flooded on the rising tide and drained on the falling tide, with freshwater rain diluting the salt to tolerable concentrations. The cultivation method requires dikes. The dikes require maintenance. The maintenance requires coordination. The coordination requires a bureaucracy. The bureaucracy requires examinations to ensure competence. The examination system was in place, in recognizable form, by the beginning of the third dynastic cycle.

This is the engineering logic of our civilization. The grain grows in managed water. The water is managed by the state. The state is run by those who pass the examinations. The examinations test whether the candidate can manage water. Everything else — our philosophy, our literature, our law, our art — is downstream of the dikes.

## Unit 1.2: The Dynastic Cycles

The Lattice has undergone seven major dynastic cycles since the founding period. Each cycle follows a predictable pattern. A period of unification and infrastructure expansion is followed by a period of stable governance, then a period of institutional decay, then a period of fragmentation and civil conflict, then a reunification under a new ruling house. The ruling house changes. The system does not.

The cycles are not mysterious. The Ministry's historical model treats dynastic transition as an engineering problem with a known failure mode: when the bureaucracy's attention to the dike system lapses, saltwater intrusion damages the paddi harvest, grain reserves are depleted, the population suffers, and legitimacy passes to whichever faction can demonstrate that it will maintain the dikes. The legitimacy transfer is called the *Turning of the Waters*. It has occurred six times. Each time, the system has survived the transition. The continuity of the Lattice does not depend on any particular ruling house. It depends on the continued function of the hydraulic infrastructure.

The current dynastic cycle began approximately eleven hundred years ago and is considered stable. The dikes are maintained. The examinations are administered on schedule. The grain reserves are adequate. There is no present indication that a Turning of the Waters is imminent.

## Unit 1.3: The Garrison Chain

The outer boundary of the Lattice's directly administered territory is marked by a chain of fortified positions known as the Garrison Chain. The Chain was established during the Third Dynasty to defend against the peripheral maritime raiders whose depredations had become a recurring problem as the core cluster's wealth grew. It has been extended, contracted, and repositioned many times since.

The Chain serves three functions. First, it provides defense against raiders. Second, it marks the limit of administrative authority — inside the Chain, Ministry regulations apply; outside, they do not. Third, it contains the outward projection of Lattice force. The Chain is as much a limit on our own expansion as it is a defense against others. The Ministry's position is that this limitation is a feature, not a defect. The Lattice does not require additional territory. Additional territory would require additional administrative overhead, which would strain the examination system and risk institutional decay.

On seven occasions during our history, reformist factions within the government have argued that the Chain should be breached and the Lattice should project force into the outer waters. Each of these episodes is called a *Pulse Expedition*. In each case, a fleet was outfitted, sailed beyond the Chain, reached distant archipelagos, gathered information about what lay beyond, and was subsequently recalled by the central administration. The recall in every case was justified on fiscal and administrative grounds. The fleet records from these expeditions are preserved in the Archive. The charts they produced are considered accurate.

The most recent Pulse Expedition occurred approximately six hundred years ago. Its charts show an archipelago in the northern temperate zone that the expedition's navigators identified as inhabited by a civilization of significant sophistication. The expedition did not make formal contact. On return, the fleet was decommissioned, the navigators were reassigned to coastal duties, and the charts were filed. The Ministry's position is that the decision to file the charts rather than act on them was correct given the administrative priorities of the period.

## Unit 1.4: The Northern Civilization

Approximately forty years ago, the Ministry's signals intelligence bureau detected sustained radio transmissions originating from the northern archipelago identified in the final Pulse Expedition's charts. The transmissions were in a language the Lattice's linguists had not previously documented. The content indicated a civilization of comparable technological development to our own, organized along principles that differ substantially from ours.

The Ministry's position is that the existence of this civilization — which calls itself the Reach — does not alter the engineering fundamentals of the Lattice. The dikes continue to require maintenance. The examinations continue to be administered. The harvests continue to be adequate. The discovery changes nothing about our daily obligations.

The Ministry's position is also that the existence of the Reach creates a new category of administrative consideration which has not yet been fully incorporated into the standard model. The Reach is not a peripheral raider; it is a peer civilization with its own hydraulic system (though theirs is based on rain-fed hillside agriculture rather than tidal-flat management), its own examination system (though theirs selects for commercial rather than administrative competence), and its own continuity of institutional memory. The Reach has been observing the Lattice for approximately seventeen years, according to analysis of their signal archives. We have been observing them for approximately forty years. Neither civilization has initiated formal contact. The present state is one of *mutual awareness without formal protocol*.

Students should understand that the Lattice's response to the Reach discovery is not a matter for public debate. The Committee on External Affairs has established a working protocol, which is classified at the level of Senior Administrator, and which will be implemented when the conditions specified in the protocol obtain. Students who encounter speculation in unofficial publications regarding Lattice-Reach relations should understand that such speculation is not authorized and does not represent the Ministry's position.

## Unit 1.5: The Current Situation (Summary)

The Lattice is the oldest continuous civilization on the planet. Its population exceeds that of any other known polity by a factor of at least two. Its agricultural system feeds its population reliably. Its examination system selects competent administrators. Its infrastructure is maintained. Its borders are defended by the Garrison Chain. Its recent awareness of the Reach has not altered any of these fundamentals. The engineering logic of the Lattice remains sound.

Students will be tested on:
1. The name and approximate duration of each dynastic cycle
2. The three functions of the Garrison Chain
3. The number and outcomes of the Pulse Expeditions
4. The approximate date of Reach signal detection and the Ministry's current position on Reach relations
5. The basic principles of tidal-flat cultivation and the role of the examination system in maintaining the dikes

The examination will not ask your opinion on any of these matters. The Ministry's position is the correct answer in all cases.
