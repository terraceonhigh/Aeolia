# 10 — VISUAL & TONE GUIDE

## The Look of Aeolia

Aeolia's visual identity emerges from three constraints: **water everywhere**, **volcanic terrain**, and **the set-dressing swap**. Every frame should communicate at least one of these.

---

## I. THE OCEAN

The ocean is the **first character in every exterior shot**. It is always present — visible from every point on every island, audible from every building, reflected in every window. Even interior scenes should carry traces of the ocean: the quality of light (maritime, diffuse, reflected off water), the sound (ferry horns, harbor machinery, wave action), the material culture (salt-resistant finishes, maritime hardware, nautical objects).

### Water Palette
The ocean on Aeolia is not one color. It's a **spectrum** determined by depth, time of day, weather, and proximity to volcanic mineral sources:
- **Deep open ocean:** Near-black indigo. Aeolia's ocean is *deep* — kilometers deep in most places. The water absorbs light aggressively. From altitude, the open ocean looks like ink.
- **Over submarine plateaus:** Lighter blue-green. The shallows are visible as color changes from altitude — navigational features that experienced sailors read.
- **Harbor water:** Dark grey-green. Turbid from harbor activity, reflecting city lights at night.
- **Tropical shallows:** Turquoise over coral and sand.
- **Storm water:** Steel grey, white-capped, violent.

### The Horizon
The horizon is always water. There are no continental horizons on Aeolia — no plains, no mountain ranges in the distance, no visual termination that isn't ocean. From any point on any island, looking outward, the last thing you see before the sky is *water*. This should be a persistent visual presence. Characters looking outward are looking at the sea. Always.

**Exception:** Looking *inward* (upslope), characters see the volcanic peak, the city climbing the slope, the built environment. The visual axis of every Aeolian city is **vertical**: ocean at the bottom, city in the middle, volcano at the top, sky above.

---

## II. THE VOLCANIC TERRAIN

### Rock
Aeolian islands are *volcanic*. The dominant rock is basalt — dark grey to black, vesicular (tiny gas-bubble voids visible in the surface), rough-textured. Older, weathered basalt may be reddish-brown. Buildings are constructed from or on volcanic rock. The rock is visible everywhere — in the walls, in the steps, in the foundations. The *warmth* of the rock (sun-heated basalt retains heat into the evening) is a sensory detail that characters interact with regularly.

### Slopes
Every settlement is on a slope. Flat land is vanishingly rare and enormously valuable (harbors, agricultural terraces). The visual language of every city is **terraced** — buildings stepping up the slope, streets climbing at steep grades, staircases cut into rock connecting levels. The visual effect is of a civilization *pressed against a wall* — the wall being the volcanic slope — building upward because there's nowhere else to go.

### Vegetation
Tropical to temperate, depending on latitude. Lush on the windward slopes (heavy rainfall), scrubby on the leeward. The green is *intense* — volcanic soil is rich, rainfall is heavy (thick atmosphere holds more moisture), and the vegetation has had hundreds of millions of years to adapt. The contrast between dark volcanic rock and vivid green vegetation is a signature visual element.

---

## III. THE CITY

### The Hong Kong Template
Every major city is a **vertical harbor city**. The template:
- **Waterfront:** Dense, commercial, loud. Markets, docks, warehouses, naval facilities. The lowest elevation. The poorest neighborhoods. The most diverse (immigrant communities, transient populations, naval personnel on shore leave).
- **Mid-levels:** Residential and commercial. The aspiring classes. Progressively quieter and wealthier as you climb. Stepped lanes, inclined railways, elevators as public transit.
- **Upper slopes:** Wealthy residential. Colonial-era mansions (in the Reach's capital). Government buildings. The observatory at the summit. Clean air, silence, views.

### Density
Aeolian cities are *dense*. Tokyo-dense. Hong Kong-dense. Buildings pressed together, streets narrow enough to touch both walls, laundry on lines crossing between buildings, light entering apartments in narrow shafts. There is **no sprawl**. The city ends where the harbor begins on one side and where the slope becomes too steep to build on the other. Between these boundaries, everything is built on everything else.

### Vertical Transportation
The visual signature of Aeolian urbanism. Inclined railways, outdoor escalators, steep stepped lanes, elevators in cliff faces. Characters navigate their cities **vertically** as much as horizontally. A commute involves a ferry (horizontal) and an incline (vertical). The altitude of your daily journey encodes your class position.

### Signage and Commercial Culture
**The Reach (Chinese-coded):** Signage uses the Reach's logographic script — visually evocative of Chinese characters but with a different underlying structure (derived from maritime pictograms). Vertical banners, brush-calligraphy shop signs, red-and-gold color accents on dark wood or stone facades. The visual density of signage is high in the waterfront commercial districts and decreases with altitude.

**The Lattice (American-coded):** Neon, steel, glass. Horizontal signage. The Lattice's alphabetic script in bold sans-serif typography. The commercial aesthetic is American mid-century — bright, graphic, functional. Strip-mall-adjacent retail in a vertical urban context produces a specific visual dissonance: the *signage* says Houston but the *city plan* says Hong Kong.

## IV. THE SET-DRESSING SWAP (Detailed Rules)

### The Reach

**Architecture:** Curved rooflines, extended eaves, red columns, courtyard-organized buildings. Materials: dark timber, stone, tile. The aesthetic is refined, restrained — Song Dynasty crossed with the specific needs of maritime-volcanic construction (storm-resistant, earthquake-tolerant, salt-air durable).

**Clothing:** Robes, layered garments, silk where affordable, cotton and linen where not. Color palette: muted — indigo, grey, cream, with red and gold reserved for ceremonial and commercial contexts. The visual effect should be *elegant* while the *behavior* (argument, commerce, waterfront roughness) contradicts the elegance.

**Interior design:** Clean lines, natural materials, attention to proportion. Furniture is low. Rooms are organized around a central space. The aesthetic says "contemplative" while the conversation happening in the room says "aggressive commercial negotiation."

**Food presentation:** Ceramic ware, small dishes, precise arrangement. The visual language of kaiseki or formal Chinese table setting. The *food itself* is maritime — raw fish, seasoned grain, fermented vegetables, tea.

### The Lattice

**Architecture:** Steel frame, glass curtain wall, concrete, corrugated metal. The visual vocabulary of American industrial and commercial construction — but in a vertical, dense, harbor-oriented city plan that American architecture never produced. Buildings look like they could be in Cleveland. The city they're in looks like Hong Kong.

**Clothing:** Denim, cotton, leather, work boots. Functional, rugged, unadorned. The visual reads as "American working class" while the social content (collective identity, institutional deference, display-avoidance) is un-American.

**Interior design:** Fluorescent lighting, plastic furniture, steel surfaces. The mess hall looks like a diner. The examination hall looks like a DMV. The bureaucratic office looks like a community college administrative suite.

**Food presentation:** Steel trays, communal serving, functional dishware. The same food (tidal grain, fermented fish, grilled accompaniments) presented with American-cafeteria directness.

## V. LIGHT

### The Maritime Light
Aeolia's light is **water-reflected**. Even in interior scenes, the quality of light should carry the signature of proximity to a harbor — a cool, diffuse, slightly blue quality that comes from sunlight bouncing off water before entering windows.

### The Thicker Atmosphere
Aeolia's 1.5–2 atm atmosphere produces **more atmospheric scattering** than Earth's. Sunsets are more vivid — deeper oranges, wider color bands, longer duration. The golden hour is longer. Midday light is slightly hazier at sea level. Colors are slightly muted at distance (more atmospheric haze).

### The Stars
At night, from altitude, the stars are *extraordinary*. Aeolia's limited light pollution (cities are small, concentrated, harbor-oriented) means the night sky from most locations is densely populated with stars. The Milky Way equivalent is a prominent band. The stars have *color* — blue-white, yellow, orange — visible to the naked eye. The sky from the observation deck of a nuclear seaplane at 3 AM over dark ocean is one of the most visually spectacular environments in the setting.

---

## VI. SOUND

### The Harbor Soundscape
The default audio environment: ferry horns (every 15 minutes), engine rumble, stevedore shouts, crane mechanisms, wave action against stone, the constant background of a working harbor. The sound diminishes with altitude — the waterfront is loud, the mid-levels are moderate, the peaks are quiet.

### The Ferry Horn
The city's heartbeat. Short, low, resonant. Sounds every 15 minutes, all night, every night. Characters register it unconsciously — its absence would be more noticeable than its presence.

### Volcanic Ambience
The low, background presence of a geologically active environment: occasional tremors (so routine nobody mentions them), the faint sulfur smell near volcanic vents, the warmth of the ground in certain locations.

### Nuclear Seaplane Operations
Takeoff: a roaring run across the harbor basin, spray sheeting from the hull, the sound building from rumble to scream as the engines reach takeoff power, then the sudden *quiet* as the aircraft lifts free of the water and the spray-slap ceases and the only sound is the engines climbing. Landing: the reverse — the approach, the touchdown spray, the deceleration, the engine note dropping, the gentle rocking as the hull settles into the water and the aircraft becomes a boat again.

---

## VII. SCALE RULES

### The World is Big
Always communicate the scale. The ocean extends to every horizon. Islands are small against the water. Cities are small against the islands. Characters are small against the cities. Every wide shot should make the human presence feel *fragile* — a thin film of civilization on a vast ocean.

### The City is Dense
Tight framing in urban scenes. Narrow streets, close walls, faces in windows. The visual language of compression. The city is the opposite of the ocean — everything is close, everything is stacked, there is no empty space.

### The Aircraft are Huge
Nuclear seaplanes are *big* — larger than any conventional aircraft, with flying-boat hulls and high-mounted engines. They should be visually imposing in harbor (dwarfing the boats around them) and visually *small* from altitude (dwarfed by the ocean beneath them). The scale contrast — dominant in the harbor, tiny against the ocean — communicates the setting's thesis about human technology and natural indifference.

## VIII. EMOTIONAL KEYNOTES

| Scene Type | Visual Keynote |
|---|---|
| Daily life | The ferry, the harbor, the routine beauty that the characters no longer see |
| The overview | The ocean from altitude — vast, featureless, humbling |
| The artifacts | Isolated objects in quiet rooms — the painting on the wall, the manuscript on the desk, the bag on the doormat |
| The military | Dark water, instrument lighting, the confined spaces of the aircraft interior |
| The contact | Two vessels — ships, aircraft, fishing boats — facing each other across open water |
| The substrate discovery | The night sky — the stars that were placed, the beauty that was designed, the enormity of the question |
