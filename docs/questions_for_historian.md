# Questions for 20th Century History Professor

Context: I'm building a procedural history simulator (Aeolia) that models 20,000 years of geopolitical history on a fictional archipelago world. The late-game mechanics — resource scrambles, deterrence, colonial extraction — are where I need the sharpest feedback. These questions target the 20th century dynamics the simulator tries to model.

---

## On the naphtha scramble (parallels oil politics, ~1900–1973)

In the simulation, a fossil fuel resource becomes commercially viable at a certain tech level, and polities scramble to secure supply chains on archipelagos they don't control. I'm trying to model the transition from "we buy this on the market" to "this is a state-level strategic imperative." How fast did that shift actually happen with petroleum? Was there a specific moment where oil went from commodity to casus belli, or was it gradual?

## On the pyra scramble (parallels uranium/plutonium, ~1940–1960s)

A fissile material becomes strategically critical overnight — not gradually like oil, but in a single discovery event. The islands that happen to have it go from irrelevant to existential targets. I'm modeling this as a faster, more militarized scramble than the fuel one. Does the Manhattan Project to early Cold War timeline support that compression? How quickly did the US go from "we need uranium" to "we will restructure entire foreign relationships around uranium access"?

## On the Dark Forest threshold (parallels Cold War nuclear detection)

Two mature polities that have traded indirectly for millennia suddenly detect each other directly — like the U-2 overflights revealing the other side's capabilities. In the sim, this triggers a deterrence standoff. My question is about the psychology of that moment: when the US and USSR first understood each other's actual nuclear capability, was the dominant response "we must negotiate" or "we must build more"? And did the existence of prior indirect contact (trade, diplomacy through intermediaries) make it better or worse?

## On energy transition economics

An archipelago whose wealth depends on a biological oil export faces displacement by a fossil fuel. I'm trying to model whether this creates diversification or desperation. Did any real 20th century resource economies successfully transition, or do they all follow the resource curse pattern?

## On the colonial-to-postcolonial transition

A high-tech polity establishes monoculture extraction on a lower-tech polity's territory. How did the transition from colonial extraction to post-colonial resource nationalism actually play out? When did producing countries start saying "we're not selling you raw materials anymore, we're processing them ourselves"? OPEC, nationalization of mines, ISI policies — what's the actual sequence?

## On quinine and habitability unlocks

The simulation models a commodity that enables tropical occupation — without it, colonizers can't hold territory in disease environments. Quinine is the obvious analog, but what's the 20th century equivalent? DDT for mosquito eradication? Air conditioning for Gulf state development? Is there a general pattern where a specific technology unlocks habitability of a previously hostile environment, and does that always trigger a resource scramble?

## The big question

The simulation models a world where the same polity that trades peacefully for centuries can pivot to military extraction when a new strategic resource is discovered. The 20th century did this repeatedly — oil, uranium, rare earths, cobalt. Is there a general pattern, or is each resource scramble sui generis? What would you tell me not to over-simplify?
