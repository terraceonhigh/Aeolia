## Preface to the Eighth Edition

Every generation of students asks the same question when they first encounter our history: *were we right to sail?* It is a question the textbook cannot answer, and your teacher should refuse to. But it is the correct question, and you should hold onto it through every chapter that follows. The ships that carried our grandparents' grandparents across the gaps carried a gift, and they carried a curse. A proper education does not ask you to forget either one.

This edition incorporates the new findings from the Cartographic Office concerning the pre-contact population estimates for the Windward archipelagos, which are substantially higher than the figures in earlier editions. The revision is uncomfortable. It should be.

— J.H., *fourteenth month of the current year*

## Chapter One: The Homeland

The Reach began as a scattering of islands in the temperate westerlies — the wide band of prevailing winds that runs eastward around the middle latitudes of our hemisphere. Our distant ancestors walked into these islands during the last great ice age, when the submarine plateaus stood above the sea and the crossings were dry. When the waters rose, the roads drowned, and our ancestors were cut off from every other human population on the planet. They did not know this. Their flood stories remembered it, but their maps did not.

For perhaps eighty thousand years, the people of the homeland islands lived without outside contact. They farmed **emmer** on the volcanic hillsides, terraced the slopes against the rain, raised **kri** for milk and leather, and sailed small boats between the islands for trade and marriage. They developed writing, metallurgy, and the first **navigators' guilds**. They learned to read the stars — our pole star is dim but steady, and our ancestors built correction tables that are still used today.

The homeland geography shaped everything that followed. The islands are spaced widely — a day's sail between the nearest pair, a week between the most distant. This meant no single ruler could hold them all. Island-states competed. Merchants grew powerful because they could move between rival states. The idea that *the people might constrain the prince* was old already when it was first written down. It was not an idea borrowed from anyone. It came from the geography.

## Chapter Two: The Gap Crossings

Five thousand years ago, a captain whose name we still remember (though scholars disagree about which historical figure she actually was) took a heavy-keeled ship west across an open stretch of ocean that every previous navigator had turned back from. She was gone for eleven months. When she returned, she brought back four passengers from an archipelago that had been isolated for ninety thousand years, the first new human face any Reach sailor had ever seen.

The contact was a catastrophe. Within two generations, the population of that archipelago had fallen by perhaps six parts in ten. Diseases our ancestors carried without symptoms killed the people who had no defense against them. Our textbooks call this *the First Grief*. We do not know exactly what was lost, because no record survives from the side that lost it.

This pattern repeated. Approximately once every century for a thousand years, a Reach expedition bridged a gap, and a civilization that had been isolated for tens of thousands of years discovered that it was not alone. The expeditions brought back **char**, **awa**, **pinang**, **qivu**, strange fruits, strange philosophies, strange gods. They took back epidemics. By the sixth crossing, the pattern was well enough understood that a debate had begun in the free press of the Capital: *should we keep sailing?* The debate was not won by either side. It is not won today.

The later expeditions were different in character. They knew what was coming. Some captains carried physicians and quarantine protocols. Some negotiated first contact through elaborate distance rituals. Some refused to disembark. The moral weight accumulated: by the tenth crossing, the Reach intellectual class contained as many voices against further contact as for it. But the ships kept sailing. Merchants wanted new markets. The commercial houses wanted **seric**, which only the paddi zone civilizations produced, and which commanded prices that made the crossings profitable even when the moral case against them was strongest.

## Chapter Three: The Settler Archipelago

The third major contact, approximately four thousand years ago, revealed an archipelago of unusual promise: good harbors, tall volcanic peaks, mineral deposits including copper and the rarer metals that would much later become critical, and — most importantly — a position along the main westerly corridor that made it an ideal base for further expansion. The indigenous population was small and devastated by contact disease. Within two centuries, settlers from the homeland outnumbered them. Within four centuries, the settler archipelago had declared independence from the Capital — our first civil war, which our ancestors lost. Within eight centuries, the settler archipelago was the dominant power of the Reach.

You are reading this book in the Capital, which is on the homeland. The government that publishes your textbook is on the settler archipelago. These are not the same place. Our grandparents' grandparents moved the capital of everything except the *name* to the new islands, because the new islands had the harbors and the metals and the strategic position. The homeland kept the culture, the literature, the navigators' college, and the tea. The settler archipelago took everything else.

The indigenous people of the settler archipelago still exist. They live on the outer islands and in specific legally protected communities. They have their own flood stories, which match ours. They have their own examination system, which they developed independently before contact, and which they refuse to integrate with ours despite centuries of pressure. The question of their legal status is the unresolved question of our political life. You will argue about it in your lifetime. There is no agreed answer.

## Chapter Four: The Pearl-String

For two thousand years, the Reach expanded westward along the wind corridor. Each new contact became a garrison, then a trading post, then a colony. The chain of garrisons — we call it the pearl-string — allowed ships to travel enormous distances while resupplying at friendly ports every few hundred kilometers. At its greatest extent, the pearl-string ran more than forty thousand kilometers along the westerlies, linking fourteen major archipelagos and perhaps three hundred smaller ones.

The pearl-string was fragile. One lost garrison severed the chain. The Reach's naval doctrine was entirely oriented around protecting the links. This made us excellent at sea warfare and poor at land warfare — a limitation that only mattered later, when we encountered civilizations that had built up land armies without needing to protect sea routes.

The pearl-string is now in decline. The nuclear seaplane makes the chain of garrisons unnecessary — a fleet can project anywhere without resupply stops. Many former garrisons are now independent states, or in the process of becoming so. The Reach accepts this with varying degrees of grace. Some of our politicians argue we should accelerate decolonization. Others argue we should slow it. Your grandparents voted on this. Your parents voted on this. You will vote on this.

## Chapter Five: The Lattice

Two centuries ago, a Reach surveillance aircraft on a routine deep-ocean mapping run detected something that should not have existed: dense structured radio traffic, originating from an archipelago roughly fifty-five thousand kilometers from the Capital, in the opposite hemisphere, along a dense island cluster we had not previously identified as inhabited. The aircraft's navigator — a woman whose name you will learn when you study the military history course — photographed the city lights from altitude and flew home without transmitting, on instinct, knowing that whatever she had seen was too important to broadcast.

The Lattice had been watching us for longer than we had been watching them. When the analysts at the Capital finally compiled the pattern, it turned out that their radio traffic contained references to Reach vessels going back four decades. They had discovered us first. They had simply not told us. Neither, it turned out, had we told them — our own admiralty had been tracking anomalous signals from the south for seventeen years without connecting them to a civilization.

The Lattice is a peer. This is the sentence your teacher will repeat until you can feel its weight. For a thousand years, the Reach's intellectual tradition has assumed we were the dominant civilization of the planet — not arrogant about it, but certain. The Lattice is older than we are by approximately fifteen thousand years. Their population is roughly two and a half times ours. Their agricultural system supports cities of a scale we do not have. Their philosophical tradition is as rich as ours, developed from entirely different root metaphors. Their political system bears no resemblance to ours and works better than you want to believe.

Formal contact has not yet occurred. When you graduate, you may be the generation that makes it.
