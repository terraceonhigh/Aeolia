# 01 — PLANET & PHYSICS

## Governing Constraint

A single transonic aircraft (~Mach 0.9, ~1,100 km/h) requires **one week** of continuous flight to circumnavigate the planet at the equator. All other physical properties are derived from or constrained by this requirement.

## Internal Structure

A mean density of 1,200 kg/m³ rules out a conventional iron-silicate body. Canonical structure:

**Core:** Small rocky/metallic core, perhaps 20–30% of the planet's radius. Provides the magnetic field (essential for atmospheric retention and radiation protection). Dense enough to anchor the interior but small relative to the total volume.

**Mantle:** Deep layer of high-pressure ice (Ice VII and above) overlaid by liquid water. The ice mantle is not cold — it exists at high pressure, not low temperature. The boundary between high-pressure ice and liquid water is deep, perhaps 500–1,000 km below the surface.

**Ocean:** A global ocean tens to hundreds of kilometers deep. The surface ocean is where all life and civilization exist. The depth is effectively infinite from a human perspective — no technology on Aeolia can reach the bottom.

**Crust:** No continental crust in the terrestrial sense. Exposed land consists of volcanic features — mid-ocean ridges, hotspot island chains, volcanic arcs — that breach the surface. The crust is thin, young, and volcanically active. Earthquakes and eruptions are common. Islands are geologically transient on long timescales — they erode, subside, and are replaced by new volcanic activity.

The 24-hour day is a narrative convenience, not a physical necessity. Slower rotation (30–48h) is plausible and would weaken Coriolis effects, producing broader, more sluggish weather systems.

| Property | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Composition | N₂/O₂ (~78/21%) | Breathable |
| Sea-level pressure | ~1.5–2 atm | Higher than Earth; planet retains volatiles easily |

| Scale height | ~8.5 km | May be larger given surface pressure |

**Narrative consequences of thicker atmosphere:** More lift per unit wing area (favors large, heavy aircraft). Slightly higher speed of sound (marginal). Better sound propagation (the harbor is louder). More vivid sunsets (atmospheric scattering increases with density). Slightly higher drag at transonic speeds (marginal for nuclear aircraft with unlimited fuel). Weather systems carry more moisture and produce more intense precipitation.

## Weather & Climate

Aeolia's weather is *recognizably Earth-like* in character but *vastly larger* in scale.

**Trade winds and westerlies** exist in the same latitudinal bands as Earth's, driven by the same Hadley/Ferrel/Polar cell circulation. Each wind belt is thousands of kilometers wide. Within the belts, prevailing winds are the strategic highways — sailing with the belt is cheap, sailing across it is expensive.

**Cyclones** form in the tropics and mid-latitudes. They are similar in absolute size to Earth's (a few hundred km diameter) but tiny relative to the planet's surface. Hundreds of independent cyclone systems can coexist in each hemisphere at any given time. The mid-latitude storm track is not a single band but a *field* of independent systems. Weather forecasting is enormously important and enormously difficult because the forecast domain is so vast.

**Ocean currents** are driven by wind and Coriolis force. The major gyres are larger than Earth's but follow the same physics. Currents are the invisible highways of the sailing age — knowing the current systems is the foundation of long-range navigation.

## Bathymetry & the Drowned Roads

The ocean floor is Aeolia's most strategically important geological feature.

### The Submarine Plateau System

**What they are:** Vast, shallow platforms on the ocean floor — remnants of ancient volcanic flood basalts and accumulated biogenic sediment on the flanks of mid-ocean ridges. They form a discontinuous but traceable web connecting most of Aeolia's major archipelagos.

**Depth:** 100–300 m below current sea level on the shallowest sections. Deeper troughs (500–2,000 m) separate plateau segments.

**Extent:** Individual plateaus range from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of km². Collectively ~5–8% of the ocean floor.

**Current-era significance:** Rich fisheries (shallow water supports productive ecosystems). Navigational hazards for deep-draft vessels. Anomalously low wave heights. Sacred/cultural significance for coastal civilizations that know the shallows. The submarine cable network preferentially routes across plateaus (shorter cable runs, shallower water, easier maintenance).

### Glacial Emergence

During glacial maxima, polar ice sheets lock up enough water to drop sea levels by **150–250 m**. When the water drops:

- Shallowest plateaus emerge as vast, flat, marshy lowlands — Dogger Banks the size of subcontinents.
- Deeper troughs between plateaus become narrow straits — crossable by crude watercraft, sometimes visible shore to shore.
- Deepest gaps remain submerged but narrow significantly.

**Result:** A braided land-bridge network that connects archipelago to archipelago — the infrastructure of hominin dispersal. The bridges are impermanent: they emerge during glacials and drown during interglacials. The current interglacial has drowned all bridges. The populations dispersed across them are now isolated.

**Detection:** In the narrative present, the plateaus are mapped by depth sounding, sonar, and limited satellite imagery. The braided-network topology is one of the "seams" that reveals the world's artificial substrate — the connectivity is statistically implausible for natural geology.

## Celestial Engineering

The sky is the most constrained and most carefully designed element of the physical environment. It is *infrastructure*, not decoration.

### Navigation Stars

**Count:** ~6,000 naked-eye-visible stars. ~57 designated as primary navigation stars.

**Distribution:** Near-uniform across the celestial sphere, using a modified Fibonacci spiral with stochastic perturbation. The distribution ensures navigational coverage (8–12 usable stars above the horizon at any point, any time, any season) while passing statistical tests for natural stellar population distribution. The uniformity is *detectable* with sufficiently advanced statistical methods — this is the first "seam" the astronomer discovers.

**Color diversity:** Navigation stars span the full spectral range (blue-white to orange), with color-balanced distribution in each sky region to permit identification by color as well as position.

**Proper motion:** All stars have assigned proper motions consistent with a realistic galactic population. Key navigation stars have hand-tuned proper motions that are slightly slower than natural — stabilized to keep them in their constellation figures over the 100,000-year civilizational timescale. Eleven stars have anomalous proper-motion values that constitute a second detectable "seam."

### Pole Stars

**Northern:** A dim star (magnitude ~3.8) approximately 2.3° from the north celestial pole. Visible on clear nights, invisible in haze. Useful as a rough reference; requires correction tables for precision work. The correction gap between approximate and precise is where the navigator's skill lives — justifies the navigator guild's institutional prestige.

**Southern:** No pole star. The south celestial pole is empty. The Lattice's navigation system (southern hemisphere) is built on circumpolar star groups — calculating the center of rotation from the group's current orientation. This is harder than using a pole star, requires more training, and produces a navigator corps with higher institutional prestige. The empty pole becomes a philosophical metaphor in the Lattice's tradition: truth as something calculated from movement, not observed as a fixed point.

### Constellation Systems

The star field supports at least **14 independent constellation systems** — different cultural parsings of the same stars by different macro-civilizations. Each system is internally coherent, supports practical navigation, and uses culture-specific pattern language. The same star may be "the eye of the great fish" in one system and "the fourth tide-marker" in another.

### The Star as Clock

Proper motion creates a datable record: ancient star charts from different civilizations, compared to the current sky, yield consistent dates for the shared pre-Drowning origin period. The stars are both navigation infrastructure and archaeological evidence.
