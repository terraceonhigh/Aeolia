# 02 — HISTORICAL TIMELINE

All dates approximate. "BP" = before narrative present. The timeline is divided into geological, pre-contact, serial-contact, and modern eras.

---

## I. GEOLOGICAL & EVOLUTIONARY DEEP TIME

### ~4 billion BP — Biosphere Seeding
Aeolia's ocean develops a marine ecosystem from founding organisms. Evolutionary diversification proceeds over hundreds of millions of years, producing a rich and complex marine biosphere. Volcanic archipelagos develop terrestrial ecosystems — scrub forests, grasslands, insect and bird analogues. Megafauna evolve on the larger islands.

### ~500,000–300,000 BP — Hominin Origins
Hominins evolve in one of Aeolia's larger tropical archipelagos — the **origin archipelago**, a cluster of volcanic islands with sufficient landmass and ecological diversity for the full suite of evolutionary pressures. Megafauna competitors are in gradual decline (a combination of climate-driven habitat change and selective pressures). The ecological niche for a tool-using omnivore opens.

Key condition: Aeolian hominins are **maritime from the earliest period**. The origin archipelago is a cluster of islands, not a single landmass. Inter-island crossings of 50–200 km in outrigger canoes are routine before anatomically modern humans appear. The boat is not an invention of civilization — it's a precondition for it.

Cognitive note: Aeolian humans possess an innate **navigational cognitive module** — a neurological predisposition toward three-category spatial epistemology (landmark-based, celestial, and calculated position knowledge). This module is detectable in the universal three-way navigational evidential present in all Aeolian languages.

### ~300,000–200,000 BP — Anatomically Modern Humans
Modern humans appear in the origin archipelago. Population concentrated on a few large islands with arable coastal plains. Maritime capability already mature — outrigger canoes, inter-island trade, coastal foraging.

---

## II. THE DISPERSAL & THE DROWNING

### ~200,000–100,000 BP — The First Dispersal (Glacial Pump)
A major glacial period drops sea levels by ~200 m. Submarine plateaus emerge as land bridges — vast, flat, marshy lowlands connecting the origin archipelago to neighboring island groups previously separated by 2,000–5,000 km of open water.

Humans walk. They follow coastlines, cross exposed shelves, ford narrow straits in reed boats. Migration is slow — a few kilometers per generation. Over 50,000–80,000 years, humans spread across 3–5 major archipelago groups connected by the shelf network.

Sub-populations settle on islands that will become isolated when the water rises. They don't know this.

### ~100,000 BP — The Drowning
The glacial maximum ends. Sea levels rise 200+ m over several thousand years. The land bridges submerge.

**The world breaks apart.**

Each isolated group carries:
- A shared ancestral language, already diverging
- A Neolithic toolkit: stone tools, fire, basic horticulture, outrigger watercraft
- Oral traditions that will become flood myths — every one of them true

The genetic and cultural clock starts. Pathogen pools diverge. Languages become mutually unintelligible within a few thousand years. Within ten thousand years, the separated populations are effectively different civilizations with a common origin that only mythology remembers.

---

## III. INDEPENDENT DEVELOPMENT (~100,000–20,000 BP)

Each isolated archipelago group develops independently. The resource lottery determines trajectory:

**Domesticable species:** Which archipelago has a cereal grain? A domesticable animal? Useful timber? The winners industrialize first.

**Mineral deposits:** Volcanic archipelagos may have copper, tin, iron. Coral atolls probably don't.

**Archipelago geometry:** Tight clusters with short crossings → complex maritime trade early. Scattered atolls with long gaps → fragmentation.

By ~20,000 BP, the most favorably situated groups have independently developed agriculture, metallurgy, writing, and complex states. Others remain Neolithic. The gap is comparable to Eurasia vs. pre-contact Australia — enormous, but a product of circumstance, not capacity.

### The Lattice's Formation (~60,000–20,000 BP)
The dense core-cluster archipelago in the southern hemisphere develops tidal-flat agriculture using a starchy grain requiring collective hydraulic management. Competitive island-states fight and consolidate cyclically. The examination system emerges. The philosophical tradition crystallizes around tidal metaphors. The garrison chain is established against peripheral maritime raiders.

### The Reach's Formation (~40,000–20,000 BP)
The temperate mid-latitude archipelago develops a competitive maritime culture. Island city-states rival each other, driving innovation. Deep-keeled monohull sailing ships evolve for the open-ocean conditions. A goat-equivalent provides supplementary protein and leather. Mineral deposits support metallurgy.

---

## IV. INTRA-ARCHIPELAGO ANTIQUITY (~20,000–5,000 BP)

The most advanced regions enter classical periods. City-states form leagues. Philosophy and law are codified. Maritime trade within each archipelago group matures into sophisticated relay networks — the **Aeolian Silk Roads**: goods and ideas pass through dozens of intermediary ports across routes of 10,000–30,000 km. Routes dead-end at the shelf edges where shallows give way to abyssal ocean.

Navigators push outward. Depth soundings discover the submarine plateaus — the water gets shallow, then deep again, with no land in sight. Traditions accumulate about "the road beneath the sea."

### The Lattice's Pulse Explorations
The Lattice cyclically pushes outward — reformist emperors or ambitious naval factions send expeditions beyond the garrison chain. Each pulse reaches farther than the last. Each is recalled by the bureaucratic establishment (resources needed for the dikes, admirals getting too powerful, peripheral raiders probing while the fleet is away). Abandoned outposts and dusty charts accumulate in the archives.

---

## V. THE SERIAL CONTACT ERA (~5,000–500 BP)

### ~5,000–4,500 BP — The First Gap Crossing
In the most technologically advanced regions, shipbuilding, celestial navigation, and provisioning converge to make trans-oceanic voyaging conceivable. The first crossings are one-way gambles. Most expeditions vanish. A few succeed. The successful captain who returns is a near-mythological figure.

### The Pattern Repeats (~4,500–500 BP)
**One New World every century for a millennium.** Each successful crossing triggers:
- **Epidemiological shock.** Diseases devastate populations isolated for 30,000–80,000+ years.
- **Economic upheaval.** New commodities, technologies, crops flood in.
- **Theological crisis.** The discovery of people with their own gods and flood myths.

The crossings are not simultaneous or coordinated. Different civilizations bridge different gaps in different eras. There is no single Age of Exploration — there are **several**, staggered across time and space. Each one is a regional apocalypse and renaissance.

**Moral accumulation:** By the fifth or sixth crossing, the discovering civilizations have living memory of what happened to the first four. An anti-colonial intellectual tradition develops *concurrent* with ongoing expansion. This produces richer moral debates than Earth's — "should we cross?" is argued while the ships are being outfitted, with the evidence of previous contact disasters as ammunition.

**Late contacts:** By the seventh or eighth century, some contacted civilizations have heard rumors — garbled reports of strange ships, plagues, alien trade goods appearing at the end of exchange chains. Some prepare. Some quarantine. Some send their own expeditions.

### ~2,000–500 BP — Colonial Maritime Empires
Civilizations that mastered blue-water sailing first build colonial networks: **pearl-strings** — chains of island garrisons along wind-belt corridors. The empires are structurally linear, fragile (one lost garrison severs the chain), and existentially dependent on naval supremacy. Navigation is the decisive strategic technology. Charts are state secrets.

The Reach emerges as the dominant colonial power, inheriting and expanding the longest pearl-string network. The Reach contacts more civilizations than any other power.

---

## VI. THE INDUSTRIAL AND NUCLEAR TRANSITIONS (~500 BP – 50 BP)

### ~500–200 BP — Industrial Transition

**Energy sequence:** Wood/charcoal → wind/tidal → expensive coal → oil → nuclear. Aeolia's energy sequence differs from Earth's because fossil fuels were never cheap (coal deposits are submarine, on drowned plateaus; oil is offshore). Wind and tidal energy fill the gap between pre-industrial and fossil-fuel eras, producing a "tidal-industrial" phase with no Earth parallel.

**Key developments:**
- **Steam propulsion.** Frees ships from wind dependency but doesn't bridge the widest gaps (still needs coaling stations).
- **Telegraphy.** Submarine cables connect islands within archipelagos. Information travels faster than ships for the first time.
- **Chemical aviation.** Powered flight emerges. Useful for intra-archipelago transport (range ~5,000 km), strategically irrelevant for trans-oceanic operations.
- **Cartographic reckoning.** Industrial-era science maps the submarine plateaus. Geologists, biologists, and linguists converge on the realization that all populations share a common origin separated by rising seas. Aeolia's Darwin + Wegener + Out-of-Africa moment.

### ~200–50 BP — The Nuclear Threshold

Atomic physics matures. The implications are recognized instantly: unlimited range. The gaps cease to exist for whoever has the technology.

**Consequences:**
- A new arms race. Nuclear-capable nations become global powers overnight.
- The end of linear empires. Pearl-strings become obsolete.
- An intelligence revolution. Nuclear aircraft map and monitor the planet for the first time. Uncontacted civilizations are discovered from the air.
- The Reach achieves nuclear propulsion first. A unipolar moment that is now ending.

---

## VII. THE NARRATIVE PRESENT (~50 BP – Now)

### The Nuclear Aviation Era
Multiple great powers operate nuclear seaplane fleets. Endurance limited by crew provisions and fatigue (~3 weeks per patrol, extendable with tender support). The world is connected for the first time — but the connection is military and tenuous.

### The Reach-Lattice Discovery
The Reach discovers the Lattice via surveillance overflight — dense radio traffic, city lights, reactor isotopes from an archipelago 55,000 km away. Subsequent analysis reveals the Lattice already knows about the Reach. The discovery is **mutual**. Both sides have been monitoring each other's radio traffic for years. Neither has made formal contact.

**Current state:** Known mutual awareness without formal contact. Surveillance flights skirt each other's airspace. War plans are developed. The public on both sides is mostly unaware.

**The break:** First contact is made not by diplomats but by a **fishing fleet** — a trawler from a Reach-aligned client state encounters a Lattice fishing boat on a shared submarine plateau. The crews stare at each other. Someone waves. Both sides radio home.

### Active Threads
- The civilian nuclear cruise industry is developing, creating new class dynamics and strategic anxieties.
- The golden age of inter-archipelago aviation is emerging as reactor technology is gradually declassified.
- The substrate discovery is underway — the astronomer has found the star-placement anomaly, the geologist has found the plateau connectivity, the geneticists are closing in on the marine genome.
- The "unknown provenance" artifacts (literary works, visual art, consumer goods, food) are accumulating in the Reach's cultural intelligence files, pointing toward an external civilization that may not be *on* Aeolia at all.
- Approximately 15% of the planet's surface remains unmapped. The Dark Forest logic applies to the unmapped zones: any could contain peer-level threats.

### The 8,000–12,000 Cultures
Across Aeolia's surface, an estimated 8,000–12,000 distinct ethnolinguistic groups exist — comparable to Earth's pre-colonial count but distributed across 21× the surface area. The depth of divergence between macro-civilizations separated by gaps (up to 100,000 years) exceeds anything on Earth. Different writing systems, different musical scales, different agricultural bases, different philosophies. The nuclear age is connecting them all, for better or worse.
