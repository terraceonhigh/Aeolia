# AEOLIA — SERIES BIBLE

### A World of Ocean, Nuclear Seaplanes, and the Question of What Connection Costs

---

## Logline

On a planet 4.6 times Earth's size and 95% ocean, human civilizations developed on volcanic archipelagos isolated by impassable gaps for 100,000 years — until nuclear-powered seaplanes collapsed the distances and forced a world of strangers to become neighbors. The question is whether they can do this without becoming enemies.

## Core Thesis

**Connection is the most dangerous and most necessary thing a civilization can achieve.** Every technology that bridges distance — the sailing ship, the submarine cable, the nuclear seaplane — simultaneously enriches and endangers the civilizations it connects. The Silk Road carries silk and plague. The gap crossing carries trade goods and epidemiological catastrophe. The nuclear aircraft carries diplomats and the implicit threat of annihilation. Aeolia is a world where the cost of connection has been paid ten times over a millennium, and the question is whether the species has learned enough from the payment to get the next one right.

## Setting in One Paragraph

Aeolia is an ocean world — 185,000 km in circumference, 21 times Earth's surface area, 95%+ ocean. Exposed land consists entirely of volcanic archipelagos, the largest island roughly the size of Borneo. Human civilizations evolved on these islands and were separated by rising seas roughly 100,000 years ago. They developed independently for millennia, were serially reconnected through age-of-sail gap crossings (each a Columbian Exchange–level event), and have now entered the nuclear aviation era, in which reactor-powered flying boats can reach any point on the planet. Two peer civilizations — the Reach (structural analogue: the Anglo-Saxon thalassocratic arc; aesthetic: Chinese-coded) and the Lattice (structural analogue: imperial China; aesthetic: American-coded) — have recently discovered each other across 55,000 km of ocean. Neither has made formal contact. Both are watching. The Dark Forest is not interstellar. It's planetary.

## Tone

Aeolia is not dystopian. It's not utopian. It's *complicated* — the way Earth is complicated, the way any world is complicated when populated by intelligent beings who want contradictory things and have nuclear reactors. The tone is literary realism applied to a speculative premise: the world functions according to rigorous physical and social constraints, and the stories emerge from the characters navigating those constraints with the tools their civilization gave them.

The emotional register ranges from the intimate (a woman on a ferry looking at the harbor and thinking about a painting she saw) to the geopolitical (an admiral eating a fish sandwich at 2 AM and writing a memo that will reorganize national intelligence priorities) to the cosmic (an astronomer discovering that the stars were placed by hand). The register can shift within a single scene. The specificity of the physical world — the volcanic rock, the harbor smell, the ferry horn every fifteen minutes — is what grounds the larger themes.

Humor is present but not dominant. The comedy is structural — the gap between the cosmic scale of the world and the human scale of the people living in it. The admiral's fish-sandwich memo is funny because it's an existential first-contact document written in the cadence of a naval duty report. The civilian nuclear cruise is funny because it's an apocalypse-capable technology repurposed for cocktail hours. The DoorDash delivery is funny because interplanetary contact is mediated by a gig worker from Glendale.

## The Set-Dressing Swap

The single most distinctive production decision: the Reach (structurally Western/Anglo-Saxon) is presented in Chinese material culture. The Lattice (structurally Chinese/imperial-bureaucratic) is presented in American material culture. The dissonance between the aesthetic surface and the institutional structure beneath it is deliberate and productive. It prevents the audience from collapsing either civilization into a familiar stereotype. The Reach is not "space China." The Lattice is not "space America." Both are *Aeolian* — shaped by Aeolia's specific environmental pressures — and the borrowed aesthetics are costumes that highlight rather than conceal the alien structure underneath.

**Production rule:** Every scene in the Reach should produce a moment where the visual language says one thing and the institutional behavior says another. A parliamentary shouting match in a hall with curved eaves and red columns. A free-market trading floor where the traders wear silk robes. A waterfront bar fight where the combatants are dressed like Tang Dynasty gentlemen. The friction between the surface and the structure is the visual signature of the setting.

**Production rule:** Every scene in the Lattice should produce the inverse. A bureaucratic examination hall with fluorescent lighting and plastic chairs. A state-run newscast from a studio that looks like CNN. A political remonstrance delivered by an official in denim and work boots. The American surface conceals and reveals the bureaucratic structure beneath.

## Themes

**The sea as the universal condition.** On Aeolia, the sea is not a setting — it's the medium. Everyone is maritime. Every city is a harbor city. Every journey is a voyage. Every metaphor is nautical. The sea is what connects and what separates, what feeds and what kills, what carries the fleet and what swallows the fleet. The sea is the *first character in every scene* even when no one is looking at it.

**The cost of contact.** A millennium of serial New Worlds has given the Reach a rich, painful, unresolved relationship with the act of discovery. Every contact enriches and destroys. Every gap crossing is a gift and a plague. The Reach's intellectual class is simultaneously proud of its exploratory heritage and ashamed of what exploration has cost the explored. The encounter with the Lattice is the contact that might redeem the pattern or repeat it.

**The parochialism of universalism.** Both the Reach and the Lattice believe their values are universal — the Reach's liberty and open markets, the Lattice's procedural meritocracy and relational obligation. Both are wrong. Both are *specifically* wrong — their values are products of specific environmental conditions (archipelago spacing, tidal-flat agriculture) that are local, not cosmic. The encounter between the two civilizations is the moment when each discovers that its deepest convictions are *choices*, not truths. Whether this discovery is liberating or shattering depends on the character experiencing it.

**The artificial and the authentic.** The substrate-discovery arc — the astronomer who finds that the stars were placed, the geologist who finds the plateaus are connected, the physicist who finds the fine-structure constant is rational — raises the question of whether a designed world can produce authentic meaning. The publicist's answer ("the fish is still good") is the setting's thesis statement: the meaning is ours regardless of who built the conditions. The question is not settled. The question is the engine.
