# 05 — TECHNOLOGY

## Organizing Principle

Technology on Aeolia is shaped by two constraints that Earth's technology doesn't face: **everything crosses water** and **fossil fuels were never cheap**. These constraints produce an energy sequence, a logistics topology, and a consumer-electronics ecosystem that are structurally different from Earth's even when the underlying physics is identical.

---

## I. ENERGY SEQUENCE

| Earth Sequence | Aeolia Sequence |
|---|---|
| Wood/charcoal → coal → oil → nuclear/renewables | Wood/charcoal → wind/tidal → expensive coal → oil → nuclear |

**The tidal-industrial phase** has no Earth parallel. Before coal, Aeolian civilizations (especially the Lattice) developed sophisticated energy extraction from tidal flows — channeled currents driving mills and factories. This phase lasted centuries and produced industrial output (textiles, metalwork) using renewable maritime energy. Coal supplemented rather than replaced this system.

**Coal** exists but is submarine — deposited on drowned plateaus during glacial periods, mined from coastal margins. Never cheap. Mining is wet, dangerous, geographically constrained. The Industrial Revolution ignites slower and concentrates near shallow-water coal deposits.

**Oil** is offshore. Found in marine sediment on submarine plateaus. Offshore drilling is a smaller conceptual leap for maritime civilizations. Oil arrives later than on Earth but is exploited readily.

**Nuclear** is the breakthrough. Competes not with cheap fossil incumbents (as on Earth) but with *expensive* fossil fuels. The economic case is overwhelming. Every civilization that can build a reactor wants to. The nuclear transition is faster, more enthusiastic, and less politically contested than on Earth.

**Greenhouse consequence:** Less fossil fuel burned = smaller carbon debt at the nuclear threshold. Climate politics focus less on anthropogenic warming, more on the next glacial cycle and what happens when the drowned roads re-emerge.

## II. MARITIME TECHNOLOGY

### The Reach's Sailing Lineage
**Environment:** Open mid-latitude ocean, long-fetch swells, frequent storms.
**Hull evolution:** Outrigger → double-hulled voyaging canoe → deep-keeled monohull → caravel/carrack equivalent → full-rigged ship.
**Key transition:** Multihull to monohull. Multihulls are fast but structurally vulnerable in steep swells. Deep monohulls sacrifice speed for survivability.
**Rig:** Square rigs for running before westerlies. Heavy canvas, thick cordage, massive spars. The ability to reduce sail quickly is valued above maximum sail area.
**Character:** Toughness over speed. Brutal competence. Ships built to take punishment and bring you home.

### The Lattice's Sailing Lineage
**Environment:** Shallow, sheltered inter-island waters. Tidal currents, reef passages, narrow channels.
**Hull evolution:** Outrigger → multihull raft → flat-bottomed barge → junk equivalent.
**Key design:** Flat bottom. Shallow draft. Can navigate tidal flats, beach at low tide, enter harbors impassable to deep-keeled vessels.
**Rig:** Junk rig — balanced lug on battens. Easy to handle, efficient on all points of sail, self-feathering in gusts. The bureaucrat's sail: demands procedure, not virtuosity.
**Character:** Efficiency and standardization. Fleets of identical vessels on tidal schedules. The individual ship is a component in a logistics system.

### Naval Encounter
The Reach's deep-keeled ships dominate blue water. The Lattice's flat-bottomed fleet dominates littoral waters. In the Lattice's home waters, the Reach's "superior" ships *cannot operate* — too deep-drafted for the shallow channels. Context determines advantage.

## III. NUCLEAR SEAPLANES

### Why Seaplanes
- **No runways.** Volcanic islands lack flat land for 3,500+ m runways. The ocean is the runway.
- **The drag penalty doesn't matter.** Nuclear propulsion makes the flying-boat hull's aerodynamic inefficiency irrelevant. The reactor doesn't care about drag.
- **The maritime civilization already fights salt water.** Anti-corrosion technology is mature. Salt water isn't a special problem — it's Tuesday.
- **Dispersal.** A seaplane can land on any sheltered water. Millions of potential landing sites. Nearly impossible to destroy the fleet in a first strike.

### Airframe Characteristics
Both the Reach and the Lattice converge on similar designs (same physics, same constraints):
- Gross takeoff weight: hundreds of tons
- Flying-boat hull: deep-V forward, stepped planing surfaces, spray rails
- Engines: high-mounted on pylons above the wing (prevents spray ingestion)
- Reactor and shielding: low, in the hull (weight aids water stability)
- Crew: 20–40 (four-watch rotation)
- Endurance: 2–4 weeks (limited by provisions, not fuel)
- Silhouette: bulkier, more nautical than conventional aircraft. Ships that fly.

### Operational Differences

| Aspect | The Reach | The Lattice |
|---|---|---|
| **Reactor philosophy** | Reliability, modularity. Designed for field repair by competent technicians following documented procedures. Systems over people. | Elegance, integration. Requires specialist engineer. People over systems. |
| **Command structure** | Collegial. Department heads have autonomy. Challenge culture. Commander sleeps through night watch. | Hierarchical. Information flows vertically. Commander briefed on everything. Junior officers don't challenge seniors. |
| **Crew rotation** | Aggressive. Multiple crews per airframe. Aircraft belongs to the service. | Permanent assignment. Same crew for years/decades. Aircraft has a personality. |
| **Mission philosophy** | Expeditionary. Forward presence. Independence. | Defensive. Perimeter patrol. Coordination from center. |
| **Failure mode** | Systemic rigidity (procedure doesn't cover the situation) | Individual fragility (the specialist is incapacitated) |
| **Aesthetics** | Restrained naval livery. Named after historical figures and literary references. Interior: understated elegance, natural materials, ordered calm. | Bold markings. Named after geographic features (reefs, currents, straits). Interior: utilitarian steel and polymer, fluorescent lighting, diner-aesthetic mess. |

### The Seaplane Tender
A surface ship carrying provisions, spare parts, and fresh crew for mid-ocean rendezvous. The tender extends patrol endurance from weeks to months. Not an aircraft carrier — the seaplane provides its own flight capability. The tender is the logistical umbilical.

### Harbor Operations
Nuclear seaplanes operate from harbor basins — the same harbors as cargo ships, fishing fleets, and civilian seaplanes. Military and civilian infrastructure is colocated by necessity. Maintenance from floating docks. Ordnance loaded from barges. Crew quarters onshore in the waterfront district.

## IV. CHEMICAL AVIATION

Chemical-fueled aircraft exist and fill the same niches as on Earth, but their *strategic* significance is radically different:

| Earth Equivalent | Aeolia Role | Range | Who Uses It |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flanker / F-15 | Archipelago defense fighter | ~1,500 km combat radius | National air forces |
| Gulfstream | Executive inter-island transport | ~8,000–12,000 km | Political/economic elite |
| 737 / WestJet | Regional airline workhorse | ~2,000–5,000 km | General public |
| C-130 | Island freight and resupply | ~3,000–4,000 km | Military logistics, cargo |
| Cessna / turboprop | Short-hop inter-island | ~500–1,500 km | Commuters, mail, medevac |

**The wall:** Chemical aviation stops at ~12,000 km. Nuclear aviation has no range limit. There is no mid-range option. Hub airports where passengers transfer from the chemical to the nuclear network are the most strategically important real estate in any country.

### Golden Age of Inter-Archipelago Flight
Emerging as reactor technology is gradually declassified. Requirements: small civilian reactor (molten-salt, composite shielding, ~35% mass of military standard), production-line manufacturing, civilian fuel authority, harbor-network upgrades, and a ticket price at roughly two months' average salary. The 200–300 passenger nuclear seaplane as airline workhorse. See Cultural Atlas (07) for social implications.

## V. COMMUNICATIONS & INFORMATION

### The Cable Network
**Every cable is submarine.** There is no terrestrial fiber, no terrestrial copper, no buried cable of any kind. Every connection crosses ocean floor.

**Cost:** 2–5× terrestrial equivalent per km. The network is the most expensive infrastructure project in the Reach's history.

**Topology:** Radial, not mesh. Hub islands connected by fat trunks; minor islands on thin branches. Very little redundancy. A single trunk failure can isolate entire archipelago groups.

**Vulnerability:** Cables break constantly — volcanic seafloor, tidal scour, anchor strikes, trawler damage. Mean failure rate higher than Earth's. Cable-repair fleet is a critical national asset.

**Strategic implication:** An adversary that cuts cables drops the Reach from information-age to telegraph-age economy. The admiralty monitors cable status in real time.

### Radio
HF/VHF/UHF/microwave. Essential backup to cable. Every island, every ship, every aircraft has radio. HF ionospheric skip reaches ~3,000–5,000 km — a sliver of the planet's surface.

**Bandwidth:** Kilobits/sec (HF) to megabits/sec (microwave). Six orders of magnitude less than fiber. Radio carries voice and text. It does not carry video or rich media.

**Two-tier information society:** Cable-connected islands have full-bandwidth access. Radio-connected islands have text and voice only. The smartphone experience is location-dependent in a way Earth's hasn't been since 2010.

### Satellites
~12, in expensive orbits. Launch costs ~4× Earth's per kg. Provide limited communication, some navigation data, some imagery. Sparse coverage — revisit times in days. No GPS. No satellite Internet. No satellite phone network. Satellites are strategic luxuries, not consumer infrastructure.

### The Data Courier
A person or service that physically transports storage devices between islands on nuclear flights. Provides bandwidth-on-demand to communities the cable network doesn't serve. Arrival is a social event on outer islands.

### Social Media
Exists but shaped by propagation delay. Posts spread outward from cable hubs at speeds determined by infrastructure quality. Inner islands live in the present; outer islands live in the recent past; frontier islands live in the deep past. Discourse is temporally stratified. Outrage cycles are dampened by delay. Accidentally healthier than Earth's social media.

## VI. THE SMARTPHONE

**Hardware:** Waterproof as baseline (maritime environment). Sealed housing, gasketed ports. Includes cellular radio, Wi-Fi equivalent, Bluetooth equivalent, AND a long-range HF transceiver (survival feature for open-water emergencies). Display, camera, inertial sensors.

**The navigation app:** Uses phone camera for star identification, computes celestial fixes, maintains dead-reckoning track. The most sophisticated software on the device. Politically controversial — the navigator guild opposes it as a threat to institutional knowledge. Both sides of the debate have valid points.

**Supply chain:** All-maritime. Every component crosses water. Silicon from one island, wafer fab on another (chosen for low seismic activity), packaging on a third, assembly on a fourth. 10–15 individual sea voyages from raw material to finished device. Total ocean transit per device: ~15,000–20,000 km. Supply chain is fragile, buffer-dependent, and adds cost that terrestrial supply chains don't face.

## VII. SPACEFLIGHT

### The Rocket Equation on Aeolia
- Delta-v to low orbit: ~19–20 km/s (vs. Earth's ~9.5 km/s)
- Chemical propulsion mass ratio: ~85:1 (absurd; payload fraction < 0.5%)
- Nuclear thermal propulsion mass ratio: ~9:1 (comparable to Earth chemical rockets reaching Earth orbit)
- Nuclear thermal is the only viable option. The Reach already has reactor expertise.

### Launch Infrastructure
All maritime. Floating launch platforms anchored in deep water. Rockets fueled from tanker vessels. Countdown managed from ships. First stage falls into ocean. Nuclear second stage is a waste-disposal headache.

### Reentry
Velocity: ~17 km/s (vs. Earth's ~7.8 km/s). Kinetic energy nearly 5× higher. Conventional ablative heat shields may be insufficient. Skip-reentry profile: shallow atmospheric entry, damped oscillation over 30–60 minutes, gradual energy bleed. Splashdown in the ocean (the one easy part).

### The Space Program
Small. ~12 launches total. 3–4 crewed missions. Single-person capsules (mass budget is brutal). The navigator-astronaut who made the first orbital flight coined "We're barnacles" — the defining image of Aeolia from space (a blue sphere with no visible features except clouds). The orbital photograph reshapes planetary self-understanding.

The program doesn't grow into an industry. The cost-per-kg is too high. Most space-based services (communication, navigation, observation) are cheaper to provide terrestrially. The Reach chose cables over satellites. The sky is a door that opened once and mostly closed again.
