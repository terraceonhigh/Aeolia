# 06 — CHARACTERS & POV

## Casting Principle

The Reach's professional and institutional class is **substantially female** — a structural consequence of 100,000 years of maritime gender economics where men's long absences at sea put women in control of the shore economy, the institutions, and the continuity of civilization. This is not a political decision. It's an environmental one. See Cultural Atlas (07) for full analysis.

## PRIMARY CHARACTERS

### The Urbanite
**Role:** The audience's entry point. An educated private citizen of the Reach's capital.
**Demographics:** Mid-30s. Lives in a mid-level apartment on the volcanic slope. Works in a white-collar profession (unspecified — she could be an architect, an insurance analyst, a university lecturer). Single or partnered, not central to her characterization.
**Function:** She is the *reasonable person* who encounters artifacts from an unknown civilization and interprets them through the lens of her own experience. Her interpretations reveal Aeolia by revealing the limits of her understanding. When she looks at a Turner painting and can't identify the river, the gap between what she sees and what we see IS the world.
**Voice:** Observational, reflective, quietly precise. She notices sensory details — the smell of the stairwell, the warmth of the volcanic rock, the specific quality of evening light on the harbor. She processes intellectually but feels physically. Her emotional responses manifest as bodily sensations before they become thoughts.
**Arc:** From comfortable insularity (the ferry, the apartment, the harbor she knows) to vertiginous awareness (the world is larger, stranger, and possibly *constructed*). She doesn't become a different person. She becomes the same person with a wider horizon.
**Signature gesture:** Putting her hand on the warm rock wall of her stairwell every evening. The rock is specific, familiar, hers — and will eventually become evidence of design.
**Relationship:** Close friend of the publicist. They drink together at a harbor bar and process the artifacts over drinks.

### The Publicist
**Role:** The intellectual engine. The person who publishes the artifacts and frames them for the Reach's reading public.
**Demographics:** 40s–50s. Runs a publishing office in the capital's mid-level literary district. Career editor with a reputation for literary judgment and editorial courage.
**Function:** She is the *institutional actor* — the person whose professional decisions (what to publish, how to frame it, what editorial note to write) shape the Reach's collective understanding of the artifacts and, eventually, of its own constructed nature. Her editorial notes are the setting's thesis statements.
**Voice:** Precise, controlled, intellectually fearless. She writes the way a surgeon operates — with economy, confidence, and full awareness that mistakes are permanent. Her editorial notes are the best prose in the Reach.
**Arc:** From professional curiosity (interesting texts from unknown origin) to classified knowledge (the artifacts describe a civilization that built our world) to the most widely read editorial in the Reach's history ("the fish is still good"). She moves from *publishing* meaning to *making* meaning.
**Key line:** "Whoever built the world built the conditions. We built the meaning. And the meaning is ours."
**Relationship:** Close friend of the urbanite. Professional contact with the admiral (they've never been friends; the fish sandwich and the substrate discovery make them collaborators). Receives the astronomer's letter.

### The Admiral
**Role:** The strategic mind. The woman who commands the Reach's nuclear seaplane fleet and who must assess the implications of the artifacts for national security.
**Demographics:** Late 50s. Twenty years in the nuclear fleet, risen through the ranks during the gender-integration period. Career military with the intelligence community's trust.
**Function:** She translates the artifacts from *cultural* objects to *strategic* intelligence. The publicist asks "what does this mean?" The admiral asks "what does this threaten?" She's the person who eats the fish sandwich at 2 AM and writes the memo that reorganizes intelligence priorities.
**Voice:** Clipped, precise, emotionally contained in professional settings. The private notebook entries are where her interiority shows — reflective, honest, surprisingly tender. The contrast between her public voice (naval duty report) and her private voice (the fish tasted like home) is her characterization.
**Arc:** From threat assessment (the source civilization is a strategic unknown) to recognition (the source civilization built our world) to the hardest question of her career: *is this a threat or an invitation?*
**Key line:** "I am not accustomed to interpreting fish sandwiches as diplomatic communications."

### The Astronomer
**Role:** The discoverer. The scientist who finds the seams in the substrate.
**Demographics:** 30s–40s. Works at the national observatory on the highest volcanic peak. Careful, thorough, trained to look at distributions rather than individual data points.
**Function:** She provides the *evidence* that transforms the artifact question from "who is out there watching us?" to "who *built* us?" The star-distribution anomaly is the first seam. Her subsequent investigation (proper motions, color diversity, resource distribution, language universals) assembles the case.
**Voice:** Scientific precision in professional writing. The letter to the publicist is where her humanity shows — frightened, grateful, reaching across disciplinary boundaries because the evidence is too large for any single discipline.
**Arc:** From routine astrometry (updating the star catalog) to the most disturbing discovery in Aeolian history (the stars were placed by hand). She doesn't become an activist or a prophet. She remains a scientist. The courage is in the *publishing*, not in the *believing*.
**Key line (from the letter):** "I am frightened. I am also, in a way I did not expect, grateful."

### The Navigator-Astronaut
**Role:** The witness. The one person who has seen Aeolia from outside.
**Demographics:** Late 30s at time of orbital flight. Selected from the nuclear seaplane fleet's test-pilot cadre. A navigator, because the Reach sends navigators into the unknown.
**Function:** She provides the *overview* — the orbital perspective that shows Aeolia as it truly is: a blue sphere with no visible features except clouds. Her radio transmission ("we're barnacles") reshapes planetary self-understanding.
**Voice:** Laconic in professional mode (test pilot/navigator). The orbital descriptions are where she opens up — precise, awestruck, slightly bewildered by the scale of what she's seeing.
**Arc:** She goes up, she comes down, she never flies again. She becomes a navigation instructor at the naval academy, teaching the old skills with an intensity born from having seen the stars from the other side of the atmosphere. She's the person who *went through the door* and can never go back.

### The Chef
**Role:** The craftsman. The unconscious artist. The person who embodies the setting's thesis without knowing it.
**Demographics:** 60s. Forty-three years in the same basement restaurant. Six seats. No sign.
**Function:** He is the *proof* that the meaning is real regardless of who built the conditions. His forty-three years of knife work, his twelve-piece omakase, his mastery of a fish that was (unknown to him) genetically designed — all of this is *his*, authentically, irrespective of the substrate's artificial origin. He is the publicist's thesis in human form.
**Voice:** He barely speaks. He communicates through fish. His one spoken line in the setting's key moments: "The fish is still good."
**Arc:** He doesn't have an arc. He is the *constant*. He was there before the artifacts, before the discovery, before the crisis. He will be there after. The fish is still good. The restaurant is still open. The knife still moves.

## SECONDARY CHARACTERS

### The Geologist
Colleague of the astronomer. Discovers the submarine plateau connectivity anomaly independently. The Monte Carlo simulation: ten thousand random volcanic-activity models, none producing a connected network linking more than three archipelago groups (the actual network links eleven).

### The Young Essayist
Twenty-three. University student from a working-class outer island. Wins a lottery ticket for the civilian nuclear cruise. Writes the essay about the overview that redefines the cruise from luxury product to philosophical experience. "I thought the sea was the thing between the islands. From up here I can see that the islands are the thing between the sea."

### The Committee Chair
Retired admiral. Reads the astronomer's findings on the Mulan ballad's authenticity. His judgment: "My analysts could not have written that. No intelligence operation produces lines like that. The poem is real."

### The Lattice's Mulan Figure
Known only through the ballad. A woman who disguised herself as a man and served twelve years in the garrison military to protect her elderly father. Refused the institutional reward. Asked for a fast ride home.

## THE GAIAN CHARACTERS (Meta-Layer)

### Ricky
**The DoorDash driver.** Twenty-six. Drives a 2019 Honda Civic with a cracked windshield. Sees a $347,000 tip on the app and delivers a Big Mac across an ontological threshold in wet jeans. Does not understand what happened. Keeps the app open.
**Function:** He is the *anti-narrator* — the person who participates in first contact without knowing it's first contact. His ordinariness is the point. Arthur Dent with a paper bag.

### Deb
Retired postal worker. Bakersfield. Owned the 1992 Toyota Corolla for thirty years. Cried when she sold it. Her car is now the most studied artifact in Aeolian history. She doesn't know.
