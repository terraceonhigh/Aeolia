# Chapter 9: The Saak Transition — Energy Economics and the Industrial Scramble

*From* Introduction to Reach History, *3rd ed., eds. Halarua & Temani, University of the Northern Reach Press*

---

## 9.1 The Energy Regime Before Naphtha

To understand the industrial scramble, one must first understand what it displaced — and why the displacement was so rapid and so total.

The pre-industrial Reach ran on biological energy. Grain, taro, and papa starch fed populations. Seric and kapas fiber clothed them. Saak oil lit their interiors and waterproofed their hulls. The six great fisheries species — sthaq, saak, tunnu, sardai, bakala, kauri — provided protein, fat, currency, and export commodity simultaneously. Of these, saak held a unique structural position: it was the only high-energy-density fuel available in sufficient quantity for industrial-scale use before the exploitation of subsurface carbon.

Rendered saak oil — pressed from the small, oily fish that ran the cold-water river systems and coastal deltas each spring — was the lamp fuel of the northern Reach. The grease trails documented by Temani (*Oral Cartography of the Pre-Contact North*, Northern Reach Press, 14th ed.) were fundamentally saak oil distribution networks, their routes determined not by population centers but by the locations of rendering sites and the seasonal movements of the fish. Cities in the temperate interior were, in an important sense, lit by saak.

The economics were stable. Saak oil production was labor-intensive, geographically constrained to cold-water coastal systems, and not meaningfully scalable beyond what the fish population could sustain. A polity with excellent saak access had a real but bounded advantage over one without. The Varandei houses, which dominated the northern rendering industry across most of the third and second millennia before present, could charge monopoly prices for saak oil to the inland settlements — but those prices were ultimately constrained by the biological ceiling of what the fisheries could produce.

Naphtha removed that ceiling overnight.

## 9.2 The Geological Survey as Military Intelligence

The history of the naphtha scramble cannot be separated from the history of geological surveying. Surface seeps — natural bitumen formations visible as tar springs, oil slicks on coastal water, or the legendary "eternal flames" that burned from gas vents in volcanic terrain — had been known to coastal populations for millennia before their industrial exploitation. The Baku culture sites in the volcanic northern archipelagos had worshipped the eternal flame for generations; the bitumen springs of the equatorial belt were used for waterproofing hull planks and rope preservation since the early relay era; the subsurface carbon formations of the shelf-edge islands were known to geological practitioners by the first century of the colonial period.

What changed in the early industrial era was not the knowledge of where the deposits were. It was the ability to exploit them.

The first working refineries — processing surface seeps into lamp oil, then into combustion fuel for stationary engines, then into fuel for mobile propulsion systems — appeared in the late fourth century before present. Within fifty years, the price of saak oil in northern relay markets had fallen by two-thirds. Within a century, the Varandei rendering industry had collapsed. The grease trails of the oral cartographic tradition became, within two generations, useless geography.

The polities that grasped the strategic significance of this transition earliest were not necessarily those with the largest naphtha deposits. They were those with the geological survey capacity to locate deposits in advance of competitors, and the naval reach to claim them before anyone else arrived.

The admiralty archives of the southern confederacy (catalogued in full by Halarua, *The Carbon Surveys: Admiralty Intelligence and Resource Colonialism*, Vol. I-III) document a systematic program of geological intelligence-gathering beginning approximately 380 years before present — several decades before the first working refinery was operational. Survey vessels operating under commercial cover were mapping potential naphtha deposits across the frontier archipelagos while the technology to exploit them was still being developed. The geological survey had become an instrument of forward military planning before it was an instrument of forward industrial planning.

## 9.3 Three Phases of the Scramble

Historians of the industrial transition conventionally divide the naphtha scramble into three phases, though the boundaries between them were never clean and varied substantially by region.

**The Commercial Phase** (roughly years -370 to -290) was characterized by the establishment of commercial extraction rights rather than direct territorial control. Trading companies with admiralty backing would approach island authorities holding surface seep sites, offer technical assistance in exchange for extraction monopolies, and gradually establish a physical presence that was commercial in legal form but military in practical function. The island populations were rarely in a position to refuse — the asymmetry of technical capacity was too large — but neither were they formally colonized. The legal fiction of commercial relationship persisted for as long as it was convenient.

**The Consolidation Phase** (roughly years -290 to -220) saw the conversion of commercial extraction rights into territorial control as the strategic importance of naphtha became undeniable and the leading powers moved to eliminate ambiguity about ownership. The pearl-string garrison technique, refined across three centuries of colonial expansion, proved equally well-suited to the defense of carbon-rich island groups as it had to the protection of sea-lane chokepoints. The doctrine of the feitoria — the fortified commercial post that constituted the smallest unit of administered control — was adapted without significant modification.

**The Depletion Phase** (roughly years -220 to present) describes the ongoing negotiation of an energy economy in which accessible naphtha deposits are declining in productivity while the infrastructure dependent on them continues to expand. This phase has no terminus in the historical record for the obvious reason that we are currently inside it. Polities at the leading edge of tech capacity are beginning to access subsurface deposits that were inaccessible to earlier survey techniques; polities at lower tech levels are exhausting their accessible deposits while unable to reach deeper reserves. The strategic map is still in motion.

## 9.4 The Saak Cultures: A Case Study in Economic Displacement

The communities most directly harmed by the naphtha transition were those whose economic basis was saak oil production. The Varandei rendering houses represent the best-documented case, but the pattern was reproduced across the northern Reach wherever saak oil had constituted a significant economic base.

The displacement followed a predictable sequence. First, naphtha imports from early production sites undercut the local lamp oil market. The price decline was rapid — typically 40–60% within a decade of reliable supply — and immediately destroyed the marginal economics of the more distant saak rendering operations. The small family-scale renderers, who had sustained themselves by selling oil to the local market at prices that covered their seasonal labor costs, were the first casualties.

The larger Varandei houses had the capital to survive the initial price shock and attempted to reposition. Several invested in the emerging naphtha distribution network, using their existing trade relationships to become brokers of naphtha rather than producers of saak oil. Some succeeded. The majority did not. The key asset of the Varandei houses — their relationships, their knowledge of the relay routes, their grading standards — transferred poorly to a commodity market that ran on volume logistics rather than artisanal quality differentiation.

The saak fisheries did not immediately collapse. The fish were still caught; the population still ate them. What collapsed was the economic logic that had sustained the communities as trading peoples rather than subsistence fishers. A saak fishing village in the third century before present had been embedded in a commercial network that stretched across a dozen archipelagos. A saak fishing village in the first century before present was isolated. The network was gone because its commodity was gone.

Oral histories collected from descendant communities in the Varandei archipelago (Temani, *The Last Renderers*, Field Research Archive Vol. 7) describe a generational memory of the transition that runs as follows: the grandparents' generation lived as merchants; the parents' generation lived as fishers; the grandchildren's generation lives as harbor laborers for ships using naphtha. The descent is described not as tragedy but as a kind of puzzlement — how did something that had sustained us for so long become worthless so quickly?

The answer, which the oral histories do not quite arrive at, is that the value of saak oil was never intrinsic. It was a function of its position in an energy regime. When the regime changed, the oil changed with it.

## 9.5 Structural Lessons

The saak transition has received less analytical attention than it deserves, partly because its victims were marginal actors who left limited documentary records, and partly because the naphtha scramble has been so thoroughly studied that it has absorbed most of the available scholarly attention for the industrial period.

What the saak transition illustrates is a principle that recurs throughout Reach economic history: **the value of a resource is always relative to the alternatives**. Saak oil was not replaced because it became scarce. It was replaced because something denser, more energy-rich, and more scalable became available. The fishing communities whose economic position depended on saak oil's market value had no mechanism to adjust to a change in that value that originated outside their ecological system entirely.

The pyra situation that now confronts the Reach has certain structural similarities. Pyra ore was, for most of its known history, a geological curiosity — present in the volcanic substrates of certain island groups, mined in small quantities for use in specialty alloys and certain chemical processes, valued by geologists and metallurgists but not by admiralties. The demonstration of controlled fission changed that. In the time it took for news of the first chain reaction to reach the outlying archipelagos of the Reach — measured in months, not years, at the communication speeds of the industrial era — pyra deposits went from geological curiosity to existential military resource.

The populations living above those deposits had no warning. They did not know what was under their islands. Many of them still don't.
