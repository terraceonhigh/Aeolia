# Chapter 8: What the Sovereignty Owes — Taxation, Distribution, and the Obligations of Citizenship

*From* The Circuit and the Contract: A Young Person's History of the Southern Sovereignty, *4th ed., Polytechnic Press of the Southern Sovereignty, Educational Division*

*Approved for use in Sovereignty schools, Years 6–8*

---

## The Basic Question

Every student reading this book has probably heard a Sovereignty adult say something like: "We pay taxes so we can have schools, roads, and defense." This is true. It is also incomplete.

Taxes in the Sovereignty do not simply pay for services. They express a theory of membership: who is part of the Sovereignty's political community, to what degree, and what that membership obligates them to contribute. The services provided by taxation are what membership produces. The question of who pays for what is the question of what membership means.

This chapter describes what the Sovereignty's membership structure looks like and why taxation works the way it does. It also describes some genuine disagreements about whether the current structure is fair.

---

## Three Tiers of Membership

The Sovereignty's membership structure has three formal tiers.

**Full citizenship** — held by the populations of the Sovereignty's core archipelagos, those that were original signatories to the First Connectivity or joined in the founding generations. Full citizens vote in Circuit Assembly elections, are eligible for Commerce Council and Coordination Executive positions, have access to the full range of the Sovereignty's commercial courts and social services, and pay the full Sovereignty taxation schedule.

**Circuit membership** — held by the populations of archipelagos that joined the Sovereignty's commercial and legal network after the founding period but have accepted the Sovereignty's full commercial standards, dispute resolution, and legal framework. Circuit members do not vote in Circuit Assembly elections; they have representation through the Commerce Council's regional allocation (chapter 6 described this). They pay a reduced taxation schedule — approximately 75% of the full citizen rate — because they have less formal representation in the Sovereignty's governance. They have access to commercial courts and most social services but with longer processing times and some restrictions on appeals to the Circuit Assembly.

**Treaty relationship** — held by polities that have commercial agreements with the Sovereignty but have not accepted full legal integration. Treaty polities use the Sovereignty's Bridge Documentation and can bring commercial disputes to RSC arbitration, but they are not within the Sovereignty's fiscal community. They pay commercial fees for the services they use but no general taxation.

This three-tier structure reflects the Sovereignty's founding logic: membership is earned through integration, not imposed by force. Polities enter the fiscal community by accepting the legal and institutional obligations that membership entails.

## What Taxes Fund

The Sovereignty's taxation revenue is divided by the Circuit Assembly's annual allocation into four major categories.

**Infrastructure:** The relay network's physical maintenance — ports, navigation aids, communication relays, and the administered shipping schedule's operational support — consumes approximately 35% of taxation revenue in most years. This is the largest single category because the relay network is the Sovereignty's core commercial asset, and its maintenance is a genuine collective good: no individual polity could maintain the full network, but every polity benefits from it.

**Commercial services:** The Commerce Council's quality certification system, the RSC's Bridge Documentation administration, and the commercial banking system's liquidity support consume approximately 20% of revenue. These services are directly commercial rather than physical infrastructure, but they have the same collective-good property: their benefits are non-excludable (any polity trading in the Sovereignty's network benefits from consistent quality standards) and non-rival (one polity's use of quality certification does not reduce another's).

**Defense:** The Sovereignty's pyra arsenal, fleet operations, and the Defense Standing Committee's classified programs consume approximately 25% of revenue. This was the smallest category before the Strange Peace; it has grown to its current size over the forty-seven years of the deterrence era. The defense allocation is the most politically contested spending category because the benefits of deterrence are shared by all Sovereignty members, but the arsenal's strategic position and the fleet's deployment patterns favor some members' geographic security more than others'.

**Social services:** Health, education, and emergency support consume approximately 20% of revenue. These services are distributed to circuit members and full citizens; treaty relationship polities do not participate.

## The Western Basin Debate

Chapter 3 described the Western Question: the persistent tension between the eastern basin's commercial sophistication and the western basin's caloric foundation role. The taxation structure is where this tension becomes most practically contentious.

The western basin pays the full taxation schedule on its emmer, sago, and papa production — these are taxed at the same rate as the eastern basin's nori oil production and char exports. But the western basin's goods, as chapter 7 described, receive lower commercial energy in the relay network (the Prebisch-Singer terms-of-trade problem). They pay full taxes on income that is structurally discounted.

Western basin delegates to the Circuit Assembly have argued for four decades that the taxation schedule should be adjusted to account for the terms-of-trade disadvantage: that bulk calorie producers should be taxed at a lower rate, or that their infrastructure allocation should be proportionally larger, to compensate for the structural discount they face in commercial exchange. Eastern basin delegates have resisted these adjustments on the grounds that the taxation schedule reflects income equally, and that providing different rates for different commodity classes introduces distortions that would damage the administered trade system's integrity.

The Commerce Council's most recent review (Year 40 SP) proposed a compromise: a "primary goods investment credit" that would allow western basin producers to deduct a portion of infrastructure investment from their tax liability, recognizing that the equatorial belt's infrastructure needs are higher relative to its commercial income than the temperate zones' needs. The credit has been approved in principle but not yet funded; the annual allocation process has not provided sufficient budget to implement it at the scale the western basin delegates consider meaningful.

This debate illustrates a general tension in the Sovereignty's governance: the fiscal structure was designed to be neutral between commercial sectors, but commercial sectors are not symmetric in their structural positions. Neutrality in a structurally asymmetric system produces asymmetric outcomes.

## The Defense Allocation Controversy

The defense allocation has produced a different but equally persistent controversy.

When the Strange Peace began in Year 1 SP, the Sovereignty's defense spending was primarily naval: fleet operations and the relay network's security against piracy and commercial disruption. The pyra arsenal was small, concentrated, and accounted for a small fraction of total defense spending.

Over forty-seven years, the pyra arsenal has grown. Not because the Sovereignty has decided to achieve offensive capability — the Sovereignty's declared strategic doctrine is second-strike deterrence, and the arsenal's design is calibrated to that doctrine — but because second-strike credibility requires resilience against first-strike scenarios, and resilience requires redundancy, dispersal, and ongoing investment in delivery reliability. A credible second-strike force is expensive to maintain.

The question of how defense spending is allocated within the Sovereignty has no clean answer because the deterrent's collective good property is imperfect. The Reach's potential first strike would affect Sovereignty population and economic centers, but not all Sovereignty population and economic centers equally. The most likely Reach target scenarios, according to the classified planning that the Circuit Assembly receives in redacted form, prioritize the eastern basin's industrial infrastructure and the central spine's relay network. The western basin is a target because it feeds the Sovereignty, but it is a lower-priority target than the cores of the Sovereignty's economic capacity.

This means the eastern basin and central spine benefit more from the pyra deterrent than the western basin does — not because the deterrent is designed that way, but because the Reach's targeting logic responds to economic importance. The western basin pays an equal share of the defense budget for a deterrent that primarily protects others' economic assets.

Western basin delegates have raised this issue in three consecutive Assembly sessions. The Coordination Executive's response has been that deterrence is an indivisible collective good — that the Sovereignty cannot provide partial deterrence, that the deterrent protects all members or none — and that unequal risk distribution is an unavoidable feature of the Sovereignty's geography, not of its fiscal choices.

This is formally correct. It is not a satisfying answer to someone whose commercial income is structurally discounted AND whose defense contribution disproportionately subsidizes others' security.

## What Students Should Know

Two things are worth holding in tension as students finishing this chapter move on to the rest of their education.

The first: the Sovereignty's taxation and membership structure was built to be fair, and by most measures it is fairer than the arrangements it replaced. The First Connectivity's contract system had no western basin representation at all; the commerce flowed through relay nodes that extracted a share of the value created by western basin agricultural production without giving western basin producers any voice in how that value was distributed. The Circuit Assembly, with its proportional representation and minimum seat guarantees, was a genuine improvement.

The second: a system that is fairer than what came before can still be structurally unfair to some of its members. The western basin's combination of terms-of-trade disadvantage, equal taxation, and lower proportional defense benefit is not the result of anyone's malice. It is the accumulated result of design choices made at each stage of the Sovereignty's history by people who were trying to build something that would work — and who were not, at the relevant moments, primarily concerned with whether the western basin's specific situation was being fairly addressed.

These two things can be simultaneously true. The Sovereignty's governance literature refers to this as the "structural fairness problem" — the gap between a system's formal neutrality and its distributional outcomes. The gap does not close automatically. It requires ongoing political attention from people who take the time to understand why the gap exists.

You are now some of those people.

## Questions for Discussion

1. The Sovereignty's three-tier membership structure means different people have different rights and obligations. Is this fairer than a structure where everyone has the same rights and obligations regardless of how they joined? What would you need to know to answer that question?

2. Taxation pays for collective goods — things that benefit everyone but that no individual can provide alone. The text gives three examples: infrastructure, quality standards, and deterrence. Are all three genuinely collective goods in the same way? Who benefits from each, and are the benefits equally distributed?

3. The western basin pays equal taxes but receives structurally lower commercial income. Should tax systems adjust for structural disadvantage, or is equal-rate taxation inherently fair? What would "fair" mean in this context?

4. The Coordination Executive argues that deterrence is indivisible — you can't provide partial deterrence. Is this true? Can you imagine a deterrence architecture that would protect the western basin proportionally less while protecting the eastern basin more? Would such an architecture be desirable?

5. The chapter ends by saying the structural fairness problem "requires ongoing political attention from people who take the time to understand why the gap exists." What does this mean in practice? What would it look like for a student to be one of those people?
