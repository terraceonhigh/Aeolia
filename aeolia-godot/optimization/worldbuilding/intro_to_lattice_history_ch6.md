# Chapter 6: The Sovereignty's Constitution — Legal Integration in Three Basins

*From* Introduction to Lattice History, *2nd ed., ed. Selvanayagam, Polytechnic Press of the Southern Sovereignty*

---

## 6.1 The Problem the Constitution Had to Solve

The Lattice's founding political challenge — the problem that all subsequent constitutional arrangements have been attempts to manage — is the three-basin coordination problem described in Ch. 1. Three basins with different oceanographic characters, different seasonal windows, different crop ecologies, and different institutional histories had to be governed as a single unit capable of coordinated strategic response. The question was not whether to coordinate — the commercial benefits of integration were sufficient to make coordination desirable for all three basins — but how to coordinate without reducing the constitutional arrangement to the dominance of any one basin over the other two.

This question is structurally familiar from other polycentric political arrangements: federal systems in Earth's history faced the same challenge of aggregating distinct regional interests into coherent central policy without allowing any component unit to capture the central apparatus. What is distinctive about the Lattice's version of this problem is the specific asymmetry of its component units: the western basin's demographic majority, the eastern basin's commercial dominance, and the northern basin's geographic intermediacy produced a three-way tension that no simple majority rule or equal-weighting rule could resolve.

The constitutional arrangement that emerged from approximately three hundred years of negotiation — roughly the period between the First Connectivity's founding and the consolidation of the Southern Sovereignty — is the product of this tension and the successive compromises it required.

## 6.2 The First Connectivity's Constitutional Weakness

The First Connectivity's founding bargain, as Ch. 1 described, was legal rather than military: a central authority offered contract enforcement in exchange for jurisdictional recognition. This bargain produced a functional commercial system, but it produced a weak political system. The Connectivity's central authority could enforce contracts. It could not easily impose political decisions on member polities that disagreed with those decisions.

This constitutional weakness was not accidental. The relay node operators who accepted the Connectivity's founding bargain accepted it because it enhanced their commercial position without requiring them to subordinate their political autonomy. They wanted contract enforcement; they did not want a central authority that could override their local governance. The founding bargain delivered exactly what they wanted: commercial reliability without political subordination.

The consequence was that the First Connectivity's political institutions were weak by design. Decisions that required coordinated action across the three basins could be blocked by any basin's representatives; enforcement of politically contested decisions was difficult; the central administration could facilitate commercial exchange but could not mandate it.

This weakness was manageable during the relay era, when the Connectivity's primary function was commercial. It became increasingly difficult to manage as the Reach's administered trade standards put pressure on the Connectivity to respond with a coordinated commercial policy, and as the Reach's military projection capacity put pressure on the Connectivity to respond with coordinated security arrangements.

## 6.3 The Constitutional Crisis and the Sovereignty's Founding

The immediate trigger for the constitutional reconstitution that produced the Southern Sovereignty was, in most accounts, the Commercial Standards Crisis — the period approximately two centuries before the Catch-Up when the Reach's administered trade standards were gaining sufficient traction in the intermediate belt that Connectivity merchants trading outside the three basins faced significant disadvantages relative to Reach-aligned competitors.

The Commercial Standards Crisis produced a demand for coordinated Connectivity-wide commercial policy: standardization of the Connectivity's own commercial documentation, harmonization of the contract enforcement system across all three basins, and — most contentiously — a centralized authority capable of negotiating with the Reach over standards compatibility.

The demand for a more centralized authority reopened every unresolved constitutional question from the Connectivity's founding.

The eastern basin's commercial interests wanted a central commercial administration with real authority to set and enforce standards — an administration that would naturally be led by eastern basin commercial factors, given their dominance of the Connectivity's commercial infrastructure.

The western basin's agricultural interests wanted protections against central commercial policy that would disadvantage bulk calorie producers — and, specifically, wanted structural guarantees that the central authority could not set terms of trade that systematically favored the eastern basin's commercial model over the western basin's agricultural model.

The northern basin's relay node operators wanted to preserve their position as intermediaries between the eastern and western basins — and feared that a more centralized authority would eliminate the information asymmetry on which their commercial position depended.

The constitutional settlement that produced the Southern Sovereignty addressed all three concerns, but not equally.

## 6.4 The Settlement's Architecture

The Southern Sovereignty's constitutional structure, in its final form, distributed power according to a formula that none of the three basins considered ideal but all three could accept.

The central commercial administration received real authority: it could set Sovereignty-wide standards for commercial documentation, negotiate with external polities (including the Reach) over standards compatibility, and enforce those standards through Sovereignty-wide commercial courts. The eastern basin's commercial interests won this structural point.

The western basin received structural veto power over two categories of policy: terms of trade that would significantly disadvantage bulk calorie producers relative to luxury good producers, and resource allocation decisions that would prioritize industrial or commercial development at the expense of agricultural system stability. The western basin's political representatives did not need to win every debate; they needed to be able to block decisions that would damage their constituents' interests. The constitutional settlement gave them that capacity.

The northern basin's relay node operators received guaranteed representation in the central commercial administration, including permanent membership on the arbitration panels that heard inter-basin commercial disputes. This was less valuable than the complete autonomy they had previously enjoyed, but more valuable than the powerlessness they would have had without it.

The settlement's architecture is called, in the academic literature, the Three-Weight System: each basin has a weight in Sovereignty-wide governance that reflects neither its demographic size (which would give the western basin a majority) nor its commercial weight (which would give the eastern basin a majority) nor its territorial extent, but rather a constitutionally defined combination of all three that was negotiated over three generations and has been the subject of periodic revision attempts ever since.

## 6.5 The Sago Bloc as Constitutional Actor

The political formation called the Sago Bloc — described in Ch. 1 and Ch. 9 in terms of its policy positions — is, at a constitutional level, the organized exercise of the veto power that the western basin received in the Sovereignty's founding settlement.

The Sago Bloc's veto power is not unlimited. The western basin's representatives cannot block all policy decisions; they can block decisions that fall within the defined categories of agricultural and terms-of-trade policy. Within those categories, their veto is effective and has been used regularly throughout the Sovereignty's history.

What gives the Sago Bloc its outsized influence beyond those categories is a constitutional feature that the western basin's founding negotiators built into the settlement: the requirement for supermajorities for major commercial policy changes. Most Sovereignty-wide commercial policy requires not just a majority of the Three-Weight Assembly but a supermajority that effectively requires at least partial western basin consent.

This supermajority requirement has been the central mechanism through which the Sago Bloc has maintained its policy influence across several centuries of structural economic change. The commercial transitions that would most benefit the eastern basin — rapid standardization, free-trade expansion, industrial-era commercial reforms — have consistently required the Sago Bloc's at least partial cooperation, which the Bloc has consistently exchanged for policy concessions on agricultural protection and transfer payment guarantees.

Whether this constitutional architecture is functional or dysfunctional depends significantly on what question you are asking. If you are asking whether the Sovereignty makes commercially optimal decisions, the supermajority requirement is clearly a source of suboptimal outcomes — the decisions that maximize aggregate commercial efficiency are regularly blocked by a political coalition that has legitimate interests in not internalizing the costs of efficiency gains that would primarily benefit other basins. If you are asking whether the Sovereignty maintains sufficient political cohesion to survive major external shocks — the Reach's administered trade standardization, the Commercial Standards Crisis, the Catch-Up period, the pyra race, the Strange Peace — the supermajority requirement has arguably been a source of stability: it prevented any single basin from capturing the Sovereignty's central apparatus, and it created bargaining processes that, while slow and expensive, produced outcomes that all three basins could accept.

The Sovereignty's political survival across eight hundred years of external pressure is not conclusive evidence that the constitutional architecture was optimal. It is evidence that the architecture was not fatally defective. Whether a better architecture was available given the actual interests of the three basins in the founding period is a counterfactual question that the academic literature has not resolved.

## 6.6 The Constitution and the Catch-Up

The Catch-Up period's political dynamics — described in Ch. 9 — are only fully intelligible against the background of the constitutional architecture this chapter has described.

The Sago Bloc's cooperation during the pyra race emergency was not a departure from normal constitutional behavior. It was the Sago Bloc exercising its constitutional leverage at the moment when that leverage was most valuable: agreeing to the emergency allocation of industrial capacity to the pyra program in exchange for post-emergency resource transfer commitments that were constitutionally binding.

The commitments that the Sago Bloc extracted were specific and substantial: guaranteed minimum prices for bulk calorie exports for a defined period following the achievement of deterrence parity, infrastructure investment in western basin transportation and processing at specified levels, and a formal constitutional amendment that expanded the categories of policy subject to western basin veto to include post-emergency industrial allocation.

All of these commitments were honored in the immediate post-emergency period. The resource transfers occurred. The infrastructure investment occurred. The constitutional amendment was ratified.

The problem, which Ch. 9 describes as the "postponed transition," emerged from the longer-term consequences. The resource transfer commitments maintained the western basin's living standards but did not close the development gap that the emergency allocation had widened. The infrastructure investment improved western basin transportation and processing but did not trigger the industrial transition that the eastern basin's advocates had argued would follow from infrastructure improvement. The constitutional amendment made the western basin's veto more robust but did not change the underlying structural economics that made western basin industrial transition difficult.

The Sago Bloc had negotiated successfully for the specific concessions it could extract from the pyra race emergency. It had not, and could not, negotiate a solution to the structural conditions that made the western basin's situation difficult in the first place. Those conditions — the Prebisch terms-of-trade dynamics described in Ch. 5, the sago crop's resistance to quality differentiation, the western basin's geographic isolation — are not conditions that constitutional architecture can alter.

The Sovereignty's constitution is excellent at distributing political power among its component units. It is less good at changing the material conditions that determine the value of the political power each unit holds.
