# Chapter 7: The Intermediate Periphery — The Lattice's External Territories in the Colonial Era

*From* Introduction to Lattice History, *2nd ed., ed. Selvanayagam, Polytechnic Press of the Southern Sovereignty*

---

## 7.1 The Lattice's Colonial Self-Understanding

The Lattice's historiographical tradition has long maintained a distinction between the Reach's "empire of force" and the Lattice's "empire of law" — a distinction that the academic literature has accepted as descriptively accurate while being skeptical of its normative implications. The Lattice did not, in general, administer its external territories through garrisoned military occupation. It administered them through commercial and legal integration: the extension of Lattice contract jurisdiction, the incorporation of external polities' commercial infrastructure into Lattice-standard relay networks, and the provision of Lattice financial services to external commercial actors.

This distinction is real. The Lattice's approach to colonial administration was genuinely different from the Reach's, and the difference produced different institutional residues in the polities it administered. An intermediate belt polity under Lattice administration typically maintained its political institutions, its local governance structures, and its cultural life with more autonomy than a comparable polity under Reach administration. The Lattice administered through legal and commercial infrastructure, not through garrison.

This chapter argues that the distinction, while real, has been used to evade questions that it does not answer. "Empire of law" describes the mechanism of Lattice colonial administration. It does not describe its effects. Whether the mechanism of domination is military or legal does not determine whether domination occurred. This chapter attempts to describe both the mechanism and the effects.

## 7.2 The Mechanism of Legal Integration

The Lattice's extension of its commercial jurisdiction into external polities worked through a recognizable process that repeated, with variations, across multiple expansion episodes over the colonial era.

Phase one: commercial introduction. Lattice factor houses established presences in intermediate belt relay nodes, offering commercial services — credit, insurance, quality certification, dispute arbitration — at competitive rates. The factor houses were genuinely useful: they reduced transaction costs for local merchants and provided access to Lattice-standard commercial documentation that facilitated trade with Lattice-aligned polities.

Phase two: legal integration. Once Lattice commercial services were established, contracts between local merchants and Lattice factor houses were written under Lattice contract law. Disputes arising from those contracts were adjudicated in Lattice commercial courts. The gradual accumulation of Lattice-standard commercial relationships built a local legal infrastructure that was oriented toward Lattice procedures, Lattice precedents, and Lattice-trained arbitrators.

Phase three: standard dependency. As the proportion of local commercial relationships documented under Lattice standards increased, the cost of switching to an alternative legal system increased. Local merchants who had invested in understanding Lattice commercial procedures, who maintained commercial relationships with Lattice-standard counterparts, and who had credit histories documented in Lattice commercial records had substantial incentives to continue operating under Lattice standards even if an alternative became available.

Phase four: formal integration. At the point where Lattice-standard commercial infrastructure was dominant in a polity's commercial life, the formal political integration of that polity into the Sovereignty's legal jurisdiction was, from the commercial perspective, largely a formalization of an existing reality. The sovereignty transfer involved an extension of the Sovereignty's administrative authority over the polity, the payment of integration fees (described in Lattice administrative documentation as "circuit access fees"), and the formal enrollment of the polity's commercial infrastructure in the Sovereignty's relay network.

This process was slower than military conquest — it typically took between one and three generations — and it required genuine investment in commercial services rather than military force. It was also, in the Lattice's assessment, more durable: polities integrated through commercial dependency tended to maintain their commercial orientation toward the Lattice more reliably than polities that were administered through military occupation, because commercial dependency creates ongoing material interests in continued integration rather than ongoing resentment of continued occupation.

## 7.3 What Integration Meant in Practice

The Lattice's model of legal integration was not without costs for the integrated polities. This section describes the costs honestly, because the academic literature has, in the Lattice's historiographical tradition, sometimes soft-pedaled them.

**Commercial infrastructure displacement.** The most immediate cost of Lattice integration was the displacement of pre-existing commercial infrastructure. Polities that had developed their own relay networks, their own credit systems, their own dispute resolution mechanisms, found those systems under competitive pressure from Lattice-standard equivalents. The Lattice's factor houses were typically better capitalized and connected to larger commercial networks than local equivalents; local commercial institutions that competed directly with Lattice-standard equivalents often found themselves unable to offer competitive terms.

This displacement was not always permanent. Some polities maintained parallel commercial systems — local networks for internal trade, Lattice-standard documentation for external trade — that preserved some of their pre-integration commercial infrastructure. But the trend over multiple generations was toward increasing reliance on Lattice-standard systems, and the local commercial expertise that had supported the pre-integration systems atrophied as Lattice-standard equivalents became dominant.

**Terms-of-trade deterioration.** The Prebisch-Singer hypothesis — that the terms of trade between bulk commodity exporters and manufactured good exporters tend to deteriorate over time for the commodity exporters — operated within the Lattice's colonial system as well as outside it. Intermediate belt polities that were integrated into Lattice relay circuits primarily as bulk commodity suppliers found their terms of trade with Lattice commercial centers tending to worsen over time, for the structural reasons described in Ch. 5.

The Lattice's administered trade system included provisions for "circuit equity adjustments" — mechanisms for recalibrating the terms of trade when they deteriorated beyond defined thresholds. These mechanisms existed. They were also controlled by the Lattice's commercial administration, which had institutional incentives to define the thresholds conservatively and to apply the adjustments slowly. The academic literature's assessment is that the equity adjustment mechanisms were real but insufficient: they slowed terms-of-trade deterioration without preventing it.

**Legal system orientation.** The extension of Lattice contract jurisdiction into external polities produced a legal system that was, by design and habit, oriented toward Lattice commercial culture. Disputes involving Lattice commercial actors were adjudicated in Lattice commercial courts, under Lattice legal standards, by arbitrators trained in Lattice legal traditions. External polity actors who had disputes with Lattice commercial actors were arguing cases in a system built by, and optimized for, the other party.

The Lattice's academic literature acknowledges this asymmetry consistently. The standard defense is that the legal system, despite its orientation, provided more reliable commercial enforcement than the pre-integration systems had provided — that an imperfect but functional legal system was better than a fairer but less reliable alternative. This defense is substantively accurate. It does not resolve the asymmetry; it provides a reason why the asymmetry was accepted.

## 7.4 The Grievance Pattern

The simulation's grievance mechanic — which builds from excess extraction and amplifies sovereignty recovery in subject territories — models a pattern that Lattice colonial administration documented internally for several centuries before the academic literature formalized it.

The pattern: integration proceeded smoothly until the commercial dependency phase, when the costs of the Lattice's commercial infrastructure became visible alongside the benefits. Polities that had initially welcomed Lattice commercial services because they reduced transaction costs and expanded market access began, after a generation or two, to experience those same commercial services as constraints: constraints on developing competing infrastructure, constraints on renegotiating terms, constraints on commercial relationships with non-Lattice polities that might offer better terms for some categories of goods.

The resulting political movements varied in their intensity and form, but they shared a common structure: they appealed to the pre-integration period as a lost autonomy, identified the Lattice's commercial dependency as the mechanism of continued subordination, and demanded either renegotiation of the terms of integration or withdrawal from the Sovereignty's commercial jurisdiction.

The Lattice's administrative response to these movements was, characteristically, legal rather than military: renegotiation processes, equity adjustment mechanisms, appeals to the commercial benefits of continued integration, and, when these failed to resolve the dispute, commercial pressure — the threat of withdrawal of Lattice commercial services and the exclusion of the resistant polity from Lattice-standard relay circuits.

Commercial pressure was effective in most cases. The dependency that integration had created was real, and the costs of losing access to Lattice commercial infrastructure were, for most integrated polities, higher than the costs of accepting the Lattice's renegotiation terms. But the effectiveness of commercial pressure in resolving political disputes did not reduce the grievances that produced the disputes. It resolved the disputes by demonstrating that the costs of exit were higher than the costs of staying, not by addressing the conditions that made staying feel costly.

## 7.5 The Welfare Question

The central evaluative question about the Lattice's colonial system — whether it made the integrated polities better off or worse off than they would have been without integration — is not resolvable with the information available. The counterfactual is not observable.

What can be said:

The Lattice's commercial integration provided real benefits to integrated polities: reduced transaction costs, expanded market access, capital availability, legal infrastructure for large-scale commercial relationships. These benefits were unequally distributed within integrated polities, tending to benefit the commercial actors most actively engaged with Lattice commercial networks and less directly benefiting subsistence producers and non-commercial populations.

The Lattice's commercial integration imposed real costs on integrated polities: displacement of pre-existing commercial infrastructure, terms-of-trade deterioration for bulk commodity producers, legal system orientation toward Lattice commercial culture. These costs were also unequally distributed, tending to fall most heavily on polities with bulk commodity economies and least heavily on polities whose commercial profiles were similar to the eastern basin's.

The net welfare effect — whether the benefits exceeded the costs in aggregate, and for whom — varied significantly across integrated polities and across periods of integration. The academic literature's current consensus is that integration was, on average, net beneficial for integrated polities in the medium term and net costly in the long term: the commercial infrastructure benefits materialized relatively quickly, while the terms-of-trade and dependency costs accumulated over generations.

This is not a flattering assessment of the Lattice's colonial record. It is the assessment the evidence supports.

## 7.6 The Intermediate Belt in Historical Perspective

The intermediate belt polities — those that were neither fully Reach-administered nor fully Lattice-integrated, that maintained their political independence through the colonial era by navigating between the two commercial systems — are, in some respects, the most instructive case.

The intermediate belt polities paid the dual compliance costs described in Ch. 4: maintaining documentation and relationships with both commercial systems, unable to specialize fully in either, denied the full network benefits of complete integration with either. Their commercial development was slower and more expensive than those of more decisively integrated polities.

They also avoided the specific long-run costs of integration into either system: the terms-of-trade deterioration of Lattice commercial dependency, the more direct extraction of Reach tributary administration. Their political autonomy was preserved through the Cold War logic of the colonial era — each hegemon unwilling to absorb them fully because absorption would provoke the other.

The Strange Peace's establishment formalized this situation. The intermediate belt's neutrality, which during the colonial era had been a strategic condition maintained by balance-of-power logic, became an institutionalized status recognized by both anchor civilizations. The costs of maintaining that status — the dual compliance costs, the contested alignment competition described in Ch. 10 — became permanent features rather than temporary conditions.

Whether being the intermediate belt was, on balance, better or worse than being absorbed by either hegemon is the question that intermediate belt historians debate, and that intermediate belt citizens experience in their daily commercial and political lives.

The short answer: it depended on when and which polity. Some avoided both hegemons' costs. Others absorbed both hegemons' costs without receiving either's full benefits. The variance in outcomes within the intermediate belt is higher than the variance within either hegemon's integrated territory.

High variance, in the long run, is the characteristic condition of polities that navigate between great powers. Sometimes it produces exceptional outcomes. More often it produces median outcomes with occasional catastrophic ones.
