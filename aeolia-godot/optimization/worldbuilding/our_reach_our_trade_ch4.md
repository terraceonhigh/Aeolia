# Chapter 4: When Everything Sped Up (The Time of Naphtha and Fast Ships)

*From* Our Reach, Our Trade: Social Studies for Young Learners, *Reach Education Bureau, 4th printing*

---

## The Problem With Sailing

In Chapter 3, you learned how goods traveled across the Reach, one island at a time. A bundle of dried bakala might take two or three years to travel from a cold northern island to a warm southern market! And most of that time, the bakala was just sitting on a dock somewhere, waiting for the next ship going the right direction.

Why did it take so long?

Because sailing ships have a problem: **they need wind**.

Wind is free. Wind is everywhere. But wind doesn't always blow the direction you want to go. Sometimes you have to wait weeks for the right wind. Sometimes you have to sail a longer path to catch the right breeze. The sailors of the old relay trade knew the winds of the Reach better than they knew their own cousins — which seasons brought winds from the north, which months the great circling storms made the southern passages dangerous, which narrow straits could be crossed only in the early morning before the weather turned.

All of that waiting and zig-zagging made trade slow. Slow trade meant high prices, because every merchant along the way added a little bit to the cost for their trouble.

For hundreds of years, no one could make trade much faster than the wind would allow.

And then someone found a way.

## The Black Liquid Under the Islands

Far beneath some of the volcanic islands of the Reach, there is a dark, thick liquid that seeps up through cracks in the rock.

For a long time, the people who lived near these seeps used the dark liquid in small ways. They rubbed it on boat hulls to keep out water. They mixed it with sand to fill cracks in walls. They burned it in small lamps, though it smelled terrible. This dark liquid had different names on different islands, but over time, the traders who carried it called it **naphtha**.

Naphtha was a useful thing, but not an important thing. Not yet.

Then, slowly, clever people began to realize something: naphtha had an enormous amount of energy hiding inside it. Much more energy than wood, or dried dung, or any of the fuels people had been burning before. If you could build a machine that captured that energy — that turned the burning of naphtha into movement — you could do something remarkable.

You could move a ship without wind.

## Ships That Made Their Own Wind

The first naphtha-powered ships were not beautiful. They were large, loud, and they produced enormous clouds of dark smoke. Sailors who grew up with the quiet of sail-powered ships found them frightening and unpleasant.

But they were **fast**.

A naphtha ship could sail directly against the wind. It could maintain the same speed in a calm as in a gale. It could cross the big open-water passages in a fraction of the time that sail required. A journey that had taken three months by the old relay route might take three weeks.

When ships got faster, everything got faster.

More goods could be moved in a year. More islands could be reached. Merchants who had spent their whole lives learning the wind patterns found that their most important skill no longer mattered very much. The merchants who knew where to find naphtha — and who owned the machines that burned it — became the powerful ones.

## Who Had the Naphtha?

Here is where things got complicated.

Naphtha was not evenly spread across the Reach. It was found under certain volcanic islands, in certain geological formations, and nowhere else. The polities who happened to be near naphtha deposits became very powerful very quickly. The polities far from naphtha deposits had to buy it, or trade for it, or find clever ways to get along without it.

This created a kind of scramble. Merchants and fleets from the big polities began to search every island, every sea floor, every volcanic seep they could find. Any island with naphtha became suddenly very important. The polities who controlled the most naphtha controlled the fastest ships. The polities with the fastest ships could move their soldiers and goods farther and faster than anyone else.

Do you remember the relay traders from Chapter 3? They had spent generations building their trading relationships, one island at a time. The naphtha ships arrived so quickly that the relay chains that had worked for centuries were disrupted almost overnight.

An island that had been an important stopping point on the relay route — because it had a good harbor, and the winds were favorable, and the merchants there knew everyone — might suddenly find itself bypassed entirely. The big naphtha ships didn't need to stop at every island. They could just go directly where they wanted.

Some relay trading communities found new jobs in the new world. Others disappeared.

## The New Rules

The naphtha age changed the rules of the Reach in three big ways.

**Rule one: Big polities got much bigger.** With fast ships, a powerful polity could send soldiers to faraway islands and back before anyone else could respond. Polities that had controlled a few dozen islands found they could now control hundreds. Some polities grew so large that the islands at one end of their territory barely knew the islands at the other end.

**Rule two: The same things became important everywhere.** Before naphtha, different islands were important for different reasons — this island had marich, that island had bakala, another island had good timber. After naphtha, the most important thing on almost any island became either *naphtha itself*, or *things you could trade for naphtha*. The wide variety of the old relay trade began to narrow.

**Rule three: Everyone needed something from far away.** A naphtha ship needs fuel. A machine that burns naphtha needs metal parts. Metal parts need chrysos and aes from specific mines. Even the most powerful polity couldn't produce everything it needed inside its own islands. Which meant that no matter how big and powerful you became, you still needed to trade with other polities — even ones you didn't like.

This last rule would become very important later. Remember it.

## The Thing No One Expected

The naphtha age lasted several hundred years. It brought fast ships and loud machines and a world where even the most distant parts of the Reach were connected faster than anyone had imagined.

But it also brought something else.

The geological surveys that polities conducted to find naphtha sometimes found another mineral hidden in the deep volcanic rock. A dense, slightly warm, faintly glowing mineral that the geologists logged as interesting but not immediately useful.

They called it **pyra**.

For a long time, pyra was noted and set aside. The survey teams were looking for naphtha. Pyra was a curiosity.

Then the scientists figured out what was hidden inside pyra — and as you will learn in Chapter 5, that changed everything even more than naphtha had.

- Why did polities still need to trade with each other, even after they got very big and powerful?
