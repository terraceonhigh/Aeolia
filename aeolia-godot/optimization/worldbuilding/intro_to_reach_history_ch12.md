# Chapter 12: What Comes After the Strange Peace?

*From* Introduction to Reach History, *3rd ed., eds. Halarua & Temani, University of the Northern Reach Press*

---

## 12.1 The Question Historians Cannot Answer

This chapter will attempt to address a question that historians are professionally unsuited to answer.

History is the study of the past. It proceeds by archival method, documentary evidence, and interpretive argument about what happened and why. The question of what comes after the Strange Peace is not a question about the past. It is a question about the future — and the future is, notoriously, beyond the historian's jurisdiction.

Why, then, is this chapter in a history textbook?

Because the Strange Peace is not only a historical phenomenon. It is a present reality, and its future is a problem that people alive now must think about. The historian's comparative toolkit — the knowledge of how similar systems have behaved, how deterrence structures have formed and dissolved, how the Reach's political landscape has absorbed previous discontinuities — is relevant evidence for thinking about what comes next. The historian cannot predict. She can identify which analogies are most apt, which failure modes are best documented, and which assumptions about perpetual stability should be treated with skepticism.

This chapter is therefore offered as historical analysis rather than prediction. It proceeds by examining five scenarios that the available historical evidence suggests are non-negligible, and analyzing the conditions under which each would occur.

## 12.2 Scenario One: The Peace Continues

The simplest scenario is that nothing changes in the fundamental structure of the Strange Peace. The two anchor civilizations continue to maintain second-strike capability. The mutual surveillance infrastructure continues to function. The intelligence analysts on both sides continue to correctly distinguish routine activity from attack preparation. The near-miss incidents that occur — and they will occur — continue to be managed without crossing the threshold into exchange.

This is not an implausible scenario. The Strange Peace has now persisted for approximately two centuries, and the institutional knowledge that maintains it has been transmitted across several professional generations. The command cultures of both anchor civilizations have been shaped, for two hundred years, by the assumption that nuclear use against a peer is not a rational option. This assumption has become embedded in professional training, military doctrine, and the informal social norms of the relevant officer and analyst classes.

The scenario's weakness, as identified by theorists of deterrence stability, is that it assumes no change in the conditions that produced the equilibrium. In fact, the conditions are changing continuously. Pyra weapon technology does not stand still. The delivery systems of the 1st year of the Strange Peace are not the delivery systems of the 200th year. Surveillance technology continues to advance. Artificial calculation systems capable of analyzing threat indicators faster than human deliberation can engage them are increasingly deployed in both anchor civilizations' early-warning networks. Whether these changes strengthen or weaken deterrence stability is genuinely uncertain — and it is the kind of uncertainty that, historically, has preceded instability events.

## 12.3 Scenario Two: Managed Decline to Non-Nuclear Multipolarity

A second scenario, argued most extensively by the Kosei school of Reach political theory, holds that the Strange Peace will dissolve not through catastrophe but through slow erosion of the conditions that produced it.

The core logic is as follows. The two-hegemon structure of the Strange Peace rests on the economic and technological dominance of the two anchor civilizations. That dominance is not permanent. Both anchor civilizations are, in the long run, confronted with the structural features that have challenged every previous hegemonic system: the costs of maintaining large client-state networks, the diffusion of technology to polities that were previously non-capable, the internal political pressures generated by long periods of defensive investment. As the material basis for hegemony erodes, the dominance required to prevent third parties from acquiring independent nuclear capability becomes harder to maintain.

In this scenario, the Strange Peace ends not with an exchange but with the slow emergence of a multipolar nuclear system — three or four nuclear-capable great powers, each too costly to first-strike and insufficiently surveilled to be fully deterred by the old bilateral framework. The literature on multipolar deterrence is less sanguine than the literature on bilateral deterrence. Multiple-party equilibria are less stable than two-party equilibria for structural reasons: with three or more nuclear powers, the game-theoretic calculations that make first strikes irrational in the bilateral case become vastly more complex, and the opportunities for misperception multiply accordingly.

The historical precedent for managed transitions of this kind is limited. The pearl-string empire system dissolved without destroying the civilizations it had organized — but it also did not dissolve while those civilizations held weapons capable of ending each other.

## 12.4 Scenario Three: Technical Breakthrough

The standard deterrence model rests on the assumption that second-strike capability cannot be eliminated by a first strike. This assumption is not a physical law. It is a statement about the current technology available to both parties.

A sufficiently advanced detection-and-interdiction system — capable of locating dispersed delivery aircraft across the global ocean, destroying them before they can launch, and simultaneously neutralizing known base harbors — would break the second-strike assumption. The challenge is formidable: an ocean world with no runways produces millions of potential landing sites, making comprehensive first-strike targeting seem nearly impossible. "Nearly" is the operative word. A civilization that achieved this capability before the other side would be in the position of the Vaenish Consolidated Directorate in Year 1: in possession of weapons the other side could not retaliate against.

Whether such a capability can ever be achieved is a question for engineers rather than historians. The historian can note that the expectation of technical breakthrough has been a persistent feature of both anchor civilizations' strategic planning since the first detonation, and that the expected breakthrough has consistently been revised forward. Every generation has had its putative near-term game-changer; none has yet materialized. This track record argues for skepticism about any specific technical breakthrough claim, while not ruling out that such a breakthrough could eventually occur.

What the historian can also note is that the *anticipation* of technical breakthrough is itself destabilizing. A civilization that believes its rival is three to five years from a first-strike-enabling capability has incentives to act before that capability is achieved — which is the worst possible moment to act, since the resulting conflict would be fought with full second-strike capability on both sides.

## 12.5 Scenario Four: Command Failure

Of the three instabilities identified by deterrence theorists — technical, perceptual, and command — the command instability is the least susceptible to institutional mitigation.

Technical instability is addressed by building second-strike capability and maintaining it. Perceptual instability is addressed by surveillance, back-channel communication, and the early-warning data-sharing protocols that grew out of the Sable Straits Alert. Command instability — the failure of the decision process itself, through distributed authority, incomplete information, or time pressure that forecloses rational deliberation — is harder to architect away. The very features that make command systems resilient against decapitation first strikes (distributed authority, autonomous action under communication failure) also make them vulnerable to unauthorized use.

The documentation from transition period near-misses suggests that command instability is the historically most proximate cause in cases where exchange nearly occurred. The Sable Straits Alert came close because the command structure placed decision authority in a single individual in the early morning, operating under severe time pressure, on information that was subsequently proved incorrect. The institutional reforms that followed reduced the probability of a recurrence — but they also increased the complexity of the command system, and complex systems have failure modes that simple systems do not.

The command failure scenario — in which an exchange occurs not because either polity rationally chose to exchange, but because the decision system produced an authorization that neither polity's leadership intended — is perhaps the most consistently underrated risk in the popular literature on the Strange Peace.

## 12.6 Scenario Five: Exchange and Aftermath

The scenario that the literature least often examines at length, and that the available historical evidence argues is the most catastrophic, is an actual exchange.

This chapter will not offer a detailed projection of what a nuclear exchange between the anchor civilizations would produce. The range of estimates in the academic literature is wide, and the uncertainty is genuine. What the historical record provides is this: no previous technology of mass destruction has produced the long-term environmental effects associated with pyra weapons use at scale. The economic models, population projections, and climate assessments that academic institutions have produced under various assumptions all agree on the direction of the effect. They disagree substantially on the magnitude.

The political question that follows an exchange — if civilization on the sufficient scale required for political life survives — is the question that Reach political theory has barely begun to address. No framework currently exists for a post-deterrence reconstruction. The Strange Peace has lasted long enough that the generations alive today have no experience with any other organizing principle for inter-polity relations.

This is, perhaps, the most significant observation available to the historian: not that the Strange Peace is permanent, but that it has persisted long enough to become the *only* peace that living memory knows.

## 12.7 The Historian's Honest Conclusion

The five scenarios outlined above are not predictions. They are the structural possibilities that historical analysis can identify. They are not equally probable — but ranking their probability would require the kind of fine-grained knowledge of current strategic capabilities and decision-maker intentions that the historian cannot access from public sources.

What historical analysis can contribute is a set of questions worth asking. Is the institutional knowledge of the transition period being transmitted? Are the back-channel relationships between anchor civilizations being maintained by the current generation of analysts and diplomats? Is the surveillance infrastructure keeping pace with delivery system development? Are command structures designed to prevent unauthorized use, and do those designs introduce new failure modes?

These questions have answers. The answers are not available in university archives. They are available in the classified records of both anchor civilizations' intelligence services, and they are known, in varying degrees, to the small number of individuals whose professional responsibility includes knowing them.

The Strange Peace, as noted in Chapter 11, is not a natural state. It is an artifact. The question of what comes after it is, in part, the question of how diligently the artifact is maintained — and whether the people doing the maintenance understand what they are maintaining, and why.

This textbook cannot answer that question. It can only insist that the question be asked.
