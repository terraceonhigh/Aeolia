# Chapter 6: The Things We Still Trade (and Why We Still Need Each Other)

*From* Our Reach, Our Trade: Social Studies for Young Learners, *Reach Education Bureau, 4th printing*

---

## What Happens to Trade When Neighbors Are Afraid of Each Other?

You learned in Chapter 5 that the Strange Peace began when the two big families both had a special kind of fire that could destroy everything. And you learned that neither family wanted to use it.

But here is something interesting: the two families still needed things from each other. They still needed food from certain islands. They still needed the plants that grew only in certain climates. They still needed the clever tools that the other family's engineers had learned to make.

When you need something and you are also afraid of someone, what do you do?

Usually, you find a way to keep trading — but very, very carefully.

## The Three Kinds of Things That Keep Moving

Even during the Strange Peace, three kinds of things have never really stopped moving between the two big families.

**The first kind is food.** No matter how scared two families are of each other, people still need to eat. Some islands grow crops that no other climate can produce. Marich, the pungent dried spice that flavors so many meals, only grows on the warm equatorial archipelagos. Sthaq, the great silver fish, only runs in the cold northern waters. No amount of pyra changes where crops grow and where fish swim.

**The second kind is materials.** The engineers on both sides need metals and fibers that come from specific places in the world. Chrysos. Kapas. Seric. A polity that runs out of aes — the hard, useful alloy used in so many machines — will find its technology beginning to rust and fail, no matter how powerful it is.

**The third kind is knowledge.** This one surprises many people. You might think that two families who watch each other so carefully would hide all their knowledge. And it is true that they hide *some* of it — the knowledge about pyra devices and delivery systems and military things. But other knowledge travels freely, because it travels in the shape of goods: a cloth dyed a new way, a tool built to an unusual tolerance, a food preserved by a method no one has seen before. Knowledge does not always look like knowledge.

## The Go-Betweens

The trading in the Strange Peace does not usually happen directly between the two big families. It happens through the **smaller polities in the middle**.

Think about what this means for a small island that happens to sit between the two big families. In the old days, such a small island had to be careful about who it sold to, because being too friendly with one side might make the other side angry. This is still true!

But now the small island also has something the big families desperately need: a **safe place to trade**.

When merchants from one family want to sell grain to the other family, they often use the smaller islands as middlemen. The grain goes from the first family to the island, and then from the island to the second family. The smaller island takes a small fee. The big families pretend, a little bit, that they are not trading with each other directly.

This is not as silly as it sounds. Sometimes it matters very much to be able to say: *we are not trading with them. We are trading with our friends on the island, who happen to then trade with our rivals.* There is a difference — a political difference — even if the marich ends up in the same soup either way.

The clever merchants of the middleman islands became very good at this. The best ones knew exactly which things one big family would need and which things the other would pay well for. They kept two sets of relationships, two sets of prices, and — in the best qahwa houses — two entrances, so that a merchant from the Northern side and a merchant from the Southern side did not have to walk past each other when they arrived.

## Ships With Unusual Papers

A merchant traveling in the Strange Peace carries more paperwork than a merchant in the old relay days.

In the old days, a merchant might carry a letter of introduction from her trading house, a list of her cargo, and perhaps a letter of credit she could use to borrow money if her ship was delayed. That was usually enough.

Today, a merchant who wants to trade in the border zones between the two big families carries something different: **a letter of transit**. This is a paper — often stamped by three or four different governments — that says: *this merchant is allowed to be here, trading these goods, and is not a spy.*

Of course, some merchants are spies. The letters of transit do not change that. But having the letter means that the officials on both sides can pretend to believe you are not a spy, which makes everything go more smoothly.

The qahwa houses in the neutral ports of the Strange Peace are not just places to drink and negotiate. They are also the places where letters of transit are arranged, where fees are paid to the right officials, and where the careful work of being officially recognized gets done. The harbor-masters who run the busiest neutral ports have become some of the most powerful administrators in the Reach — not because they are strong, but because *everyone needs them*.

## The Things That Cannot Be Replaced

Here is the deepest reason why trade continues even in the Strange Peace: some things simply cannot be found anywhere else.

You might think that a polity with enough technology could make anything it needed. And this is partly true. With enough engineering, a polity *could* grow marich in glass houses heated by steam pipes. It *could* breed fish in inland tanks. It *could* spin artificial fibers in factories.

But it would cost so much — so much energy, so much time, so much attention from engineers who are needed for other things — that it is cheaper to buy. The economists call this **comparative advantage**. It means: do the things you are best at, and trade for the rest.

Even the two big families, with all their power and all their pyra, have discovered that some things grow better on the other side of the world, and some crafts are perfected by the other family's workers, and some knowledge has simply not been discovered yet on their side of the ocean.

So the ships keep sailing. Not freely — there are more checks, more fees, more careful paperwork than in the old relay days. Some things are forbidden to trade. The families are careful about who gets access to certain metals, certain chemicals, certain machines. The watch-keepers on both sides inspect cargoes, look for hidden materials, count ships in harbors.

But the merchant houses of the Strange Peace have learned to sail between the watching eyes as cleverly as the old relay traders once navigated the dangerous straits. The form has changed. The purpose has not.
