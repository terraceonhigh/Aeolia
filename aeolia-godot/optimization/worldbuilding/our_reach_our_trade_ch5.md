# Chapter 5: The Strange Peace

*From* Our Reach, Our Trade: Social Studies for Young Learners, *Reach Education Bureau, 4th printing*

---

## When the Exploring Stopped

For thousands and thousands of years, the peoples of the Reach kept exploring. Every generation, brave sailors set out to find new islands, make new friends, and bring back goods that people at home had never tasted or touched.

But something very strange happened about two hundred years ago. The exploring almost stopped.

Not because people forgot how to sail. Not because all the islands had been found (though most of them had been). Something else happened — something that is a little hard to explain, and very hard to understand.

## The Two Biggest Families

By the time your great-great-grandparents were alive, all the islands of the Reach belonged to one polity or another. The Reach had lots of different polities — maybe you can name some of them! — but two of them had grown much, much bigger than all the others.

Imagine two very big families who live on opposite sides of the same street. Both families have very big houses. Both families have lots of cousins and friends on all the nearby islands. And — here is the important part — both families have learned a very, very powerful kind of fire.

This special fire was so powerful that if one family used it against the other, both families would be destroyed. And they both knew this.

So what happened? Did they fight? Did they become friends?

They did **neither**. They watched each other, very carefully, for a very long time.

## Pyra: The Rock That Changed Everything

The special fire came from a heavy, dull-colored rock called **pyra**.

For hundreds of years, pyra was interesting to miners but not very important to anyone else. It was dense. It was slightly warm to the touch, which was strange. It glowed faintly in the dark, which was also strange. But you couldn't eat it, or make tools from it, or trade it for anything useful.

Then the scientists figured out how to unlock the energy hiding inside pyra. And that changed everything.

When pyra releases its hidden energy, it releases an *enormous* amount all at once. More energy than a whole forest fire. More energy than a volcano. One single device, built by clever engineers, could destroy an entire archipelago — every building, every ship, every fish in the sea, every qahwa tree on every hillside.

When the two big families each made their own pyra devices, they both had to think very carefully. If I use mine, the other family will use theirs. And then we will both lose everything. Forever.

This thought — *if I do it, they will do it, and then we both lose* — is what created the Strange Peace.

## The Watchers and the Watchers

The Strange Peace lasted, and still lasts, because both big families spend an enormous amount of time and energy **watching each other**.

Where are their ships? Where are their pyra devices? What new tools are their engineers building? Are they moving more soldiers to this island? Why did their envoy cancel the meeting? 

Each family has thousands of people whose only job is to find the answers to questions like these. And the other family has thousands of people watching the watchers.

This sounds exhausting, doesn't it? That is because it is.

In your great-great-grandparents' time, sailors still dreamed of finding something new over the horizon. Today, sailors are much more likely to be carrying messages between diplomats, or checking that an agreement is being followed, or just sailing slowly past an island to count how many ships are in the harbor. The spirit of exploring is still alive — but its job description has changed.

## The Smaller Families Had to Choose

When the two big families stopped expanding, the smaller polities in the Reach had a new problem. In the old days, a small polity might survive by being very good at one thing — making beautiful nili-dyed cloth, or knowing every current in a tricky strait, or growing the best marich in the region. The relay trade let them prosper without being big.

But now, the two big families were competing in a different way. Instead of sending ships to find new islands, they were sending gifts, and advisors, and merchants with very favorable trade terms, to all the smaller polities. Each big family wanted the smaller polities to be on their side.

For a small polity, this was a strange new world. Suddenly two enormous neighbors were both being very helpful and very friendly. Each was offering to build you a harbor, or teach your engineers new techniques, or send you grain if your harvest failed.

The catch, of course, was that whichever big family you accepted help from expected you to be helpful in return — and not helpful to the other big family.

The older traders called this **playing both sides**, and the careful ones got quite good at it. The careless ones sometimes found themselves owing favors to two enormous, suspicious neighbors at the same time, which was not a comfortable place to be.

## Peace Is Not the Same as Quiet

People sometimes mix up *peace* and *quiet*. Peace means that the two big families are not using their pyra devices against each other. It does not mean that nothing dangerous is happening.

In the Strange Peace, there are:

- Ships that disappear without explanation
- Agreements that almost work and then fall apart
- Islands where two different polities both claim to be in charge, and neither one backs down
- Scientists who work very long hours on things they are not allowed to talk about
- Diplomats who have the most important conversations in the world without ever writing anything down

Is the Strange Peace good? Yes — much better than the alternative! Is it a complete solution to the problem of living near someone with an enormous, dangerous amount of power? 

That question is still being argued about in the qahwa houses. It probably always will be.

---

*Something to think about:* The relay merchants of the old days needed to trust the merchant on the next island — enough to hand over goods, shake hands, and sail away. What does trust look like between the two big families today? Is it the same kind of trust? If not, what would you call it?
