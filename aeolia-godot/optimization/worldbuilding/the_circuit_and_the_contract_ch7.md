# Chapter 7: What the Sovereignty Produces — The Circuit Economy in the Strange Peace Era

*From* The Circuit and the Contract: A Young Person's History of the Southern Sovereignty, *4th ed., Polytechnic Press of the Southern Sovereignty, Educational Division*

*Approved for use in Sovereignty schools, Years 6–8*

## Two Kinds of Production

The Sovereignty produces two broad categories of goods.

The first category is what economists call **primary goods**: raw materials and agricultural products that are valuable because of what they are, not because of what has been done to them. Emmer grain, sago, papa root, raw mineral ores, raw naphtha, raw pyra ore, and unprocessed fish all fall in this category. Primary goods are the foundation of any economy: without food and raw materials, there is nothing else.

The second category is **processed goods**: things that have been transformed through skill, machinery, or knowledge from raw materials into something more valuable. Refined nori oil, manufactured char concentrate, processed emmer flour, refined naphtha fuel, precision-manufactured components, commercial banking services, and quality certification services all fall in this category. Processed goods generally sell for more per unit than the raw materials that went into them.

The Sovereignty produces more primary goods and fewer processed goods than the Reach does. This is not a coincidence. It is the result of several centuries of commercial history, and understanding it is essential for understanding the Sovereignty's current economic position.

## Why the Sovereignty Produces What It Produces

The relay network that became the Sovereignty developed around the commercial needs of the polities connected to it. In the relay era, those polities produced and traded what their geography and climate allowed: sago and papa from the western basin's productive wetlands, emmer and char from the northern basin's temperate zones, nori and preserved fish from the eastern basin's productive coastlines.

These goods traveled along the relay network to markets where they were processed: nori pressed into oil at eastern basin refineries, char processed into commercial concentrates in northern basin manufactories, emmer milled into flour at the relay nodes with the necessary equipment. Over time, the processing capacity for the Sovereignty's primary goods concentrated in those nodes where processing was already established — which meant, increasingly, in nodes that had early access to the capital and equipment that processing required.

Access to capital came from the relay network's commercial finance system. The earliest commercial finance providers in the Sovereignty's network were eastern basin relay node operators, who had accumulated reserves from centuries of information brokerage and quality certification. When a western basin sago producer wanted to build a processing facility, they needed to borrow from eastern basin commercial houses that charged eastern-basin interest rates and wrote contracts in eastern-basin legal frameworks.

The interest rates were not malicious. They reflected real risk assessment: a new processing facility in a region without established processing traditions was genuinely more risky than an expansion of existing facilities in proven processing centers. But the cumulative effect of eastern-basin-controlled capital was that most processing capacity developed in the eastern basin, and western basin producers continued primarily as primary good producers.

This pattern — primary good production in the periphery, processing in the commercial center — is what economists call a **structural terms-of-trade problem**. The price of primary goods relative to the price of processed goods tends, over long periods, to fall. Raw emmer loses value relative to milled emmer flour as milling capacity expands and competition among millers drives down the processing margin. Raw nori loses value relative to pressed nori oil. The Sovereignty's primary good producers find, across generations, that their goods buy fewer of the processed goods they need.

The economists Prebisch and Singer, working in a different world, documented this pattern in detail. The Sovereignty's history is a case study of what they described.

## What the Strange Peace Changed — and What It Did Not

Before the Strange Peace, the Sovereignty's terms-of-trade problem was embedded in the competitive commercial relationship between the Sovereignty and the Reach. The Reach's administered trade system set the prices for the goods that crossed the boundary — and the Reach's system set those prices in ways that reflected Reach commercial interests. Reach-processed goods were priced to capture the processing margin; Sovereignty primary goods were priced as inputs to Reach processing rather than as final goods with their own value.

The Strange Peace removed the threat of conquest but did not change the structure of commercial exchange. The RSC's Bridge Documentation, described in Chapter 5, gives Sovereignty merchants access to Reach-proximate markets. It does not change what is being exchanged: Sovereignty primary goods for Reach processed goods, at terms that still reflect the Reach's greater processing capacity and commercial sophistication.

This is the central economic challenge of the Strange Peace era: the Sovereignty has security it did not previously have, but it has not yet converted that security into the commercial development that security was supposed to enable.

The challenge is not simple to solve. Building processing capacity requires capital. Capital accumulation requires surplus. Surplus requires that the terms of trade for primary goods be favorable enough that primary good producers retain enough income to invest. But the terms of trade for primary goods are not favorable, precisely because primary good production is what the Sovereignty currently specializes in.

The economic reformers who have argued, in each generation of the Strange Peace, that the Sovereignty must develop its processing capacity faster describe this as a vicious cycle. The governance structure described in Chapter 6 — with its regional balance requirements and its multiple veto points — makes rapid reallocation toward manufacturing and processing investment difficult. The western basin, whose sago and papa producers would bear most of the short-term costs of a rapid transition, has the political weight to slow transitions that impose concentrated costs on it, even if the long-run benefits would be distributed more widely.

## What the Sovereignty Does Well

The preceding paragraphs can give a misleading impression if read without balance. The Sovereignty's economic position is not simply one of disadvantage.

The Sovereignty produces goods that the world needs. Sago and papa feed a substantial fraction of the ocean world's equatorial populations, and the Sovereignty's western basin produces them more reliably than any alternative source. The Sovereignty's mineral deposits — particularly the western basin's pyra ore deposits, which made the Sovereignty's deterrence program possible — are not replicated elsewhere. The Sovereignty's nori fisheries and its northern basin emmer fields are among the world's most productive.

The Sovereignty also has an institution that the Reach does not: the circuit relay network's accumulated knowledge of the intermediate belt. The Sovereignty's relay operators have been in continuous commercial contact with intermediate belt polities for longer than the Reach has been administering trade in the northern spine. They know the belt's markets, its commercial customs, its seasonal rhythms, and its political sensitivities in ways that Reach merchants do not. This knowledge is not in any document; it is distributed across the relay operators who have been working those routes for generations.

In a world where the intermediate belt is the primary zone of commercial competition between the Sovereignty and the Reach — which it is, and which Chapter 5 described in terms of the RSC's jurisdictional gap at contested relay points — this accumulated knowledge is a genuine asset. The Sovereignty's commerce is slower to start in a new market, but it is less likely to make the kind of mistakes that damage long-term commercial relationships.

## What the Circuit Economy Looks Like in Year 47 SP

Students of this book are living in Year 47 of the Strange Peace. The Circuit Economy that you are part of has some features that earlier generations predicted and some that they did not.

The predicted: the terms-of-trade problem is ongoing. The Sovereignty's primary goods still sell for less, relative to processed goods, than they did forty years ago. The processing capacity expansion that reformers have argued for since Year 3 SP has proceeded, but slowly. The eastern basin's commercial banking system still provides more of the financing for industrial development than the western and northern basins' own institutions.

The unpredicted: the RSC's Bridge Documentation has worked better than either side expected. Cross-boundary commercial volume is substantially higher in Year 47 than in Year 3. Sovereignty merchants who learned to navigate both the Oran system and the Reach system are genuinely valuable intermediaries in a way that neither the Reach's administered trade system nor the Sovereignty's circuit system anticipated. A generation of Sovereignty merchants has grown up who are commercially fluent in both systems, and their expertise is in demand throughout the intermediate belt.

This generation — your parents' generation, or slightly older — is what the Sovereignty's commercial future most depends on. Not the institutions, which are what they are, nor the terms of trade, which have their own structural logic. The people who know how both systems work, and who can find the trades that neither system identifies on its own.

That knowledge does not appear in the Commerce Council's statistics. It is worth understanding anyway.

## Questions for Discussion

1. The Sovereignty's primary-good specialization developed through a series of commercial decisions that no one intended to produce a permanent disadvantage. Does unintended disadvantage create obligations? If so, whose obligations are they?

2. The terms-of-trade problem means that the Sovereignty's goods buy fewer processed goods over time. One response is to demand better terms. Another is to develop processing capacity so that the Sovereignty produces more of the processed goods. A third is to accept the current terms and use primary good income as efficiently as possible. What are the tradeoffs of each response?

3. The Sovereignty's knowledge of the intermediate belt is described as a "genuine asset." But knowledge distributed across individual relay operators is not the same as knowledge that an institution controls. How does the Sovereignty capture the benefit of knowledge it doesn't own?

4. The text says a generation of commercially bilingual merchants is "what the Sovereignty's commercial future most depends on." Do you think that's true? What are the limits of individual expertise as a foundation for institutional development?

5. The Strange Peace gave the Sovereignty security. It did not automatically produce commercial development. What would the Sovereignty need to do differently to convert its security into commercial advancement? What obstacles stand in the way?
