# Chapter 3: How the Peoples of the Reach Traded With Each Other

*From* Our Reach, Our Trade: Social Studies for Young Learners, *Reach Education Bureau, 4th printing*

---

## The Gifts of Each Archipelago

Have you ever traded something with a friend? Maybe you had two apples and your friend had two oranges, and you each gave one away so you could both enjoy something new. The peoples of the Reach did the same thing — but instead of apples and oranges, they traded the special things that grew or swam near their homes!

Every archipelago in the Reach had something special to share.

**In the cold northern islands,** the fishermen caught sthaq. Sthaq are big, strong fish that swim up the rivers every autumn. When the sthaq came, the whole village worked together! Fathers and mothers and grandparents would all help catch the fish, and then they would split them open and hang them up to dry in the wind. Dried sthaq could last all winter long.

The northern islands also had bakala — a different kind of fish that could be dried until it was as hard as a piece of wood. Bakala lasted even longer than sthaq. You could put bakala in a bag and carry it for months and months, and it would still be good to eat! That made bakala very useful for sending to faraway places.

**In the warm southern islands,** something very different grew. On the wet, green mountainsides, farmers tended little bushes covered in tiny berries. When the berries were picked and dried in the sun, they became marich — a spice that made food taste wonderful! Just a tiny pinch of marich could make a whole pot of soup taste warm and exciting.

Marich could only grow where it was hot and rainy all year round. The people in the cold north could never grow their own marich, no matter how hard they tried. And the people in the warm south could never catch sthaq, because sthaq only swim in cold water. So they needed each other!

## How Did Goods Travel So Far?

The Reach is very, very big. No one person could sail from the farthest northern island to the farthest southern island — it would take years and years! So how did the northern fishermen get their marich, and how did the southern farmers get their bakala?

The answer is: **one island at a time.**

Imagine a line of children standing in a row. The first child has a ball. She hands it to the next child, who hands it to the next, who hands it to the next — all the way down the line! The ball travels a long, long way, even though each child only had to reach out her arm.

Trade in the Reach worked just like this. A fisherman on a northern island would trade his dried bakala to a merchant on the next island south. That merchant would trade the bakala to another merchant farther south. And on and on the bakala traveled, until — many months later — it reached an island where someone wanted to trade marich for it!

This is called **relay trade**, and it was how the peoples of the Reach shared their gifts with each other for hundreds and hundreds of years.

## The Merchants of the Middle Islands

Some islands were right in the middle of the Reach, between the cold north and the warm south. The people who lived on these islands became very good at trading, because everything passed through their hands!

These middle-island merchants had special trading houses called **Tahan houses**. A Tahan house was a busy, important place. Merchants would bring their goods there — bales of kapas cloth, jars of saak oil, baskets of dried marich — and the Tahan traders would check that everything was good quality. They would put a special mark on the best goods, called a **grading seal**. When other merchants far away saw that seal, they knew they could trust what was inside the package.

The Tahan traders also helped people who didn't have enough goods right now but would have some later. "I will give you this kapas cloth today," a Tahan trader might say, "and you can pay me back with bakala when the fishing season comes." This is called **credit**, and it helped people trade even when they didn't have something to offer right away.

## Saak Oil: The Gift That Lit the Way

Of all the things the northern peoples made, one of the most important was **saak oil**. Saak oil came from a tiny, oily little fish that swam up the rivers in springtime. The people would catch the saak fish and cook them down in big pots until all the oil came out.

What was saak oil used for? So many things!

- People burned saak oil in lamps to light their homes during the long, dark winters
- They rubbed saak oil on leather and wood to keep the rain out
- They mixed saak oil into food to help preserve it for months

Saak oil was so important that the very first trade routes in the Reach were created just to carry it! These routes were called the **grease trails**, because the oil made everything along the path a little bit slippery. (Well, not really — but that is how they got their name!)

The grease trails connected the coast, where the saak fish were caught, to villages far inland that had no fish of their own. Later, merchants began carrying other goods along the grease trails too, and that is how the great relay trade routes were born.

## Kapas and Nili: Making Something Beautiful

On the warm islands of the middle Reach, farmers grew a fluffy white plant called **kapas**. When the kapas was picked and spun into thread, it could be woven into soft, comfortable cloth. Almost everyone in the warm parts of the Reach wore kapas cloth.

But plain white kapas cloth was not very exciting, was it? That is where **nili** came in!

Nili is a plant that makes a deep, beautiful blue color. The nili dyers — skilled workers who knew exactly how to turn nili leaves into bright blue dye — would soak the kapas cloth in nili until it turned the most wonderful shade of blue you can imagine. A bolt of plain kapas cloth might be worth a small basket of sardai. But a bolt of nili-dyed kapas cloth? That was worth ten times as much!

The nili dyers discovered something very important: **when you make something more beautiful or more useful, it becomes more valuable.** A plain thing that has been improved by someone's skill and hard work is worth more than the plain thing by itself. This is true in the Reach, and it is true in our world too!

## The Qahwa House: Where Friends Met and News Traveled

In many port towns across the Reach, there was a special building called a **qahwa house**. Qahwa is a bitter, warm drink brewed from dried leaves. When you drink qahwa, it makes you feel awake and alert — perfect for merchants who had important business to discuss!

But qahwa houses were about more than just drinking qahwa. They were the places where people from different islands could meet each other, share stories, and learn what was happening in faraway places. A merchant who had just sailed in from three islands away might sit down in the qahwa house and tell everyone what he had seen along the way. "The sthaq run was very big this year on Varandei!" he might say. Or, "Be careful on the southern passage — there were storms last month."

The qahwa house was like a newspaper, a meeting room, and a restaurant all in one! It was one of the most important places in any trading town, because it was where people came together and shared what they knew.

## Let's Review!

Here are the important things we learned in this chapter:

1. **Every archipelago had something special** — sthaq and bakala in the north, marich in the south, kapas and nili in the middle
2. **Relay trade** moved goods from island to island, one step at a time
3. **Tahan houses** helped merchants trade fairly by checking quality and offering credit
4. **Saak oil** was so important that it created the first trade routes — the grease trails
5. **Nili dyers** showed that making something more beautiful makes it more valuable
6. **Qahwa houses** were where merchants met, shared news, and did business
