# The Legibility Problem

*On what administrative frameworks can see and what they cannot, and why the gap matters more than the framework itself.*

---

The administered territory system in the Reach — Class I through Class III, with discretionary associate-status transitions — is a classification framework. Classification frameworks work by making their subjects *legible*: readable, countable, assessable in terms the framework defines.

James Scott's *Seeing Like a State* (1998) identifies this as the central operation of state administration. Before a state can tax, conscript, regulate, or provide services, it must first be able to *see* the population it governs. Cadastral surveys, censuses, standardized surnames, uniform weights and measures, official languages — these are all legibility projects. They transform a complex, heterogeneous social reality into a simplified grid that the administration can read and act upon.

The simplification is not optional. It is the precondition for governance at scale. A state that could perceive the full complexity of every local arrangement, every customary practice, every informal contract would be paralyzed by information. The grid — the simplified model — is what makes action possible.

The cost of the grid is what it cannot represent.

---

The simulation encodes something adjacent to this, though the mechanism is different from Scott's historical examples.

The administered territory framework has three instruments of legibility: population counts (census), technology levels (development classification), and trade volume (commercial assessment). These are the inputs to the classification system. A territory with low population, low technology, and low trade volume is Class III. A territory with high population, moderate technology, and established trade is Class II. The transition to associate status requires — in the improvised pathway — high trade volume, stable population, and demonstrated administrative capacity.

What the classification system cannot see is *endogenous institutional capacity*. The Vaanthi's sixty-two commercial houses, their credit networks, their quality certification customs, their dispute resolution practices — these are institutional achievements that exist below the resolution of the classification grid. The grid counts trade volume. It does not count the institutions that produce trade volume.

This is Scott's legibility problem in its purest form. The framework cannot act on what it cannot see. And what it cannot see is precisely the thing that would change its assessment if it could.

---

In simulation terms, the gap between framework legibility and ground truth manifests as a lag in the extractiveness-sovereignty interaction.

The extractiveness index — the Acemoglu-Robinson institutional penalty — accumulates when a polity extracts surplus above the tolerable threshold from its periphery. The index degrades the extracting polity's institutional quality, reducing tech growth and administrative effectiveness. This much the simulation models explicitly.

What the simulation models *implicitly* is the legibility gap: the extractiveness index does not decay in response to the administered territory's increasing institutional capacity. It decays in response to *inclusive culture* — to whether the extracting polity's own cultural position favors inclusive institutions. The administered territory's development is invisible to the decay function.

This is structurally correct, even if the mechanism differs from Scott's. In Scott's account, the problem is informational: the state cannot see what the village has built because the state's instruments of perception were not designed to detect it. In the simulation, the problem is structural: the extractiveness decay depends on the *center's* cultural orientation, not the *periphery's* institutional development. The periphery can develop all the institutional capacity it wants; the center's classification framework will not register it until the center's own culture changes.

The Vaanthi petition is the moment when the periphery forces the center to perceive something the center's instruments were not designed to detect. The petition is, in Scott's vocabulary, an act of *counter-legibility*: the administered population making itself visible in terms the framework can process, by using institutional vocabulary the center recognizes, filed through channels the center monitors.

---

There is a deeper implication that Scott identifies and that the simulation's parameters gesture toward without modeling.

The legibility grid does not merely fail to see local complexity — it actively *suppresses* it. When a state imposes a cadastral survey, the customary land tenure arrangements that the survey cannot represent begin to atrophy. When a colonial administration requires a "chief" for each village, villages that had no chief acquire one, and the customary governance practices that did not require a chief lose social standing. The grid does not passively overlook informal institutions; it displaces them.

In the simulation, the administered territory framework's extraction terms displace endogenous commercial institutions by offering an alternative: the Reach's own commercial infrastructure — relay certification, contract enforcement, quality standards. For a Class III territory, this displacement is beneficial: the Reach provides institutions the territory lacks. For a territory that has developed its own institutions, the displacement is harmful: the Reach's framework crowds out endogenous arrangements that might be better adapted to local conditions.

This is the Ostrom argument applied to institutional design rather than commons governance. Ostrom (1990) demonstrated that externally imposed governance rules for common-pool resources frequently perform worse than the rules communities develop endogenously, because the external rules cannot incorporate the local knowledge that endogenous rules embody. The same logic applies to commercial institutions: the Reach's standardized contract enforcement may be less effective for the Vaanthi's specific commercial relationships than the Vaanthi's own customary arrangements — arrangements the classification framework has no mechanism for evaluating, and therefore no mechanism for preserving.

---

The simulation cannot model institutional crowding-out. It does not have a representation of endogenous institutional quality at the territory level. But the *Second Charter* question — whether to redesign the framework to accommodate endogenous capacity rather than substitute for it — is the question the simulation's structural logic produces without being able to answer.

The first charter says: we will make you legible and govern what we can see.

The second charter says: we will learn to see what you have built.

The distance between them is not a parameter. It is the distance between administration and recognition — between a grid that simplifies and a framework that adapts. Most administrations never cross it. The ones that do, do so because someone with authority to redesign the grid looked at a territory that had outgrown its classification and decided that the classification was the problem, not the territory.

The Vaanthi petition is the territory saying: your grid is wrong about us. The Deputy Director's memo is the grid's operator saying: I think the grid might be wrong. The second charter, if it comes, is the institution saying: we will build a different grid.

Whether it comes depends on whether the cost of maintaining the wrong grid — measured in accumulated extractiveness, in sovereignty stress, in the growing distance between the framework's assumptions and the territory's reality — exceeds the cost of replacing it.

The simulation models the costs. It does not model the decision.
