# The Staple Trap

*On why nori-economy polities grow rich in the relay era and fragile when the relay era ends.*

---

The simulation's world is full of variety, but from the perspective of the relay trade system, polities are not infinitely varied. They sort into recognizable types based on what they produce, and one of the most distinctive types is the relay specialist: the polity whose primary economic role is not agricultural surplus or strategic resource extraction but commercial intermediation. These polities control the chokepoints, certify the quality grades, and capture the information asymmetry that the Greif intermediary model predicts.

In Aeolia, this type concentrates most heavily in nori-growing archipelagos. Nori culture seeds the IO position at [0.35, 0.65] — outward enough to be trade-oriented, individual enough for competitive commerce — and nori's biological properties (fast-growing, high-value, requires ocean management rather than land management) produce polities whose relationship with the sea is fundamentally commercial rather than extractive. Nori polities don't just fish; they salt, dry, and ship. They don't just produce; they grade and certify. Their institutional life is organized around the relay trade in a way that paddi polities' is not.

Harold Innis (1930) analyzed this pattern in a different context — the fur trade and cod fisheries of early Canada — and named what he found: the **staple trap**.

---

## The Innis Argument

Innis's argument, developed through *The Fur Trade in Canada* (1930) and *The Cod Fisheries* (1940), was structural. Economies built on commodity export develop infrastructure oriented toward extraction and export: transportation routes that run from production zone to port, not from region to region; commercial institutions that grade and certify export quality but don't develop the internal marketing infrastructure that domestic diversification would require; political economies captured by export interests who benefit from the current structure and resist changes that would threaten it.

This is the staple trap: once you have built the infrastructure for commodity export, the infrastructure itself becomes a constraint. Switching to a different economic model requires not just new investment but the depreciation of existing infrastructure and the political defeat of existing commercial interests. The more successful the staple economy has been, the deeper the trap.

Watkins (1963) formalized this into the "staple theory of economic growth," identifying the conditions under which staple-based economies either escape the trap (by using export surplus to develop backward linkages, forward linkages, and final demand linkages) or remain locked in (when export surplus is remitted rather than reinvested, when linkages don't develop, when the political economy is captured by export interests).

---

## The Nori Economy in the Simulation

Nori polities in the simulation follow a recognizable trajectory in the relay era. High outward culture orientation → high trade exposure → high relay bonus from the Greif mechanism → growing commercial surplus → reinforced outward orientation (prosperity → Individual, trade → Outward). They are self-reinforcing commercial success stories during the period when the relay layer is dominant.

Their vulnerability appears in two specific transitions:

**The administered trade era (tech ~5).** The administered layer's arrival changes the relay system's structure. Administered trade captures bulk commodity flows; relay trade persists for specialty goods. For nori polities, whose specialty is precisely the kind of high-value commercial service that administered trade is less suited to replace, the administered era is survivable. But polities with primarily relay-specialist infrastructure face the administered era's extraction more directly than polities with more diversified production.

**Post-DF competition for the intermediate belt.** After the Dark Forest fires, the intermediate belt becomes a contested zone — both hegemons applying the stability-instability paradox's proxy bonus to non-nuclear territory. Nori polities in the relay specialist position are structurally exposed: their commercial neutrality was an asset when both hegemons wanted the relay circuit intact; in the nuclear era, their strategic position makes them interesting to hegemons looking for proxy leverage.

The Innis trap appears here not as a failure of development but as a success constraint: the very infrastructure that made nori polities commercially central makes their neutrality difficult to maintain. If they are too commercially valuable to either hegemon to be genuinely neutral, their relay specialist position becomes a liability.

---

## What the Simulation Doesn't Model

Innis's full argument includes the spatial dimension of the staple trap: the geography of extraction routes literally shapes the space of political possibility. Fur trade routes in Canada ran north-south to the ports, not east-west along the St. Lawrence; the result was a continental economy oriented toward export and resistant to internal integration.

The simulation models commercial connectivity but not transportation infrastructure specifically. Nori polities don't have identifiable "relay routes" that constrain their internal economic development; they have contact_sets and relay bonuses that reflect their position in the graph, but the physical infrastructure of their commercial orientation isn't represented.

This means the simulation captures the Innis pattern's commercial logic (relay specialists becoming dependent on the relay layer) without capturing its spatial logic (commercial infrastructure shapes what economic transitions are possible). A simulation with explicit transportation infrastructure would produce more differentiated staple-trap outcomes: relay polities whose infrastructure was oriented toward a now-defunct trade partner would face higher switching costs than polities whose infrastructure was more general-purpose.

---

## Practical Observation

The most commercially prosperous polities in a simulation run with a functioning relay layer are typically nori or emmer polities in the relay chokepoint position. Their prosperity is real. It is also fragile in ways that make them strategically interesting to analysts watching for late-game vulnerabilities.

The simulation's version of the Innis argument: polities that build commercial empires on the relay layer are building on a structure that will change. The relay layer itself won't disappear, but the political economy of the relay layer changes when administered trade arrives, and changes again when nuclear deterrence produces the proxy competition. Polities that can adapt — that use relay prosperity to build diversified institutions rather than deepening relay dependence — outlast polities that cannot. The cultural drift toward Individual/Outward that the relay layer produces is an asset for adaptation; the institutional lock-in of commercial prosperity is its counterweight.
