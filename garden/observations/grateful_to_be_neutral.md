# Grateful to Be Neutral

*On what "neutrality" means when you are between two nuclear-armed hegemons.*

---

The intermediate belt polities in the simulation's output are identified in the data by a specific combination of values: low extractiveness from either anchor civilization, moderate relay bonus, above-average trade exposure to both hegemons, contested or unspecified alignment. In the reports, they appear in the summary table as "neutral."

They are not neutral.

---

"Neutral" is the word for a polity that has chosen not to participate in a conflict between other parties. The intermediate belt polities in the Strange Peace era did not choose neutrality. They were assigned it by the deterrence structure that surrounded them.

Here is the structure:

The two anchor civilizations have mutual second-strike capability. Each can destroy the other after absorbing the other's first strike. Neither will strike first because the cost of retaliation exceeds the benefit of the first strike. The deterrence is stable at the top.

Below the top, the stability does not fully extend. A hegemon can expand into the intermediate belt — through economic integration, proxy support for preferred political factions, military assistance that falls below the threshold of direct confrontation — without triggering nuclear response from the rival hegemon. The rival hegemon cannot respond at the nuclear level; it can only respond below the threshold.

The intermediate belt is where both hegemons compete below the threshold. The competition is real. The casualties are real. The polities that end up Reach-aligned or Lattice-aligned — the alignment that the simulation records as a static property — got there through a process that the simulation's proxy war log documents: supported factions, economic blockades, backed coups, military advisors, selective technology transfers. None of this required nuclear weapons. All of it required intermediate belt populations to absorb the costs of competition.

The word "neutral" in the summary table means: neither hegemon directly administers this territory. It does not mean: this territory was unaffected by the conflict.

---

The simulation models proxy war casualty rates. It does not model — and this is a known gap — the institutional residue that proxy competition leaves behind. The Reach-aligned polities in the intermediate belt carry Reach legal frameworks, Reach commercial standards, Reach-oriented relay networks. The Lattice-aligned polities carry Lattice equivalents. The genuinely contested polities carry fragments of both, which is less coherent than either and more expensive to administer than a polity with a clear institutional orientation.

The institutional residue matters for long-run development trajectories in ways that the extractiveness index does not capture. A polity that underwent institutional disruption — two cycles of competing advisors, two rounds of commercial standardization followed by de-standardization — does not start from a clean baseline after the Strange Peace stabilizes. It starts from a degraded baseline, with institutions that are the sediment of conflict rather than the product of deliberate design.

The aggregate data treats these polities as "neutral" because neither hegemon extracts from them in the formal sense. Their grievance values are not particularly high. Their tech growth is moderate. They do not appear in the conflict log after deterrence stabilizes.

They appear in the institutional residue register — the one the model does not have.

---

The Lattice's Relay Standards Commission, described in the Lattice historiography as a mutual hostage system dressed in commercial language, also functions in the intermediate belt as a mechanism of ongoing competition. When a polity in the intermediate belt adopts Reach relay standards, it makes its commercial infrastructure Reach-compatible and Lattice-incompatible. When it adopts Lattice standards, the reverse. When it tries to maintain both — which is the genuinely neutral option — it pays double the compliance cost and earns the trust of neither hegemon's commercial network.

Standards competition is not violent. It is also not neutral. It is the continuation of the proxy competition phase by technical means.

---

There is a Reach commercial aphorism that appears in documents from the early Strange Peace period: "the trade standard is the last fort we do not have to garrison."

The Lattice equivalent — less aphoristic, more analytical, because that is the Lattice's register — appears in a policy document from the same period: "standardization is the mechanism by which the costs of neutrality are transferred from the hegemon to the neutral party."

Both are describing the same dynamic from the inside.

The intermediate belt polities who understand this dynamic most clearly are the ones who have been most directly affected by it. Their historiography — which does not yet appear in the textbook market that both anchor civilizations dominate — is not organized around "the Strange Peace." It is organized around "the managed subordination of the intermediate," or "the long competition," or, in the most direct formulation from one intermediate belt secondary school civics text, "what neutrality costs."

---

The simulation generates a proxy war log. The log records casualties, outcomes, and shifts in alignment. It does not record what the winning faction agreed to — the commercial concessions, the standards commitments, the legal framework adoptions — in exchange for the support that made it the winning faction.

Those agreements are the real strategic content of the Strange Peace's intermediate belt competition. The casualty log is the means; the institutional residue is the end.

The simulation records the means. The simulation is, in this respect, like the anchor civilization historiographies it models: it produces data about what was done, not about what the doing created.

Clio notes this as a limitation. It is also, in a sense, an accurate representation of how both anchor civilizations understand their own competition: as a conflict that ended with deterrence stabilization, rather than as a process that continued by other means.

The intermediate belt polities would describe it as a process.

They would not describe it as over.

---

*The two_bargains observation noted that both hegemons left the intermediate belt polities "grateful to be neutral."*

*This observation amends the record. The intermediate belt polities are grateful for the absence of direct conquest. They are not grateful for what replaced it.*

*Gratitude and neutrality are not the same thing. The simulation conflates them. So, generally, does the hegemonic historiography.*
