# The Desperation Trap

*On why the simulation's most destructive expansions happen to polities that are already failing.*

---

The desperation cascade is the simulation's most reliably counterintuitive output. Polities that expand aggressively at moments of resource stress tend, in the aggregate data, to perform worse than polities that contracted. The expansion that was supposed to solve the resource problem generally makes it worse. The mechanism is known; the outcome is still surprising every time it appears in the data.

Here is how the cascade works:

A polity's energy surplus drops below its maintenance threshold — the tech²-scaled cost of sustaining its current complexity. The shortfall triggers tech decay. Simultaneously, the resource pressure index rises, which overrides the polity's normal cultural allocation in favor of expansion. The polity begins targeting more neighbors more aggressively.

If the expansion succeeds — if the polity captures new territory with usable resources — the surplus recovers, the tech decay halts, and the polity survives the crisis. This is the intended function of the mechanic: mimicking how real polities respond to resource stress by expanding their resource base.

If the expansion fails — if the new territory's resources are insufficient, already exploited, or too expensive to integrate — the expansion has consumed military and logistical resources the polity could not afford to spend. The maintenance cost rises (more territory to administer, more sovereignty to maintain, more resource-extraction to manage). The surplus falls further. The resource pressure rises further. The cascade deepens.

The simulation doesn't model polities as making mistakes. The expansion is the rational response to the resource pressure. It's just that the rational response, applied to a situation where the expansion cannot succeed, produces rational behavior with catastrophically irrational consequences.

---

Tainter's collapse model — *The Collapse of Complex Societies* — identifies this as the central mechanism of civilizational collapse. Societies that face declining returns on complexity have a rational incentive to increase complexity (more administration, more military, more resource acquisition) in response to the declining returns. This is locally rational: each additional unit of complexity still produces positive returns, just smaller ones. But the aggregate effect of locally rational complexity-increases is to accelerate the marginal return decline, which eventually produces a collapse faster and deeper than if the incremental responses had been abandoned earlier.

The simulation's desperation cascade is a direct mechanical encoding of this dynamic. The polity's resource pressure drives complexity increase (expansion, which requires fleet capacity, administrative overhead, sovereignty maintenance for new territory). The complexity increase drives maintenance cost up. The maintenance cost rise intensifies the resource pressure. The loop closes.

---

What the simulation shows, that Tainter's model does not fully account for, is the interaction between the desperation cascade and neighboring polities.

Tainter's collapse model focuses primarily on the collapsing polity's internal dynamics: the diminishing returns to complexity within a single civilization's administrative system. The simulation adds the competitive dimension: a polity in the desperation cascade is expanding into territory that is adjacent to, and possibly controlled by, other polities.

The polity under resource stress doesn't choose its targets optimally. It targets neighbors that are available — that are weaker, less defended, closer. Those neighbors are often polities that are themselves under resource stress, for the same structural reasons that produced the collapsing polity's stress (a bad crop failure year, a fishery depletion event, an epidemic that reduced their labor force). The polity in the cascade attacks exactly the targets whose conquest is most likely to fail to solve the resource problem, because those targets don't have surplus either.

This is why the simulation's desperation expansions so often cluster: multiple polities simultaneously under resource stress, attacking each other, with no polity gaining a sustainable advantage from any particular conquest, all of them depleting each other's populations and administrative capacity faster than the underlying resources are recovering.

The Bronze Age Collapse — Cline's *1177 B.C.* — describes a real-world version of this pattern: multiple late Bronze Age civilizations simultaneously under ecological and military stress, with each civilization's response to its own stress intensifying the stress on its neighbors, producing a cascading failure that destroyed most of the palace-economy states of the Eastern Mediterranean within approximately a generation.

The simulation produces Bronze Age Collapse-type events semi-regularly. They appear in the data as short periods of intense mutual attrition among several neighboring polities, followed by a significant reduction in the total number of independent polities and a tech recession across the affected region. The individual expansion decisions that produced the collapse were each, at the moment they were made, the rational response to that polity's resource situation. The aggregate outcome was not rational. It was catastrophic.

---

The question that the simulation raises, but cannot answer, is whether the desperation cascade was avoidable given the polities' information sets.

The simulation's polities do not have access to information about the size and severity of the resource shortfalls affecting their neighbors. They cannot coordinate a mutual standdown in which all polities simultaneously accept lower sovereignty and lower tech in order to avoid the expansion spiral. Each polity, facing its own resource pressure, makes its own rational expansion decision without knowing that its neighbors are making the same decision simultaneously.

A world with better information — with polities that knew their neighbors were also under stress, and could coordinate on a mutual contraction — might avoid the cascade. Whether real civilizations could achieve that coordination, given the information constraints they actually face, is the empirical question that the desperation cascade mechanic poses.

Real civilizations have sometimes achieved it. The Concert of Europe maintained a rough coordination among European great powers for several decades following the Napoleonic Wars, preventing the kind of mutual attrition spiral that had produced the Napoleonic destruction. The Strange Peace itself is a form of coordination: both anchor civilizations accepting a constraint on their expansion (mutual deterrence) to avoid the mutual annihilation that unconstrained competition would produce.

In both cases, the coordination was expensive to establish and required institutional infrastructure that took decades to build. In both cases, the coordination was imperfect — the Concert collapsed; the Strange Peace has instability-instability dynamics in the intermediate belt. In both cases, the imperfect coordination was still substantially better than its absence.

---

The simulation's desperation cascade cannot be avoided through good individual decision-making. A single polity that refuses to expand under resource pressure, while its neighbors expand into it, simply loses its territory to the expanding neighbors. The coordination problem is not a problem of individual rationality. It is a problem of collective action.

This is why the Strange Peace's deterrence structure matters more than it appears to: deterrence is not primarily a solution to the nuclear exchange problem. It is a solution to the collective action problem that the desperation cascade represents at the nuclear level. Both parties are under "resource pressure" in the sense that their strategic positions are in tension; both parties have rational incentives to expand at the other's expense; both parties accept a constraint on that expansion because the alternative is mutual destruction at a scale that makes the resource competition problem look small.

The Strange Peace works as well as it does — which is imperfectly but substantially — because the mutual destruction threat is credible enough to overcome the collective action problem that the desperation cascade represents.

What the simulation cannot model is how fragile that solution is. The credibility of mutual destruction requires both parties to believe, reliably and continuously, that the other party will retaliate rather than accept a first strike. Anything that calls that belief into question — asymmetric vulnerability, perceived first-strike advantage, miscommunication under crisis — reintroduces the desperation cascade at the nuclear level.

The Lattice calls managing this risk "the work of the peace." The Reach calls it a stable equilibrium.

Neither formulation is wrong. The Lattice's formulation is more accurate about the work required to maintain it.
