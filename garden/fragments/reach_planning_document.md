# Strategic Assessment: The Southern Disposition Question

*Reach Military Planning Directorate — Restricted Distribution*
*Provenance: Declassified under the Reach Transparency Statute, Year 38 SP.*
*Original dating: approximately Year -12 BP (approximately twelve years after the Reach's first pyra test).*

---

**Classification: Eyes of Fleet Admiralty and Civil Cabinet Only**  
**Reference: RMPDSA-247-R**

---

## Subject: Assessment of Options Regarding the Lattice's Nuclear Development Program

The purpose of this memorandum is to provide the Civil Cabinet with a frank assessment of the strategic options available with respect to the Lattice's nuclear development program, and to make recommendations regarding the appropriate course of action. The Civil Cabinet has requested this assessment in response to intelligence indicating that the Lattice's program is more advanced than previously estimated.

The Military Planning Directorate provides this assessment in its professional capacity and without political recommendation. Questions of policy are for the Cabinet. Questions of military feasibility and strategic consequence are within our remit. This memorandum addresses the latter only.

---

## I. The Current Situation

The Reach achieved its first successful pyra test twelve years ago. Our program has since produced a substantial operational arsenal with confirmed delivery capability against the Lattice's major population and industrial centers.

The Lattice's program has been under development for an estimated thirty years. Our intelligence assessment, revised upward following the intercepts of the past six months, now places the Lattice's first successful test at approximately five years from present, with the revised range of three to seven years. The previous estimate was seven to twelve years.

The significance of this revision: the window of strategic asymmetry is shorter than the Civil Cabinet has been planning against. If the three-year estimate is accurate, we have less than one planning cycle before the strategic situation changes fundamentally.

---

## II. The Available Options

The Military Planning Directorate identifies three principal options.

**Option A: Preventive action.** Strike the Lattice's known and suspected nuclear facilities and command infrastructure before the Lattice achieves operational capability. If successful, this would extend the Reach's strategic asymmetry indefinitely. The Lattice's program would be set back by an estimated ten to twenty years.

**Option B: Preventive consolidation.** Use the current asymmetry to impose political settlements on the intermediate belt polities that would formalize Reach commercial and political dominance before mutual deterrence constrains our ability to do so. This is a softer version of strategic action — not destroying the Lattice's program, but using the window to lock in Reach advantages before the window closes.

**Option C: Deterrence preparation.** Accept that the Lattice will achieve operational nuclear capability within the assessment window, and focus on ensuring the Reach's second-strike capability is robust enough to make the resulting mutual deterrence stable. Use the window to expand and harden our arsenal rather than to exploit our current asymmetry.

---

## III. Assessment of Option A

The Military Planning Directorate's assessment of Option A is that it is not feasible at acceptable risk levels.

The Lattice's nuclear facilities are geographically distributed across all three basins. Our intelligence has identified eleven confirmed and six probable facility locations. A preventive strike capable of setting back the program by ten to twenty years would require simultaneous action against seventeen or more targets, including several located in interior basin positions where our strike capability is degraded.

The probability of achieving full facility neutralization against seventeen distributed targets with acceptable operational security is assessed at below 30%. The most likely outcome of a preventive strike is partial facility neutralization — destroying eight to twelve targets, leaving five to nine operational. A partial strike would eliminate the element of surprise, precipitate whatever Lattice response capability remains, and produce a retaliation against Reach population and industrial centers that our current civil defense posture cannot adequately mitigate.

We note additionally that our estimate of the Lattice's operational capability is acknowledged to carry substantial uncertainty. We place the range at three to seven years. The lower bound assumes the Lattice's program has made recent technical progress that our intelligence has not detected. If the lower bound is accurate, the Lattice may have limited operational capability already — insufficient for strategic retaliation at scale, but sufficient to deliver one or two weapons to Reach territory. A preventive strike under these conditions would produce an exchange rather than a clean elimination.

The Military Planning Directorate does not recommend Option A.

---

## IV. Assessment of Option B

Option B is feasible within the current asymmetry window and presents significantly lower risk than Option A.

The intermediate belt polities are currently navigating the existing asymmetry by maintaining dual commercial relationships with both the Reach and the Lattice. Our assessment is that approximately sixty percent of intermediate belt polities would accept formal commercial integration with the Reach under the current conditions — integration that would be difficult to reverse once established — if the terms offered were sufficiently attractive and if the Reach's commercial presence were reinforced by the kind of fleet deployments that make the costs of non-acceptance visible.

Option B would extend the Reach's commercial dominance into the intermediate belt before mutual deterrence eliminates the possibility of such extension. The intermediate belt's strategic value is primarily commercial: it contains a substantial fraction of the world's most productive relay nodes, and its commercial orientation toward the Reach or the Lattice will shape the terms of trade for the next several centuries.

The Military Planning Directorate does not oppose Option B on feasibility grounds. We note, however, that intermediate belt integration pursued under visible fleet pressure during the current asymmetry window will be understood as coercion. The political settlements achieved through coercion are less durable than those achieved through commercial persuasion, and the resentments generated may undermine the Reach's commercial position in the post-deterrence period.

This is a policy question, not a military feasibility question. We note it for the Cabinet's consideration.

---

## V. Assessment of Option C

Option C — deterrence preparation — is the option most consistent with a stable long-run outcome, but it requires the Cabinet to accept that the window of strategic asymmetry will close and that this is acceptable.

The Military Planning Directorate's assessment is that mutual deterrence, once established, is stable provided both sides have credible second-strike capability. The conditions for stability are technically achievable on the Reach's side: we have sufficient arsenal size, geographic distribution, and delivery reliability to maintain second-strike credibility against any realistic first-strike scenario.

If the Lattice achieves operational capability within the assessment window and both sides transition into mutual deterrence with credible second-strike capability, the strategic situation will be constrained but not strategically threatening. The Reach will lose the ability to impose unilateral settlements on the Lattice. It will retain commercial, technical, and institutional advantages built over the pre-deterrence period. These advantages are durable and not dependent on continued military asymmetry to maintain.

What the Cabinet must accept under Option C: the Lattice's nuclear development program will succeed. The Reach will have had a window of strategic asymmetry and chosen not to exploit it militarily. If the Lattice subsequently behaves aggressively — using mutual deterrence as cover for non-military pressure in the intermediate belt — the Reach will have accepted a constraint on its responses that it did not have before.

This is the genuine cost of Option C. The Cabinet should not be told otherwise.

---

## VI. Recommendation

The Military Planning Directorate's recommendation is Option C, with selective elements of Option B pursued through commercial rather than military means.

The basis for this recommendation: Option A's risk profile is unacceptable. Option B pursued through fleet pressure generates political costs that may exceed its commercial benefits. Option C with commercial consolidation preserves the Reach's core strategic interests — commercial dominance, institutional advantage, relay network control — while avoiding the catastrophic downside risk of a partially successful preventive strike.

We note that this recommendation reflects strategic risk assessment, not political philosophy. If the Cabinet's assessment of the Reach's interests leads to different conclusions about the acceptable risk level, this recommendation should be revised accordingly.

The fundamental judgment the Cabinet must make: is the constraint of mutual deterrence worse than the risk of a partially successful preventive strike and the exchange that might follow? Our assessment is that it is not. The Cabinet may disagree.

We request a meeting at the Cabinet's earliest convenience to discuss this assessment in the classified oral setting that the subject matter requires.
