# Internal Assessment: The Neutral Arbitrator Question

*Sovereignty Commerce Council — Internal Circulation*
*Provenance: Declassified under the Sovereignty Transparency Act, Year 52 SP.*
*Original dating: Year 12 SP.*

---

**Classification: Commerce Council Members Only**
**Reference: SCC-12-47**

---

## Subject: RSC Arbitration Crisis — Options for the Neutral Roster Negotiation

The Commerce Council has requested this assessment in response to the breakdown of three consecutive RSC arbitration proceedings in the past eighteen months. The breakdown pattern is consistent: Sovereignty and Reach arbitrators issue contradictory rulings, the nominal tie-breaking procedure produces delays rather than resolution, and the affected merchants seek resolution through private channels outside the RSC framework.

The private channels are becoming increasingly normalized. This is the problem.

---

## I. What the Current Breakdown Reveals

The RSC's Arbitration Chamber was designed with the assumption that bilateral arbitration — one Sovereignty-appointed, one Reach-appointed — would produce rulings sufficiently often to make the Chamber functional, with unresolved cases representing exceptions rather than the rule. This assumption has not been validated by nine years of operation.

Our current data: of 847 cross-boundary disputes submitted to the Chamber since Year 3 SP, 312 (37%) were resolved by bilateral agreement. 535 (63%) required the tie-breaking procedure. Of those 535 cases, 201 (38% of tie-break cases, 24% of total) were not resolved within the Chamber's twelve-month procedural deadline and defaulted to private resolution.

The structural problem: the tie-breaking procedure — currently a consultation panel of three senior arbitrators from each side, with a supermajority requirement — is not a tie-breaker. It is a higher-level bilateral forum with the same structural properties as the bilateral Chamber. It produces the same deadlocks more slowly.

The Reach's Commercial Director has privately communicated to Minister Takabori that the Reach's commercial community is losing confidence in the RSC framework and is exploring alternatives. The alternatives they are exploring — primarily a proposal to use Reach commercial courts as the default jurisdiction for all cross-boundary disputes — would be considerably worse for Sovereignty merchants than the current imperfect RSC framework.

This is the context for the neutral arbitrator proposal.

---

## II. The Neutral Roster Proposal

The proposal, which the Reach first advanced at the Year 11 SP Framework Review and which Minister Takabori has agreed to explore, is to establish a roster of neutral arbitrators drawn from intermediate belt polities, with the roster jointly approved by the Sovereignty and the Reach. Cross-boundary disputes that the bilateral panel cannot resolve within ninety days would be referred to a neutral arbitrator drawn randomly from the roster, whose ruling would be binding.

The operational question is straightforward: how do we construct a roster that the Sovereignty can accept?

The political question is harder: we are being asked to accept that a significant fraction of cross-boundary commercial disputes will be decided by parties we do not appoint and cannot directly control. The argument for accepting this is that the alternative — Reach commercial court jurisdiction — involves parties we do not appoint and cannot directly control, who are also structurally aligned with Reach commercial interests. A neutral roster is better than Reach jurisdiction as a fallback. Whether it is better than the current impasse is the question we must assess.

---

## III. Assessment of the Roster Options

The proposal envisions a roster of approximately forty neutral arbitrators from six to eight intermediate belt polities. The Commerce Council's working group has identified three distinct roster configurations that are operationally viable.

**Configuration A: Relay Specialist Roster.** Arbitrators drawn from the major independent relay node operators in the intermediate belt — the merchant houses whose commercial knowledge spans both the Reach and Sovereignty systems. These parties have practical fluency in both documentation systems, genuine commercial interests in maintaining functional cross-boundary trade, and established reputations for reliability in their own commercial networks.

The risk: relay node operators are economically dependent on cross-boundary trade volume. Their neutrality is genuine on documentation questions but potentially compromised on questions that affect trade volume — which means that rulings affecting the terms of trade, not merely the documentation standards, may favor volume over Sovereignty substantive interests.

**Configuration B: Academic and Professional Roster.** Arbitrators drawn from commercial law faculties and professional arbitration bodies at neutral institutions — the Oran Polytechnic's commercial law program, the Northern Relay Institute, the Western Basin Commercial Standards Authority. These parties have technical expertise and institutional credibility.

The risk: these institutions are less commercially fluent than relay node operators. They know commercial law; they may not know that the distinction between an Oran Grade 3 and a Reach-9 certification matters not just in principle but in the specific market contexts where it determines price. Technical rulings that are legally correct but commercially naive can produce worse outcomes than imperfect rulings from experienced merchants.

**Configuration C: Mixed Roster.** Combination of relay specialists and commercial law professionals, with the random draw weighted toward commercial law professionals for documentation disputes and relay specialists for market-value disputes. Classification of dispute type would be decided by a pre-screening panel.

The risk: complexity. A weighted random draw with a pre-screening panel introduces procedural overhead that slows the arbitration and creates additional dispute surface (parties will dispute the pre-screening classification to influence the roster draw).

The Commerce Council's working group recommends Configuration B with a structured mentorship requirement: neutral arbitrators must have completed at least one advisory engagement with an active cross-boundary commercial house before they are eligible for the binding arbitration roster. This addresses the commercial naivety risk without the complexity of a mixed roster.

---

## IV. The Sovereignty's Negotiating Objectives

If the Commerce Council authorizes proceeding with the neutral roster negotiation, our objectives should be ranked as follows:

**First priority: roster composition.** We must prevent the neutral roster from being dominated by polities within the Reach's commercial sphere. Intermediate belt polities that depend primarily on Reach commercial banking, Reach trade finance, or Reach market access are not genuinely neutral. Our working group recommends that no more than 40% of roster arbitrators should come from polities whose primary commercial relationship is with the Reach.

**Second priority: binding authority scope.** We should accept binding neutral arbitration for documentation and quality standard disputes — the cases where the Chamber is most often deadlocked and where neutral technical expertise is most useful. We should resist extending binding authority to market access disputes or disputes that involve the Sovereignty's unilateral trade policy. The RSC's mandate does not currently cover market access; accepting neutral arbitration of market access disputes would expand the RSC's effective jurisdiction in ways the Bilateral Commerce Framework did not authorize.

**Third priority: roster renewal terms.** The neutral roster should be reviewed and renewed every seven years, with each side having the right to remove up to 25% of roster members at each renewal without cause. This preserves our ability to adjust the roster composition as our assessment of individual arbitrators' neutrality develops.

---

## V. The Longer Assessment

The neutral arbitrator question is being presented to the Commerce Council as a technical fix for a procedural problem. It is not only that.

The Reach's proposal reflects a judgment that the current RSC framework is failing and that the Reach has more to gain from a reformed RSC than from letting it fail. This is correct from their perspective: Reach merchants need the RSC's Bridge Documentation, even if they need it less than Sovereignty merchants do, and the Reach's commercial courts cannot easily absorb the volume of cross-boundary disputes that the RSC currently fails to resolve.

But the Reach also has a longer-term interest in this negotiation that we should not ignore. The Reach's commercial courts are its preferred dispute resolution mechanism because Reach commercial courts apply Reach standards. A Reach proposal to extend Reach commercial court jurisdiction over cross-boundary disputes is a proposal to extend Reach standards governance over Sovereignty commercial activity. The neutral roster proposal is better than that outcome — but it is also a way of forestalling it by creating an alternative that the Reach finds acceptable.

The question the Commerce Council must ask is not only whether the neutral roster is better than Reach commercial court jurisdiction. It is whether the neutral roster is better than the current impasse — and whether the current impasse, however costly, is creating pressure that will eventually produce outcomes worse than either option.

Our assessment: the impasse is not sustainable. Private resolution channels becoming normalized is the worst outcome, because private resolution means Reach commercial norms governing by default for merchants who lack the resources to sustain formal arbitration delays. The neutral roster is imperfect. It is better than the impasse. We recommend proceeding.

We recommend that Minister Takabori be authorized to negotiate on the basis of the three priorities above, with flexibility on Configuration A vs. Configuration B, and without flexibility on binding authority scope.

*Archivist's note: The Bilateral Commerce Framework Amendment establishing the neutral arbitrator roster was signed in Year 13 SP. The initial roster comprised thirty-eight arbitrators from seven intermediate belt polities; a Northern Relay Institute majority was accepted after Reach negotiators rejected a Oran Polytechnic majority as insufficiently commercially experienced. The Sovereignty achieved its second and third priorities in full. On the first priority, the final roster composition was 44% Reach-proximate polities — 4% above the working group's recommended ceiling, accepted by Minister Takabori as the price of the second-priority gains. The 44% figure has never been publicly reported; this document is the primary source for the negotiating record.*
