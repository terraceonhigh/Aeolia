# Kalapa Confederacy Connectivity Renewal Proceedings, Year 72 SP

*Third Connectivity Renewal since Circuit accession. Abridged record of the Assessment Session, Month 4, Year 72 SP. Public session — full attendance of the Confederacy Assembly and visiting Circuit representatives.*

---

## Preliminary Assessment (Keeper of Documents, Lila Deneu-Oren)

This Connectivity Renewal is the Confederacy's third since accession. It is the first in which no member of the Assembly was present at the original deliberation. The decision we made in Year 29 is, for the youngest members of this body, not a decision at all — it is a condition. They have known nothing but Circuit membership. The relay fee structure, the court access protocols, the documentation standards — these are not policies we chose. They are the water we swim in.

I note this because it changes what renewal means. At the first renewal (Year 59 SP), we were reviewing a decision. We had memory of what life was like under the Reach's administered framework, and the comparison was available to us. At this renewal, we are not reviewing a decision. We are assessing an institution from inside it.

The assessment is structured according to the standard Circuit renewal protocol. I will present findings under three headings: commercial terms, governance integration, and strategic position.

---

## Commercial Terms

The relay fee structure negotiated at the second renewal (Year 59 SP) has performed within the expected band. Total fees paid to the Circuit over the thirteen-year period: approximately fourteen percent of gross relay transit value. This is two percentage points above the Circuit median and three points below the Reach's current administered levy for comparable-position territories.

The differential has narrowed. At our accession in Year 29, the Circuit fee advantage over the Reach's administered framework was approximately eight points. It is now three points. This narrowing has two sources: our own fees have risen modestly (the relay participation fee scales with volume, and our volume has increased); and the Reach's Civil Registry has reduced administered levies across its network by approximately three points since Year 60, as part of what the Reach's own institutional review describes as a "recalibration toward sustainable extraction levels."

I will note without further comment that the Reach's recalibration began approximately four years after the Vaanthi Basin's petition to the Commerce Council. The timing may be coincidental.

The practical implication: the commercial advantage of Circuit membership over Reach administration, for a polity in Kalapa's position, is no longer decisive. It is positive but modest. If we were making the accession decision today on commercial grounds alone, the case would be closer than it was.

---

## Governance Integration

This is where the renewal assessment is most consequential.

Over forty-three years of Circuit membership, the Confederacy has developed institutional relationships with the Sovereignty's governance framework that have no equivalent in the Reach's administered system. Specifically:

**Court access.** Kalapa merchants have standing in the Sovereignty's Commercial Arbitration Court. This standing has been exercised 247 times since accession. The average resolution time is fourteen months. The resolution rate is 83%. The Reach's administered framework does not provide court access to non-metropolitan parties; disputes are resolved through the Civil Registry's Basin Liaison Office, which has no published resolution statistics and no formal appeals mechanism.

**Documentation standards.** Kalapa's cargo documentation now complies with Bridge Documentation standards, which are recognized across the entire Circuit network. Our merchants can certify cargo for relay transit to any Circuit port without additional validation. Under the Reach's framework, cargo documentation is validated by the Civil Registry and is accepted only within the administered relay network. The documentation systems are incompatible. Reverting to Reach administration would require re-certification of our entire commercial documentation infrastructure.

**Representation.** The Confederacy holds an observer seat on the Commerce Council's External Relations Committee. We do not vote. But we are present in the room where commercial policy is discussed. Under the Reach's administered framework, policy affecting administered territories is made by the Civil Registry directorate. The territories are not present.

These governance integrations are, in practical terms, irreversible. The cost of unwinding court standing, documentation standards, and committee relationships would exceed any plausible commercial benefit from returning to administered status. We are locked in.

I say this without judgment. Lock-in is not necessarily harmful. These institutions work. They work better than the alternatives available to us. The question is not whether we should undo them — we should not and could not — but whether we should negotiate this renewal with full awareness that our bargaining position has weakened precisely because these institutions work so well.

---

## Strategic Position

The Confederacy's alignment, in the Walt framework that the Circuit's own academic consultants use, has hardened from approximately 0.12 (slight Sovereignty orientation) at accession to approximately 0.45 (moderate Sovereignty orientation) at this renewal. This is within the expected range for a polity that has been in the Circuit network for four decades.

The alignment has practical consequences. The Reach's expansion targeting of polities in our alignment band is meaningfully deterred — the Walt protection penalty makes absorption expensive. This is genuine security. It is also genuine dependency. Our security is a function of our alignment, and our alignment is a function of our institutional integration with the Sovereignty.

The strategic assessment is therefore the same as the governance assessment: we are more secure than we were before accession, and we are more dependent than we were before accession, and these two facts are the same fact described from different angles.

---

## Recommendation

This office recommends renewal on terms no worse than the current structure, with a negotiation target of reducing the relay participation fee by one point to reflect the narrowing differential with the Reach. The Assembly should enter this renewal with clarity about its bargaining position: we need the Circuit more than the Circuit needs any individual associate member, and the Circuit knows this, and the honest approach is to negotiate from that reality rather than pretending otherwise.

We further recommend that the Assembly formally acknowledge, in its renewal documentation, the institutional lock-in described above. This is not a confession of weakness. It is an accurate description of our position. Institutions that we chose forty-three years ago now choose us. The question before this body is not whether to remain — we will remain — but whether we will remain with our eyes open.

---

*[Archivist's note, Year 150 SP: The Assembly renewed on terms substantively identical to the prior period. The relay participation fee was reduced by 0.4 points, less than the Keeper's target. The Assembly's formal acknowledgment of institutional lock-in was adopted by a vote of 14-2-2. Harbor-Master Oren's son, who held his father's seat, cast one of the two dissenting votes, noting in the record that "acknowledging dependency does not require celebrating it." Keeper of Documents Lila Deneu-Oren — daughter of the two principal advocates in the original deliberation, married name joining both families — retired from the position the following year.]*
