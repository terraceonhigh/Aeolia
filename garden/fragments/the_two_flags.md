# The Two Flags

*A dispatch from the Merchant Guild, Year -50 BP, written for the general circulation press of a mid-tier commercial polity in the disputed intermediate belt.*

---

**To Our Readers in the Outer Circuits:**

You will have noticed, in recent seasons, that two sets of vessels now patrol the outer shipping lanes. They fly different colors. They do not fire on each other. They do not speak to each other, except through intermediaries. They are both, by the standards of the moment, the most powerful warships ever built — machines of a scale and destructive capacity that would have seemed, to our grandparents' grandparents, like mythology.

They are watching each other.

We have been asked, by several of our factors in the outer archipelagos, to explain what this means for trade.

The answer is that it means nothing and everything.

---

**Nothing**, in the sense that the ships do not stop merchant vessels. The Administered Trade circuits function. The char shipments move. The sago fiber moves. The kapas bales move. The relay houses in the intermediate belt are, if anything, busier than they were before the two great powers reached their current standoff. Both powers need the relay circuits. Both powers need the information the relay houses provide. The intermediate belt has, in this strange new equilibrium, become more valuable, not less.

You are safe to trade. The ships are not watching you.

**Everything**, in the sense that the geometry of the world has changed in ways that will take generations to understand.

---

Here is what we know from our factors' reports, assembled at some risk:

The two powers — we will call them the Northern Reach and the Southern Lattice, though these are approximations — have each developed weapons that cannot be defended against. This is not a figure of speech. The weapons exist. Their existence is the point of the weapons. Neither power will use them as long as the other exists to retaliate.

The stability this creates is real. We have now had, by our best calculation, two hundred and fifty years without a direct war between the major powers. The intermediate belt has not experienced a conquest from either side in living memory. Our sovereignty, such as it is, is the by-product of a standoff between forces that could end it in an afternoon.

The instability this creates is also real. In the absence of direct war, both powers compete in every other dimension. They compete for our trade routes. They compete for the smaller polities who have not yet been absorbed into either circuit. They compete for the allegiance of factors and relay houses and pilots who know the outer shipping lanes.

When two powers cannot fight each other directly, they fight through everyone else.

---

**We have seen this in the outer circuits three times in the last century.**

The first time was in the Anakala Archipelago, which had maintained its independence for eight hundred years by being too small and too poor to be worth absorbing. The Northern Reach took it in a single season. The Southern Lattice protested through diplomatic channels. Nothing happened. The Anakala Archipelago is now a Reach naval station.

The second time was in the Yemba group, which the Lattice had administered informally for three generations. The Reach established a rival trading house in the Yemba capital. The Yemba merchants, being merchants, sold to both. The Lattice expelled the Reach factors. The Reach blockaded the outer shipping lane. The Lattice backed down, or appeared to back down, in ways that are still being argued about by people who were there.

The third time was in a place we will not name, because the people who live there have not asked for attention.

Each of these events was, by the standards of pre-nuclear history, a minor commercial dispute. None of them killed more than a few hundred people directly. Each of them was also, if you understand what you are looking at, a proxy war between powers that cannot afford to fight each other with the weapons they actually have.

---

**What does this mean for your factors' operations?**

It means this: the intermediate belt is safer than it has ever been, and it is also more dangerous in ways that are harder to see.

The ships will not stop your vessels. They are not interested in your vessels. You are too small to be worth the diplomatic cost of interfering with.

You are also, precisely because you are too small to be worth direct attention, exactly the kind of territory that gets absorbed in proxy disputes. The Anakala polity was too small to matter. The Yemba group was too small to matter. Smallness is not protection. Smallness is a precondition for a certain kind of attention that feels, from the outside, like neglect.

The best protection, as it has always been, is indispensability. The relay houses that both powers use for their back-channel communications are not absorbed. The factors who know both sides of every circuit are not absorbed. The pilots who can navigate the disputed shipping lanes in fog are not absorbed.

Be useful to both. Be essential to neither. Keep the ships running.

---

*This dispatch was printed in six copies and distributed to senior factors in the outer circuits. It does not represent the official position of the Merchant Guild on any diplomatic matter.*

*The Merchant Guild has no official position on any diplomatic matter.*

*The Merchant Guild has positions on ship manifests, warehouse access, and credit instruments.*

*This is, in the current environment, the same thing.*

— *The Guild Secretary's Office, Year -50 BP*
