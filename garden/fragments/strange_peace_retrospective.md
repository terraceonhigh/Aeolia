# On the Centenary of the Strange Peace: Provisional Conclusions

*An excerpt from the introduction to* The First Century of Mutual Deterrence: Institutions, Commerce, and the Architecture of Non-War, *edited volume published by the University of the Northern Reach Press and the Polytechnic Press of the Southern Sovereignty. Year 140 SP.*

*This volume emerged from a joint academic conference held in neutral Kalapa in Year 138 SP — the first systematic academic collaboration between scholars from the two hegemon institutional networks on the subject of the Strange Peace itself. The editors acknowledge that the very existence of this volume represents a form of argument about the Strange Peace's significance.*

---

## The Question We Have Been Avoiding

Students of the Strange Peace have, for most of the first century, tended toward one of two positions. The first, associated with the Reach institutional tradition, treats the Strange Peace as primarily a military-strategic equilibrium: two polities with sufficient second-strike capability recognized that open conflict would be mutually destructive, and the absence of war that followed was the natural and more-or-less inevitable consequence of that recognition. On this view, the institutional elaboration that followed — the Sovereignty's Circuit membership expansion, the Reach's administered territory assessments, the emergence of intermediate-belt arrangements like the Kalapa model — is secondary: epiphenomenal, or at best adaptive, relative to the fundamental military-strategic constraint.

The second position, associated with the Sovereignty institutional tradition, treats the Strange Peace as primarily an institutional achievement: the fact that deterrence did not break down across a century of significant commercial competition, multiple regional crises, and substantial institutional friction between the two hegemon networks is not self-evident from the military-strategic equilibrium alone. On this view, the absence of war across the first century reflects not just the presence of deterrence constraints but the presence of institutional channels sufficient to resolve conflicts below the level of those constraints.

The editors of this volume have spent two years reading each other's work carefully, and we have reached the following conclusion: both positions are half right, and neither position explains what is most interesting about the first century.

---

## What the Military-Strategic Position Gets Right (and Wrong)

The deterrence equilibrium is real. The Admiralty's second-strike capacity and the Coordination Executive's equivalent have, to all available analysis, been maintained throughout the first century. The -12 targeting premium against direct hegemon conflict — as the Reach's strategic studies community has taken to calling it — has not been tested, and the absence of direct tests suggests that neither hegemon has concluded that the premium is worth paying.

The military-strategic position is correct that this constraint is foundational. The institutional elaboration of the first century took place within a frame defined by the deterrence equilibrium; it would have been different, and almost certainly less extensive, in the absence of that frame.

Where the military-strategic position goes wrong is in treating the deterrence equilibrium as a sufficient explanation for the Strange Peace's stability. Deterrence equilibria are not self-sustaining. They require maintenance — technical maintenance (second-strike capacity must be credible, not merely asserted), information maintenance (both parties must believe the other's capacity and commitment), and political maintenance (both parties' domestic political situations must continue to support the deterrence posture). All three forms of maintenance can fail. The historical record of deterrence equilibria prior to the Strange Peace is not encouraging on this point.

The first century has not seen a deterrence failure. It has also not seen a deterrence test. Whether the equilibrium would survive a test — whether the elaborate institutional architecture that has grown up around it would provide sufficient friction to prevent miscalculation from triggering conflict — is a question the first century does not answer, because the test has not arrived.

The Sovereignty institutional tradition's skepticism of the self-sufficiency claim is, in our judgment, well-founded.

---

## What the Institutional Position Gets Right (and Wrong)

The institutional position is correct that the first century's stability is not fully explained by military-strategic logic. Several events in the first century's history could plausibly have escalated to direct hegemon conflict under a purely military-strategic deterrence logic, and did not. The most significant of these — the Sargasso Basin relay disruption of Year 33 SP, the administered territory compliance crisis of Year 58-62 SP, the Oran Confederacy's contested-belt declaration of Year 47 SP — all had the structural properties of conflict triggers: they involved commercial interests sufficiently large to motivate action, they occurred in contested geographic zones where both hegemon networks had presence, and they featured the characteristic ambiguity of attribution that deterrence theory identifies as a precondition for miscalculation.

That these events resolved without direct conflict is attributable, in the Sovereignty institutional tradition's analysis, to the institutional channels that had developed by those years: the RSC's arbitration procedures for commercial disputes, the Reach's administered territory assessment process (accelerated by the Year 58 crisis), the emergence of ad hoc intermediary arrangements like the Kalapa Confederation's commercial neutrality protocol. These channels absorbed the conflicts before they reached the deterrence threshold.

Where the institutional position goes wrong is in treating these institutional channels as independent achievements, separate from the deterrence equilibrium that created the conditions for their development. The RSC's arbitration authority is credible in part because the parties to disputes within its jurisdiction understand that the alternative — outside the institutional channel — is a conflict that neither hegemon wants to escalate. The deterrence equilibrium does not just prevent war; it creates an incentive for both hegemon networks to develop and maintain non-war conflict resolution mechanisms, because those mechanisms are useful precisely when the alternative is a conflict that the deterrence constraint makes catastrophically expensive.

The military-strategic position's insistence that the deterrence equilibrium is foundational is, in our judgment, correct. The institutional position's failure to adequately account for the deterrence frame within which its institutional achievements occurred is a real limitation.

---

## The Synthesis (Provisional)

The Strange Peace across its first century is best understood as a deterrence equilibrium that created conditions for institutional development, and institutional development that in turn has made the deterrence equilibrium more robust. The relationship is mutually constitutive rather than hierarchical.

This synthesis is not new. Several scholars on both sides have gestured toward it. What is unusual about this volume is that scholars from the two hegemon institutional traditions are asserting it jointly, on the basis of a shared reading of the first century's evidence.

We are aware of the irony that a volume asserting the mutual constitution of deterrence and institutions is itself an institutional achievement that took place within a deterrence-framed stability. The Strange Peace has created the conditions for this conversation. Whether this conversation, and others like it, has in turn made the Strange Peace more likely to persist — whether the academic collaboration represented by this volume is itself a form of institutional maintenance — we leave to readers to judge.

---

## The Questions the Second Century Will Answer

The essays collected in this volume do not agree on everything. They share a common view that the Strange Peace is not self-explanatory and that explanation requires attention to both the military-strategic and institutional dimensions. Beyond that, the volume represents the range of current scholarly opinion rather than a consensus.

The questions we have been unable to answer — and that the second century's evidence will bear on — include the following:

**Does the Strange Peace become more or less stable as the hegemon networks mature?** The first century has seen both institutional deepening (more channels, more precedents, more embedded interests in stability) and institutional stress (the administered territory degradation pattern, the western basin structural fairness problem in the Sovereignty network). Whether deepening or stress will predominate in the second century is genuinely uncertain.

**What happens when the deterrence equilibrium is tested?** The first century has not produced a test. The structural conditions for a test — a crisis in which both hegemon networks have high-stakes interests, insufficient institutional channels to resolve the crisis below the deterrence threshold, and sufficient domestic political pressure to make action appear rational — remain present. A volume like this one, written at Year 240 SP, will know something about this question that we do not.

**Is the Strange Peace transferable?** The first century has produced the Kalapa model and similar intermediate-belt arrangements that create a third institutional category between full Circuit membership and Reach administration. Whether these arrangements constitute a generalizable template for non-hegemon participation in a deterrence-stable world, or whether they are specific achievements tied to the particular circumstances of the early Strange Peace, will be clearer from the second century's evidence.

We publish this volume at the centenary not because the questions are answered but because the questions are now, for the first time, answerable in principle — because the evidence of the first century is available, the analytical tools have been developed, and the institutional barriers to cross-hegemon scholarly collaboration have, at least provisionally, been lowered enough to permit the kind of conversation this volume represents.

The Strange Peace continues. So does the work of understanding it.
