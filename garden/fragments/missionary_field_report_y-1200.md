# Report of the Third Mission to the Kelani Basin, Year Approximately -1200 BP

*Recovered from the Reach's early expansion archive. Partial — the original document continues beyond the surviving fragment. Filed under "Peripheral Integration Records," series 4. The reporting officer's name has been lost; the archive attributes the report to the Missionary Office of the Southern Expansion, which operated for approximately three centuries before being absorbed into the Civil Registry.*

---

We arrived at the Kelani Basin in the month of high water, which was well chosen. The harbor at the outer island was suitable for three vessels. The primary settlement sits on the inner island, where the paddi terraces climb the southern slopes for approximately two hundred vertical spans. The population — I estimate six to eight thousand, based on the cultivated area and the number of dwellings visible from the harbor — is compact and well-organized. They have a council of elders who govern from a meeting house at the top of the terraces. They have a fishing cooperative that operates four large vessels. They have a tradition of navigation that extends at least three island-hops from their home base, based on the trade goods I observed in their market.

They do not have our faith.

---

I should clarify what I mean by this. They have practices that function as faith — seasonal rites, ancestor observances, a coming-of-age ceremony that involves a three-day period of fasting and isolation. They have specialists who perform these functions. They have explanatory narratives about the ocean, the fish, the seasons, the stars.

What they do not have is an institutional framework that connects personal observance to civic obligation. Their rites are personal. Their governance is secular. The council of elders deliberates on practical matters — water rights, fishing schedules, trade agreements — and does not invoke spiritual authority in its decisions. The meeting house and the ceremony house are separate structures, on different parts of the island. I asked whether the elders ever consulted the ceremony specialists on governance questions. The translator said the question was confusing and asked me to rephrase it. I was unable to rephrase it in a way that made sense to him.

This is what the Missionary Office's training manual calls a "low-integration faith environment." The manual recommends a specific approach: demonstrate the institutional advantage of integrated governance. Do not argue theology. Demonstrate that a community whose faith and governance reinforce each other is more resilient, more cohesive, and more capable of responding to crisis than one whose faith and governance operate independently.

---

The demonstration requires time and proximity. We have established ourselves on the outer island, where we conduct our own observances according to our practice. We have invited members of the Kelani community to observe. Several have attended — curious, not hostile. The fishing cooperative has been particularly receptive, because our faith includes practical obligations related to resource stewardship that they recognize as useful. The obligation to maintain fish stocks between seasons — which we practice as a spiritual discipline, and they practice as an economic necessity — is a point of convergence.

We have not argued that their approach is wrong. We have not suggested that our approach is superior. We have demonstrated that our approach works, and we have allowed proximity to do the rest.

The manual calls this "institutional absorption through demonstration." I have come to think of it as something simpler: we show them what a community looks like when its faith and its governance are one thing rather than two, and we wait for them to notice the advantages.

---

The advantages are real. A community whose members share spiritual obligations alongside civic ones has a coordination mechanism that purely secular governance does not. When the paddi harvest is poor, a secular council can organize rationing. A council that shares faith with its community can organize rationing and frame it as a shared sacrifice with spiritual meaning. The compliance rate is higher. The resentment is lower. The crisis passes more smoothly.

I write this not as a theological argument but as an administrative observation. The Missionary Office does not care, in the final accounting, whether the Kelani Basin adopts our specific doctrines. It cares whether the Kelani Basin adopts an institutional framework that makes integration with the larger Reach commercial and administrative network more efficient. Our faith is a mechanism for producing that framework. If the Kelani Basin produced an equivalent framework through other means, the Office would be satisfied. But they have not. And we have. And so we are here.

---

I am aware that this description of my work is not the one the Office uses in its reports to the metropolitan administration. The Office's reports describe the mission in terms of spiritual care, cultural exchange, and the extension of civilization to communities that have not yet received it. These descriptions are not false. They are incomplete. The spiritual care is real. The cultural exchange is real. The extension of civilization — insofar as "civilization" means an institutional framework that connects a peripheral community to the metropolitan center through shared norms, shared obligations, and shared identity — is also real, and it is the one the metropolitan administration cares about.

The metropolitan administration cares about integration speed. Integration speed is the rate at which a newly absorbed territory transitions from a net cost (requiring administrative investment) to a net contributor (producing extractable surplus). The Missionary Office increases integration speed. This is the reason it exists. The faith is the mechanism. The integration is the purpose.

I do not find this troubling. The faith is not diminished by being useful. The people of the Kelani Basin are not harmed by acquiring an institutional framework that connects them to a larger world. The extraction that follows integration is moderate, and the services the metropolitan administration provides — route security, commercial standardization, dispute arbitration — are genuine.

Whether the balance between what is extracted and what is provided is fair is a question I am not in a position to answer. I am in a position to observe that the Kelani Basin's council of elders, after eight months of proximity to our mission, has begun holding its deliberations in the meeting house that is also the ceremony house. The two buildings are becoming one building. The integration is proceeding.

---

*[Archivist's note, Year 80 SP: The Missionary Office was dissolved approximately 900 years after this report was filed. Its functions were absorbed into the Civil Registry's Basin Liaison Office, which continues to administer territories that the missions originally integrated. The Kelani Basin was reclassified as a Class II Administered Territory. Its current sovereignty index is 0.41. Its current grievance index is 0.22. The two buildings are still one building.]*
