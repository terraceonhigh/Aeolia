# Administered Territory Programme: Third Decadal Assessment

*Civil Registry of the Reach, Programme Evaluation Division*  
*Year 80 SP — Classified: Ministerial Distribution Only*

---

## Summary for the Cabinet

This assessment covers the performance of the Reach's administered territory network across the eighty-year period since the Strange Peace was established. The Programme Evaluation Division presents this document to the Cabinet in advance of the Third Connectivity Review, at which the structural terms of administered relationships will be subject to renegotiation.

**Headline finding:** The administered territory network continues to function within expected performance parameters on commercial revenue generation. It is underperforming on administrative integration, and that underperformance is accelerating.

The Division recommends that the Cabinet treat this assessment as urgent.

---

## Commercial Performance: Acceptable

Administered territory commercial revenue has grown at 1.8% annually in real terms across the eighty-year period. This is below the projection of 2.4% annual growth that the Second Connectivity Treaty used as its planning baseline, and below the growth rate of the relay associate network during the same period (2.7% annually). But it is positive growth, and the absolute revenue contribution of the administered network remains significant.

The commercial performance gap relative to relay associate performance is largely attributable to terms-of-trade structural factors that this Division cannot address within its mandate. The administered territory network is concentrated in the equatorial and southern intermediate belts, where primary commodity exports (caloric staples, bulk industrial inputs) are subject to the long-run terms-of-trade disadvantage documented extensively in the Commerce Division's trade reports. The Relay Associate Programme's commercial outperformance reflects its composition, not its institutional design. This should be considered when the Cabinet evaluates proposals to convert administered territories to associate status as a performance improvement measure.

---

## Administrative Integration: Degrading

The administered territory network's administrative integration score — measured by compliance rate, dispute resolution efficiency, and Civil Registry staffing penetration — has declined steadily across all three metrics over the eighty-year period.

**Compliance rate** (the fraction of administered levy obligations paid on schedule without enforcement action) has declined from 91% in Year 10 SP to 74% in Year 79 SP. The decline is concentrated in administered territories absorbed in the pre-Strange Peace period: territories absorbed between Year -60 BP and Year 0 show a 23-point decline in compliance rate; territories absorbed since the Strange Peace show a 7-point decline. The long-held territories are the ones drifting.

**Dispute resolution efficiency** (average time from dispute filing to Civil Registry judgment) has increased from 14 months in Year 10 SP to 22 months in Year 79 SP, driven primarily by a 35% increase in dispute filing volume. More disputes are being filed, and each dispute takes longer to resolve. The Civil Registry's administrative apparatus has not expanded proportionally to the growing administrative burden. This is a resourcing problem; it is also a signal problem, because the volume of disputes being filed is an indicator of the underlying level of grievance in the administered network.

**Civil Registry staffing penetration** (the fraction of administered territory administrative positions held by Civil Registry-trained personnel versus locally-trained personnel) has declined from 68% in Year 10 SP to 51% in Year 79 SP. This is partly a deliberate policy outcome — successive Cabinets have reduced the cost of administered territory administration by accepting locally-trained personnel in more roles — and partly an unintended outcome of attrition and hiring difficulties. The practical effect is that administered territory administration is increasingly delivered by personnel who were not trained in Civil Registry standards and who have institutional loyalties that are at least partially local.

---

## The Grievance Pattern

The Programme Evaluation Division has for the past three years been tracking a structured indicator of administered territory grievance: the frequency of administrative appeals filed against Civil Registry determinations. This metric captures deliberate, formal resistance rather than passive non-compliance; it is an upper bound on organized grievance expression.

The appeals frequency has increased 2.1-fold over the eighty-year period, with the increase concentrated in three administered territory clusters: the southern equatorial chain, the western intermediate zone, and the outer islands of the eastern administered territories. These three clusters share a common profile: absorbed in the pre-Strange Peace period, primarily bulk-commodity producers, and significantly less commercially integrated with the Reach's core relay network than their geographic position would suggest.

The Division notes that these are precisely the administered territories that the Sovereignty's Circuit membership expansion programme has targeted with associate network offers over the past thirty years. Several territories in the western intermediate zone have received Circuit membership offers in the past decade. None have formally accepted; the offers remain open.

The Division does not have visibility into the internal deliberations of administered territory polities regarding these offers. However, the correlation between territories that have received Circuit membership offers and territories showing elevated appeals frequency suggests that the mere availability of an alternative is affecting administered territory political culture even absent formal acceptance.

---

## Structural Recommendations

The Division presents three options to the Cabinet for the Third Connectivity Review.

**Option A: Deepened Administration.** Increase Civil Registry staffing in underperforming territories, strengthen compliance enforcement mechanisms, and renegotiate terms toward higher administered levy rates in compensation for increased service provision. This option addresses the resourcing problem directly. It risks deepening grievance in territories where the primary driver of non-compliance is perceived unfairness of terms rather than service inadequacy.

**Option B: Graduated Transition.** Develop a formal transition pathway from administered territory status toward relay associate status, with criteria tied to governance maturity, commercial integration, and compliance history. Territories that meet the criteria would transition to associate status over a ten-to-fifteen year period. This option reduces the administrative burden on the Civil Registry, aligns administered territory expectations with a positive trajectory, and reduces the competitive vulnerability to Sovereignty circuit offers. It involves accepting a permanent reduction in administered levy revenue in exchange for relay fee revenue from formerly administered territories.

**Option C: Status Quo with Monitoring.** Continue current policy, increase monitoring frequency, and defer structural decisions to the Fourth Connectivity Review. This option preserves optionality but accepts the continued degradation of the administrative integration scores. The Division's trend analysis suggests that at current trajectory, administered territory compliance rates will fall below 60% by Year 100 SP in the three identified underperforming clusters.

The Division does not make a formal recommendation between these options. The choice involves commercial, political, and strategic considerations outside the Division's scope. The Division observes that Option C's trajectory leads, in the medium term, to a situation in which the administered network's commercial revenue is declining relative to its administrative cost — at which point the question becomes whether the administered network serves any purpose other than preventing Sovereignty expansion into the intermediate belt.

If that is the purpose, the Cabinet should say so explicitly, because the current administrative justification — that administered territories are commercially productive and administratively integrated — will not remain true much longer.
