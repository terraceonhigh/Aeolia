# The Reversal

*A parable in the mode of institutional economics.*

---

There is a hypothesis, associated with a sequence of papers in the early 2000s,
that the most prosperous polities before the colonial era became the least
prosperous after it.

The argument is structural. High pre-colonial population density signals
agricultural productivity, which signals surplus, which signals something
worth extracting. The colonizing power therefore installs extractive
institutions in the productive places: mining administrations, plantation
systems, forced-labor mechanisms. In the less productive places — thin soils,
marginal environments, too scattered to administer efficiently — the colonizing
power either ignores them or leaves small settler communities that, for reasons
of their own survival, build inclusive institutions instead.

Four centuries later, you find the descendants of the first group poor and
the descendants of the second group rich. This is not because of anything
inherent to the populations. It is because the institutions that were
installed in each case propagated forward.

The name for this is the *reversal of fortune*.

---

What the Aeolian data adds — and this is the contribution that does not appear
in the terrestrial literature — is the mechanism by which institutional lock-in
operates under conditions of non-contiguous maritime empire.

On a terrestrial empire, extractive institutions can be enforced continuously.
A colonial administration sits in the extracted territory; garrison forces
maintain the extraction; the metropole receives the surplus directly. The
process is visible at every stage.

In the Aeolian case, the extracting polity is necessarily at a distance. The
empire is maintained through periodic naval assertion, factional client
relationships, and — crucially — the administered trade system that processes
the surplus into the metropole's energy accounts. What the metropole extracts
is not raw material but *processed value*: bulk calories, dried fish, raw
kapas, unroasted char. The processing step happens at the periphery.

This processing relationship is where the institutional lock-in takes hold.

---

Consider the Vaanthi archipelago at tick 180 (year −10,000 BP equivalent, using
the calibrated timeline). The Vaanthi are, by any measure, the most productive
polity in their contact circuit. Their paddi terraces are ancient and well-
engineered. Their population is three times the median. Their knowledge
infrastructure — the accumulated agricultural technique passed across generations
— is the highest in their region.

Then the Reach expands to them.

The Reach does not destroy the Vaanthi paddi terraces. It has no reason to.
The paddi is exactly what it wants. What it installs is an administered trade
circuit that routes the paddi surplus through Reach-controlled intermediaries,
pricing it at the metropole's terms rather than the Vaanthi's terms. The
Prebisch-Singer terms are unfavorable by design: the Vaanthi export bulk
calories; they import prestige goods and, later, manufactured tools. The
terms of trade move against them as soon as the Reach industrializes.

But the deeper damage is institutional. The Reach administration needs
reliable surplus extraction. Reliable surplus extraction requires stable
labor organization. Stable labor organization, in a paddi economy, means
preventing the cultural drift that would allow Vaanthi cultivators to redirect
their effort toward more remunerative activities. The administration therefore
systematically suppresses the Boserupian release — the shift from extensive
to intensive cultivation that would allow the Vaanthi to improve their terms
of trade through quality differentiation.

The cultivators know this. There is nothing they can do about it in the short
run. The Reach controls the ships.

---

Four hundred ticks later — twenty thousand years of simulated time — the
Vaanthi have been decolonized. They are formally autonomous. But they are
still growing paddi.

This is not inexplicable. The institutional infrastructure of the paddi
economy — the terrace maintenance networks, the water-rights adjudication
systems, the credit relationships between cultivators and local factors — was
built, over centuries of colonial administration, *for paddi*. Shifting to
a different crop or industry requires dismantling and rebuilding institutional
infrastructure that the Reach spent three hundred ticks constructing.

The Vaanthi are not trapped by culture. They are trapped by sunk institutional
costs. The institutions that served the Reach's extractive purposes are now
serving no one's purposes, but they are still there, still enforcing the
labor-organization patterns, still pricing Vaanthi output at the old terms.

Meanwhile, the isolated archipelago to the northeast — the one the Reach never
bothered to extract because its population was thin and its soil was marginal —
developed, in isolation, the decentralized maritime trading institutions that
the Reach's administered trade system prevented the Vaanthi from developing.
By the time of decolonization, that northern archipelago has four times the
Vaanthi's tech level. Its institutions were shaped by competitive necessity
rather than imposed extraction.

The reversal is complete.

---

The Acemoglu-Robinson literature asks: why did institutions persist?

The Aeolian answer is more specific: institutions persist because they are
*relationship structures*, not just rules. The paddi factor who extends credit
to cultivators, the water-rights arbitrator who mediates seasonal disputes,
the drying-house operator who certifies quality for the administered trade
circuit — these people's livelihoods depend on the continuation of exactly
the institutional arrangements that were installed to serve the extracting
power. They are not venal. They are not collaborating with the Reach out of
ideology. They are simply people whose skills and relationships were built in
a particular institutional context, and who would be made worthless by
its dismantling.

This is the deepest form of institutional lock-in. It is not maintained by
force (the Reach's fleet has gone home). It is maintained by the accumulated
human capital of the people whose lives were organized around it.

The reversal of fortune is not a one-time event. It is a process that
continues long after the extracting power leaves.

---

*This is what the simulation encodes as* extractiveness[core]*:*
*not the persistence of the extracting empire, but the persistence*
*of the institutional relationships that the extraction created.*

*The Vaanthi are free. Their institutions are not.*
